# Article II Notes

## What This Article Is Trying To Fix

The current U.S. Congress is constitutionally powerful on paper but too easy to disable in practice through delay, refusal to act, and procedural sabotage.

This article tries to preserve bicameralism while making legislative responsibility more real.

## Why The Legislature Is Still Central

This draft does not try to solve presidentialism by making Congress secondary.

It instead leans the other way: the legislature is meant to be the primary democratic lawmaking body, the main check on executive abuse, and the place where national disagreement is processed in public form.

That is why the article pays attention to:

- composition and representation
- vacancies and continuity
- mandatory votes in some constitutional contexts
- anti-obstruction rules
- appointment and investigative functions

## Why Some Procedural Safeguards Are Constitutionalized

In a healthy system, many legislative procedures can stay in chamber rules.

In a system designed against bad-faith institutional blockage, some procedures need constitutional footing or they can be neutralized by silence, scheduling games, or refusal to recognize duties.

The article therefore constitutionalizes some fallback mechanisms not because procedure is inherently constitutional, but because some procedures become democracy-critical when ordinary practice breaks down.

## Why Compression Still Matters Here

This is one of the densest core articles in the draft.

That does not mean the architecture is wrong. It means the article has had to solve many practical failure points that the current U.S. Constitution leaves to custom. The later drafting passes therefore focused on compression and simplification rather than redesign.
