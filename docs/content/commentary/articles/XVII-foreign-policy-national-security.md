# Article XVII Notes

## Why Foreign Policy Gets A Separate Article

Foreign policy is not identical to war, but it is also not an executive free zone.

This article is meant to define the constitutional space for diplomacy, sanctions, recognition, strategic agreements, and national-security action without letting that space swallow the rest of the constitutional order.

## What This Article Is Trying To Balance

Foreign policy requires speed, secrecy in some cases, and coordinated action.

But it also creates recurring temptations:

- unilateral executive lawmaking
- opaque coercive economic measures
- domestic evasion through foreign-affairs framing
- emergency-style logic migrating into ordinary governance

The article therefore tries to preserve real executive capacity without allowing foreign policy to become a constitutional escape hatch.

## Why This Article Stays Narrower Than Article XVI

War powers require especially strict constitutional discipline because armed force is so extreme.

Foreign policy still needs constraints, but it cannot be constitutionalized at the same level of operational detail without freezing too much statecraft. The drafting choice here is to define authority, limits, and reviewability while leaving much of the operational domain to law.

## Why Reviewability Matters

Foreign-affairs claims have often been used to push questions out of effective judicial scrutiny.

This draft pushes the other way: foreign policy does not become constitutionally unreviewable simply because the executive invokes national interest or external affairs.
