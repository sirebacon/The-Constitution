# Clause Note: The Healthcare Floor

## Why Put Healthcare In The Constitution At All?

Because formal liberty means less when access to basic and emergency care can be denied simply because a person cannot pay.

This clause treats healthcare access as part of the minimum material conditions of equal citizenship and human dignity.

## Why The Draft Uses A Floor Instead Of One Fixed Model

The Constitution should define what government must secure, not lock the country into one permanent administrative scheme.

That is why the draft protects:

- access to basic healthcare
- emergency treatment
- protection against inability to pay becoming a barrier to medically necessary care

without constitutionalizing a full healthcare code.

## Why This Does Not Imply Privatization

The clause is flexible about delivery, not indifferent about public duty.

It now makes explicit that the government's constitutional obligation can be carried out through public provision, public insurance, regulated private participation, or a mixed system, but that private participation never displaces the state's responsibility to secure the right.

## How This Constrains Exploitation

The clause does not try to constitutionalize a full healthcare regulatory code.

Instead, it sets a constitutional floor that makes exploitative arrangements unlawful when they undermine real access to medically necessary care. If a financing or delivery system leaves people without needed care because of wealth, denial practices, or structurally exclusionary design, the government is failing its constitutional duty even if private actors are formally involved.

That means exploitation is reached through the public obligation:

- government must secure universal access
- inability to pay cannot block medically necessary care
- Congress must regulate public and private healthcare financing and delivery against exclusion, arbitrary denial, and materially abusive practices
- outsourcing does not excuse failure

The detailed anti-exploitation machinery still belongs in law, but the constitutional baseline makes clear that a system built on exclusion or practical denial is not acceptable.

## Why This Design Is Stronger

It lets the constitutional system require real coverage while still leaving room for democratic argument over how the program is structured.
