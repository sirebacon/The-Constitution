# Article XVIII — Social, Economic, and Affirmative Rights

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

---

## Scope

Establishes social, economic, environmental, and affirmative rights of all persons within the United States. These rights impose positive duties on government to maintain constitutional floors of protection against destitution, exclusion, and preventable harm inconsistent with a minimally dignified life. They complement the civil and political rights in Article V. The general principles of Article V Section 1, including the rights floor, the prohibition on permanent suspension, absolute rights, and the rule that enumeration does not deny unenumerated rights, apply equally to the rights in this Article. Judicial enforcement under this Article is limited to deciding whether government has met the constitutional floor, whether government has impermissibly backslid from that floor, and what timely lawful remedy is required. Courts may not govern program design in the place of the political branches.

---

## Section 1 — Environmental Rights

**1.1** Every person has the right to a healthy environment, including clean air, safe drinking water, and freedom from toxic contamination.

**1.2** Every person has the right to a stable climate. The government has a duty to adopt and maintain reasonable measures, consistent with the best available scientific evidence, to prevent dangerous interference with the climate system and to protect present and future generations from the consequences of climate change. Government may not knowingly and materially dismantle existing climate protections without replacing them with measures that are constitutionally sufficient in aggregate. A court finding a violation of this duty may declare the breach, require the political branches to adopt a constitutionally sufficient remedial plan within a defined period, and retain jurisdiction to determine whether the submitted plan meets the constitutional floor. A court may not prescribe emissions targets, technology choices, tax measures, or line-item appropriations.

**1.3** In all decisions affecting the environment, the government shall give weight to the interests of future generations who cannot participate in current political processes.

**1.4** The rights in this Section create enforceable duties on government. A court may determine whether government action or inaction is plainly inadequate to the constitutional floor established by this Section, may declare a violation, may require the submission of a remedial plan within a defined period, and may enforce compliance against executive officials charged with carrying out that plan. Courts shall not prescribe specific policy instruments, manage ongoing environmental programs, or set budget levels except where necessary to prevent the executive from disregarding an existing lawful appropriation.

## Section 2 — Affirmative Rights

**2.1 Healthcare.** Every person has the right to access basic healthcare and emergency medical treatment. No person shall be denied emergency medical treatment due to inability to pay. Congress shall establish and maintain a system under which inability to pay does not bar access to medically necessary care.

**2.2 Education.** Every person has the right to free public education through the completion of secondary school. Public education shall be of sufficient quality to enable meaningful participation in civic and economic life. No state may provide substantially inferior education to any group of students on the basis of race, income, disability, immigration status, or geographic location.

**2.3 Housing security.** Every person has the right to protection against arbitrary homelessness. Government shall maintain measures sufficient to prevent destitution and to preserve access to basic shelter for persons who would otherwise be left without it through circumstances beyond their control. This right does not guarantee any specific dwelling, but it does require a constitutionally adequate shelter and homelessness-prevention floor.

**2.4 Enforcement of affirmative rights.** The rights in this Section impose duties on government that are judicially enforceable. A court finding a violation may declare that the constitutional floor has not been met, require Congress or the relevant state legislature to adopt a lawful remedy within a defined period, order the executive to use existing lawful authority in the interim, and retain jurisdiction to determine whether the adopted remedy is constitutionally sufficient. Courts may not design a program, choose among materially sufficient policy alternatives, order tax increases, or compel new appropriations as a primary remedy. Government may not deliberately dismantle an existing system of protection below the constitutional floor unless it simultaneously replaces it with protections that are materially equal or greater in practical effect. If the political branches fail to act in good faith by the court's deadline, the court may preserve existing protections against backsliding and enforce compliance orders against responsible executive officials.

## Section 3 — Economic Rights

**3.1 Right to work.** Every person has the right to work and to pursue a lawful occupation free from arbitrary government restriction. Licensing requirements for occupations must be rationally related to the protection of public health, safety, or welfare.

**3.2 Right to organize.** Every worker has the right to form, join, and be represented by a labor union or other collective bargaining organization, and to engage in collective bargaining. No law shall prohibit collective bargaining or penalize workers for organizing.

**3.3 Economic security.** Congress shall maintain programs ensuring a basic standard of living for persons unable to support themselves due to age, disability, illness, caregiving burden, or economic hardship. Such protections may not be eliminated or materially reduced without replacement by protections that are constitutionally sufficient and materially equal or greater in practical effect.

**3.4 Fair compensation.** Every worker has the right to a living wage: compensation sufficient to meet basic needs including food, housing, healthcare, and transportation in the region where they work. Congress shall establish a minimum wage floor meeting this standard, set the specific amount by law, and review and adjust it at regular intervals sufficient to prevent erosion below the constitutional floor. States may set higher minimums. Congress may not freeze, delay, or reduce the wage floor below what this Section requires.

## Section 4 — Rights of Children

**4.1 — Children as rights holders.** Children are rights holders under this Constitution. The rights enumerated in this Article and in Article V apply to children as well as adults. Where rights require modification in their application to children by reason of age and developing capacity, such modification shall be made in the child's best interests, not for the convenience of the state or other adults.

**4.2 — Right to education.** Every child has the right to free public education of sufficient quality to develop their intellectual and civic capacities. No child may be denied education on the basis of their race, national origin, disability, immigration status, gender, or the circumstances of their parents.

**4.3 — Protection from abuse, neglect, and exploitation.** Every child has the right to protection from physical abuse, sexual abuse, emotional abuse, neglect, and economic exploitation. The state has an affirmative duty to protect children from these harms, including harms inflicted by parents, guardians, and institutions.

**4.4 — Protection from child labor.** No child under sixteen years of age may be employed in work that interferes with their education, endangers their health, or is hazardous in nature. Congress shall define and enforce standards protecting children from exploitative labor. Light, non-hazardous work by older children that does not interfere with education is not prohibited.

**4.5 — Right to be heard.** In any legal proceeding that directly affects a child's welfare — including custody, adoption, guardianship, and child-protective proceedings — the child's views shall be given weight appropriate to the child's age and maturity. A child has the right to independent representation in any proceeding in which custody, placement, or comparable fundamental interests are at issue. Where a court departs materially from a mature child's expressed preference, it shall state specific reasons on the record grounded in the child's welfare.

**4.6 — Best interests standard.** In all actions by government affecting children, the best interests of the child shall be a primary consideration. This standard applies to legislative, executive, and judicial action and may not be displaced by administrative convenience, blanket stigma, or categorical assumptions about family status.

**4.7 — Children of incarcerated parents.** The incarceration of a parent does not, by itself, constitute grounds for the permanent termination of parental rights. The state shall make reasonable efforts to maintain family bonds where doing so is in the child's best interests.
