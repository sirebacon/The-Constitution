# Article VII — Campaign Finance and Political Money

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

---

## Scope

Establishes the constitutional framework governing money in federal and state elections. The core principles are that voters have a right to know who is funding political power, that political office must remain realistically accessible without wealth or large private donors, and that legislatures may regulate political money to prevent corruption, domination, concealment, and foreign influence.

---

## Section 1 — Constitutional Authority and Disclosure

### 1.1 — Authority to regulate political money

Congress shall have power to regulate, limit, and require disclosure of contributions and expenditures made for the purpose of influencing federal elections. Money spent to influence elections is subject to regulation regardless of the legal form through which it is spent.

No judicial doctrine shall be understood to forbid reasonable regulation of political spending by corporations, organizations, or other non-individual entities on the theory that such spending is categorically immune from regulation as protected speech. Regulations on individual political spending remain subject to strict scrutiny. Regulations on political spending by non-individual entities need only serve a substantial government interest.

### 1.2 — Traceable disclosure required

All contributions and expenditures made for the purpose of influencing a federal election shall be publicly disclosed. Disclosure must identify the original human source of the funds. No entity, trust, account, intermediary, nominee, or pass-through arrangement may be used to obscure that source. If the original human source cannot be identified, the contribution or expenditure is prohibited except for nominal amounts below a threshold fixed by law. Congress and the Electoral Commission shall provide by law for enforcement of this requirement through chains of intermediaries.

### 1.3 — Timely public notice

Significant contributions and expenditures must be disclosed promptly enough for voters to know their source before casting ballots. Congress may set thresholds and deadlines by law, but may not permit delayed disclosure that defeats the purpose of meaningful pre-election transparency.

### 1.4 — Nonprofit, pass-through, and affiliated-source transparency

An organization may not use tax status, pass-through status, or layered entity form to conceal the original human source of political funds. Congress and the Electoral Commission may require that affiliated or coordinated sources be treated as a single source and that disclosure requirements not be defeated by fragmentation.

### 1.5 — Coordination and acting in concert

An expenditure is not independent if it is made in coordination with a candidate, campaign, party, or their agents. Congress and the Electoral Commission may define coordination by law, establish rebuttable presumptions to prevent disguised coordination, and treat coordinated or concerted spending as a single source for purposes of limits, caps, and disclosure.

### 1.6 — Foreign money prohibited

No foreign national, foreign government, foreign corporation, or entity owned or controlled by foreign interests may contribute to or make expenditures in connection with any federal, state, or local election in the United States, whether directly or through a domestic intermediary. Congress and the Electoral Commission may take all measures necessary to enforce this prohibition, including treating willful blindness as a violation.

### 1.7 — Entity spending and source restrictions

Congress may regulate or prohibit political spending by corporations, labor organizations, trade associations, contractors, pass-through entities, tax-exempt entities, and other legal forms where necessary to prevent corruption, concealment, coercion, or circumvention.

Congress may also regulate in-kind political contributions, digital-asset political spending, and any other transfer of value used to influence an election.

## Section 2 — Public Financing

### 2.1 — Competitive public financing required

Congress shall maintain a system of public financing for federal elections sufficient to permit a well-organized candidate to run a competitive campaign for federal office without reliance on large private donors. Participation by candidates may be voluntary, but any candidate who accepts public financing may be required to accept reasonable spending conditions as a condition of participation.

The system shall include matching or equivalent support for small-dollar contributions and shall be funded through a mechanism insulated from ordinary annual political retaliation.

### 2.2 — Parameter setting and incumbency neutrality

Congress may establish the constitutional floors and ceilings of the public-financing system and broader campaign-finance framework, but the specific dollar amounts, contribution limits, qualifying thresholds, matching ratios, and comparable numerical parameters shall be set or adjusted by the Electoral Commission within those bounds.

The system shall be designed and administered so that a challenger with demonstrated grassroots support has a realistic path to qualifying for and competing on substantially fair terms with an incumbent. Courts may enforce this principle and the Electoral Commission shall periodically review whether the system satisfies it.

### 2.3 — Adjustment and adequacy

Campaign-finance thresholds and public-financing parameters shall be periodically adjusted for inflation or other objective changes in economic conditions. Law shall provide for review of whether the public-financing system remains genuinely competitive and for response to any finding of inadequacy.

### 2.4 — Self-financing and legislative self-dealing

Congress may regulate the effects of extreme candidate self-financing, including by providing responsive public support to opponents who rely on the public-financing system.

No chamber of Congress may unilaterally set the specific campaign-finance parameters governing elections to that same chamber. Congress shall provide by law for conflict-resistant parameter setting consistent with this Article.

## Section 3 — Spending Limits and Outside Spending

### 3.1 — Outside spending may be limited

Congress may limit total outside spending in connection with a federal race, across federal races, or both. It may distinguish between candidate spending, party spending, and outside spending, and may exempt bona fide news reporting, editorial activity, and other journalistic work. Law may define the election periods and forms of advocacy subject to such limits, provided those definitions are directed to real electoral influence and not evaded by formal wording tests alone.

### 3.2 — Single-candidate and concentrated outside spending

Outside spending that is substantially directed toward the election or defeat of a single identifiable candidate may be treated as presumptively coordinated and therefore subject to contribution limits and anti-coordination rules.

Congress may also limit the share of total outside spending in a race or geography that may be supplied by a single actor or by actors acting in concert, in order to prevent domination of a race by concentrated wealth or by formally separate but practically unified spenders.

### 3.3 — Pre-election restraint and anti-evasion

Congress may impose heightened disclosure, spending restrictions, or short blackout periods immediately before an election where necessary to preserve informed voting and prevent last-minute concealment or circumvention.

Spending outside formal election windows may still be regulated under this Article when it is part of a unified electoral effort or is used to evade otherwise lawful limits.

---

## Section 4 — Source Restrictions

### 4.1 — Contractors and comparable dependency relationships

Congress may prohibit or limit political contributions and expenditures by federal contractors and by entities in comparable financial relationships with the federal government where necessary to prevent pay-to-play corruption or its appearance.

### 4.2 — Anonymous digital-asset spending prohibited

Congress may prohibit political contributions and expenditures made through any means by which the original human source of funds cannot be reliably identified and disclosed, regardless of the technological form.

### 4.3 — In-kind value covered

Non-monetary transfers of value made for the purpose of influencing an election are contributions for purposes of this Article and are subject to the same disclosure, source, and anti-circumvention rules as monetary transfers.

---

## Section 5 — Transition, Inaugural, and Post-Election Money

### 5.1 — Presidential transition funding

The presidential transition is a governmental function and shall be funded only as provided by law from public funds. No private contribution, donation, gift, or thing of value may be accepted for transition operations. Campaign funds may not be converted to transition use.

### 5.2 — Inaugural and post-election fundraising

Congress may permit private fundraising for inaugural ceremonies or comparable post-election purposes only subject to the disclosure, source, segregation, and anti-corruption rules of this Article.

Campaign debt retirement, leadership political committees, and comparable post-election political fundraising remain subject to the contribution limits, disclosure rules, and source restrictions of this Article.

### 5.3 — Political advertising transparency

Congress and the Electoral Commission shall maintain a public record of political advertising sufficient to disclose its source, content, and material reach before and after each election.

---

## Section 6 — Enforcement

### 6.1 — Administration and prosecution

The Electoral Commission shall administer and civilly enforce this Article. The Accountability Commission shall prosecute willful criminal violations. Congress may establish tiered civil and criminal enforcement consistent with Article XII.

Willful concealment of political funding sources, willful use of shell entities or comparable devices to evade disclosure, willful laundering of prohibited political funds, and willful acceptance or use of foreign political money are serious offenses and may be punished accordingly.

### 6.2 — Timely remedies

Congress shall provide by law for expedited remedies sufficient to prevent unlawful political money from shaping an election before voters can know its source.

---

## Section 7 — State Elections Floor

The minimum campaign-finance standards applicable to state and local elections are established in Article X Section 1.7. This Article's full framework applies to federal elections. States may adopt equivalent or stricter standards for their own elections and may access federal campaign-finance infrastructure where this Constitution or federal law so provides.
