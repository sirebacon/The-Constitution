# How Testing Works

This project does not rely only on constitutional theory or article-by-article drafting judgment. It also uses a scenario-based simulator to stress-test the draft against recurring patterns of abuse, delay, sabotage, and institutional failure.

## What The Simulator Does

The simulator runs structured constitutional scenarios.

Each scenario describes:

- a political or legal stress event
- the institutions involved
- the constitutional provisions expected to apply
- the outcomes that should follow if the draft is working as intended

Examples include:

- election sabotage
- judicial delay
- executive defiance
- rights suppression
- federalism overreach
- emergency abuse
- corruption and institutional capture
- continuity failures during transition

## What A Scenario Tests

A scenario is not a prediction. It is a stress test.

The point is to ask:

- does the Constitution assign authority clearly?
- does it create an enforceable duty?
- does it provide a fallback if an institution refuses to act?
- does it prevent the system from drifting into paralysis or personal rule?

## What The Main Results Mean

The simulator tracks several kinds of outputs.

### Violations

These are constitutional breaches or attempted breaches within the scenario.

They do not mean the draft failed. Many scenarios are deliberately built around bad conduct so the question is whether the system can identify and contain the violation.

### Bottlenecks

These are stress points where the system still depends on an institution acting on time or in good faith.

Some bottlenecks are acceptable political-risk residuals. Others can reveal genuine design weaknesses.

### Unresolved Obligations

These are the most serious failures.

An unresolved obligation means the Constitution created a duty or expected consequence that the scenario could not complete through the available constitutional machinery.

The goal is to keep this number at zero.

## What Testing Can And Cannot Prove

Testing improves confidence, but it does not prove perfection.

It can show that:

- known stress paths have been considered
- constitutional consequence chains actually close
- some provisions are enforced in practice rather than merely stated in theory

It cannot guarantee that:

- institutions will always behave courageously
- judges will always decide correctly
- political culture will always support the constitutional order

The simulator is a disciplined stress-testing tool, not a mathematical proof of constitutional success.

## Why This Matters

Many constitutional projects stop at drafting. This one also asks whether the design can survive bad-faith actors, missed deadlines, coercive pressure, and institutional reluctance.

That makes the draft more than a list of ideals. It makes it a constitutional system that has been repeatedly tested against concrete failure modes.
