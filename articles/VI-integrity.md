# Article VI — Democratic Integrity

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

---

## Scope

Protects the democratic system itself from subversion, foreign interference, and capture of the information environment. This Article has no equivalent in the prior Constitution — it treats bad-faith actors as a design constraint rather than an anomaly. It establishes the rules governing electoral subversion, foreign meddling in elections and politics, and media plurality. Enforcement is primarily through the Constitutional Organs established in Article XII.

Government ethics — corruption, financial conflicts of interest, civil service protection, lobbying, and financial market integrity — is in **[Article VIII — Government Ethics](VIII-government-ethics.md)**. Campaign finance is in **[Article VII — Campaign Finance](VII-campaign-finance.md)**.

---

## Section 1 — Anti-Subversion and Protection of Democratic Processes

### 1.1 — The Core Prohibition

No person shall, by force, fraud, coercion, or abuse of official authority, attempt to prevent, obstruct, or reverse the lawful conduct of a federal election, the counting or certification of votes, or the constitutional transfer of power following a federal election.

### 1.2 — Definition of Subversion

For purposes of this Article, subverting a lawful election or constitutional transfer of power includes:

  (a) Using or directing the use of force, threat of force, or organized intimidation to prevent voters from voting, prevent votes from being counted, or prevent certified results from taking effect

  (b) Using official authority — including military command, law enforcement direction, or control of government agencies — to obstruct, delay, or falsify the counting or certification of votes

  (c) Deliberately destroying, altering, concealing, or fabricating election records, ballots, or certification documents

  (d) Directing or coordinating with foreign governments or foreign nationals to manipulate election outcomes, in violation of Section 5

  (e) Refusing as a public official to perform a mandatory legal duty in the certification or transfer of power process, where that refusal is designed to prevent or delay a lawful transfer. A public official who fails to perform a mandatory electoral duty within the required timeframe is presumed to have acted with intent to subvert; this presumption may be rebutted only by clear and convincing evidence of genuine incapacity.

  (f) Organizing or participating in a conspiracy to achieve any of the above

### 1.3 — What Is Not Subversion

The following do not constitute subversion under this Article, regardless of their political effect:

  (a) Filing lawsuits, petitions, or legal challenges to election results through lawful judicial processes

  (b) Requesting recounts, audits, or reviews through lawful administrative processes

  (c) Publicly claiming that an election was fraudulent or unfair, including through political speech, press releases, or media appearances. The speech protection in this subsection does not extend to false statements of fact about electoral outcomes made with actual knowledge of their falsity or with reckless disregard for their truth, where such statements are made in coordination with a broader subversion effort as defined in §1.2.

  (d) Peaceful protest, demonstration, or assembly regarding election outcomes

  (e) Lobbying election officials, legislators, or courts through lawful means

Subversion requires going outside lawful processes — using force, fraud, or coercion rather than legal and political challenge.

### 1.4 — Penalties

Any person who violates Section 1.1 shall:

  (a) Be permanently disqualified from holding any federal or state public office, appointed or elected, from the date of a judicial finding of violation. Disqualification is immediate upon conviction and does not require a separate proceeding.

  (b) Be subject to criminal prosecution under federal law. Congress shall establish criminal penalties for violations of this Section. No statute of limitations shall apply to offenses under this Section.

  (c) Be ineligible to receive a pardon for any offense arising from the violation. No President, Governor, or other official with pardon authority may pardon or commute a sentence for a violation of this Section. A pardon issued in violation of this prohibition is void.

### 1.5 — Scope of Application

This Section applies to:
  - All federal and state elected officials
  - All appointed federal officials
  - All members of the armed forces and federal law enforcement
  - All private persons who conspire with officials to commit the above acts
  - Candidates for federal office, from the date they file for candidacy

### 1.6 — Adjudication

Charges under this Section shall be prosecuted by the Accountability Commission (Article XII). The Accountability Commission's authority to prosecute under this Section cannot be overridden, transferred, or terminated by the President, Congress, or any other official.

## Section 5 — Foreign Interference

### 5.1 — Prohibition on foreign electoral interference

No foreign government, foreign national, foreign corporation, or entity owned or controlled by foreign interests shall:
  (a) Contribute money or anything of value to any federal election campaign or political committee
  (b) Make expenditures designed to influence any federal election
  (c) Direct, coordinate, or communicate with any candidate, campaign, or political committee regarding strategy, messaging, or operations in connection with a federal election
  (d) Fund political advertising, social media campaigns, or other communications designed to influence federal elections

### 5.2 — Disclosure of foreign contacts

All candidates for federal office, and all senior campaign officials, shall report to the Electoral Commission within 24 hours any offer, communication, or meeting involving a foreign government or foreign national relating to the campaign, any election, or any matter of federal policy. Failure to disclose is a criminal offense. Any campaign official who fails to report within 24 hours is individually liable for that failure regardless of whether the campaign itself reports.

### 5.3 — Foreign lobbying

All persons acting as agents of a foreign government or foreign political party in connection with political activities within the United States shall register with the Accountability Commission and publicly disclose their activities, clients, and compensation on a quarterly basis. Failure to register is a criminal offense.

### 5.4 — Prohibition on acting on foreign electoral assistance

No candidate or campaign official who receives an offer of foreign assistance in connection with a federal election may accept, act upon, or fail to report that offer. Receipt of foreign electoral assistance without reporting it is a violation of Section 1.1 of this Article. Receiving a foreign offer and failing to report it within 24 hours constitutes "acting upon" it for purposes of this Section. Willful blindness as to the foreign origin of an offer is not a defense.

## Section 7 — Media Pluralism and Information Integrity

### 7.1 — Ownership concentration limits

Dangerous concentration of media ownership in too few hands undermines the diversity of information and viewpoint essential to democratic self-governance. Congress shall establish by law limits on the share of national media audience that any single person, corporation, or entity under common ownership or control may reach across all media formats. Congress shall set these limits at levels that ensure genuine plurality of ownership and viewpoint, review them not less than every ten years, and define how audience reach is measured. The specific thresholds are legislative, not constitutional, so they may be updated as the media landscape changes without requiring a constitutional amendment. "National media audience reach" means the aggregate unduplicated weekly audience across all platforms, measured by a methodology established by the independent media regulator. The regulator shall publish the methodology and all underlying data. The cap set by Congress may not exceed 30% of the national audience.

### 7.2 — Local market limits

No single entity may own more than one television broadcast station, one daily newspaper, and one radio station serving the same local market. Congress may establish stricter limits by law.

### 7.3 — Foreign ownership

No foreign government, foreign national, or entity owned or controlled by foreign interests may own or exercise editorial control over any broadcast media outlet or major digital platform operating in the United States. This prohibition applies regardless of the form of ownership, the degree of foreign control, or whether the foreign interest is held directly or through domestic intermediaries. Congress shall establish by statute the thresholds at which foreign ownership is deemed controlling and the procedures for divestiture. The constitutional principle — no foreign control of domestic media — is fixed; the specific ownership percentage thresholds are statutory. For purposes of this Section, foreign "control" means ownership of 10% or more of voting shares, the right to appoint any member of the board of directors, or the ability to direct editorial policy through contract, debt covenant, or any other arrangement. Congress may set lower thresholds by statute but may not set higher ones.

### 7.4 — Public broadcasting

A system of public broadcasting shall be funded through a dedicated mechanism insulated from annual appropriations, to ensure it cannot be defunded as political pressure. Public broadcasting shall operate under an editorial independence charter that prohibits direction by any elected or appointed official. Its leadership shall be appointed by a process that prevents domination by any single political party. No more than 40% of the members of the public broadcasting governing board may be affiliated with any single political party. For purposes of this Section, affiliation is established by party registration, political donations above $500 in the prior five years, or public endorsement of a partisan candidate within the prior two years.

### 7.5 — Algorithmic transparency

Major digital platforms that use algorithmic systems to determine the distribution of political news, political advertising, or content related to elections shall:
  (a) Disclose to the Electoral Commission the criteria and weighting used by their algorithms in distributing such content
  (b) Submit to independent audits of their systems for discriminatory or manipulative amplification
  (c) Provide equal access to advertising tools and distribution for all lawfully registered political candidates and parties

### 7.6 — Enforcement

Congress shall establish an independent regulatory body with authority to enforce media ownership limits. This body shall be structured as an independent agency under Article XIII, removable only for cause.

## Section 8 — Enforcement

**8.1** The independent media regulator established under Section 7.6 shall be structured so that its commissioners are removable only for cause. "Cause" for removal means criminal conviction, a serious ethics violation, or incapacity to perform the duties of office. A commissioner may not be removed on the basis of the content of their regulatory decisions. The regulator shall be funded by fees assessed on entities it regulates and shall not depend on annual congressional appropriation.

The provisions of this Article are enforced by:
  - The **Accountability Commission** (Article XII) for anti-subversion and foreign interference provisions
  - The **Electoral Commission** (Article XII) for campaign finance and election integrity provisions (see Article VII)
  - The **independent media regulator** (Section 7.6) for media ownership provisions
  - See **Article VIII** for enforcement of government ethics, civil service, and lobbying provisions

**8.2** Neither the President, Congress, nor any court may direct either Constitutional Organ in the handling of individual cases under this Article. The Accountability Commission's prosecutorial independence under this Article is part of the unamendable core of Article XI.

**8.3** Any person — including a sitting President, member of Congress, or federal judge — is subject to investigation and prosecution under this Article. No office confers immunity from the provisions of this Article. A sitting President under investigation retains the ability to perform official duties but may not direct or obstruct the investigation.
