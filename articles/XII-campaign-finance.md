# Article XII — Campaign Finance and Political Money

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

---

## Scope

Establishes the constitutional framework governing money in federal and state elections. Moved from Article VI Section 2 to its own article to reflect the scope and importance of campaign finance regulation as a standalone constitutional subject. The core principle: citizens have a right to know who is funding the candidates and causes seeking to govern them. No constitutional right to anonymous political spending exists for non-individual entities. The public financing system ensures that access to office is not limited to the wealthy.

---

## Section 1 — Constitutional Authority and Framework

### 1.1 — Constitutional authority to regulate

Congress shall have the power to regulate, limit, and require disclosure of contributions and expenditures made for the purpose of influencing federal elections. Money spent to influence elections is subject to regulation regardless of the form in which it is spent. This Section supersedes any judicial interpretation holding that political expenditures by corporations, organizations, or other non-individual entities are categorically immune from regulation as a form of protected speech. Individual citizens retain the right to make political expenditures subject to reasonable regulation; regulations on individual political spending remain subject to strict scrutiny. Regulations on corporate, organizational, and other non-individual political spending are not subject to strict scrutiny and need only serve a substantial government interest.

### 1.2 — Mandatory disclosure and tracing

All contributions and expenditures made for the purpose of influencing a federal election shall be publicly disclosed. Disclosure shall include the identity of the original human source of funds — that is, the natural person or persons whose money ultimately funded the contribution or expenditure.

For purposes of this Article, "identified natural person" means a living human being whose legal name, current residential address, and government-issued identification number are on file with the Electoral Commission and have been verified. A nominee, agent, trustee, or intermediary standing in for the beneficial owner does not satisfy the identification requirement — the identification must be of the beneficial owner. The "beneficial owner" is the person who provided the funds, regardless of how many intermediary structures, entities, trusts, or accounts the funds passed through before reaching the political committee or being spent on political activity. Formal legal title does not determine beneficial ownership for purposes of this Article.

Where funds pass through one or more intermediate entities before reaching a political committee or being spent on political activity, each intermediate entity shall be required to disclose its own donors, and those donors shall in turn disclose theirs, until the chain of disclosure reaches identified natural persons. No entity may be used as a conduit to obscure the identity of the original human donor. A contribution or expenditure whose original human source cannot be identified shall be treated as an anonymous contribution and is prohibited under Section 1.3.

### 1.3 — Anonymous contributions prohibited

No anonymous contribution above one hundred dollars may be made to any candidate, political party, political committee, or entity that makes expenditures to influence federal elections. Congress shall set and adjust this threshold by law. A contribution is anonymous if the identity of the original human source cannot be established through the tracing requirements of Section 1.2.

For purposes of identifying and disclosing the original source of contributions, contributions from persons with a common employer, a family relationship within the second degree of kinship, or a shared organizational affiliation — including membership in the same LLC, trust, foundation, club, or other entity — that in aggregate exceed the disclosure threshold are treated as a single contribution from a coordinated source and must be disclosed as such, with all contributing individuals identified. The Electoral Commission shall promulgate rules identifying common affiliation indicators and providing clear guidance on when aggregate contributions trigger coordinated-contribution treatment. This provision is intended to prevent the disaggregation of a single source of funds into many nominally separate contributions for the purpose of evading the disclosure threshold.

### 1.4 — Real-time disclosure

Any political expenditure or contribution above ten thousand dollars shall be publicly disclosed within 24 hours of being made. For expenditures above ten thousand dollars made within 30 days of an election, disclosure is required within 6 hours of being made. Congress may lower but not raise these thresholds or extend these deadlines by statute. Disclosure shall be filed with the Electoral Commission and published immediately in a publicly searchable database. Delayed disclosure that prevents voters from knowing the source of political spending before an election undermines the purpose of this Article and is prohibited.

An expenditure made without timely disclosure as required by this Section is void. The spender shall remediate a void expenditure by withdrawing or retracting the spending to the extent feasible, and by making a corrective disclosure. Media outlets and platforms that publish or continue to run a void expenditure after receiving written notice from the Electoral Commission that the expenditure is void are jointly and severally liable for the violation alongside the original spender. The Electoral Commission shall issue notices of void expenditures promptly upon discovering a disclosure failure.

### 1.5 — Non-profit and tax-exempt organization spending

Any organization holding tax-exempt status under federal law that makes political expenditures shall immediately and fully disclose all donors who contributed one thousand dollars or more to the organization in the twelve months preceding the expenditure. The political activity of a tax-exempt organization does not require that organization to forfeit its tax-exempt status, but it does require full donor transparency with respect to funds used for political purposes. An organization that cannot or will not make the required disclosures may not make political expenditures.

For tax-exempt organizations, the one thousand dollar disclosure threshold applies to cumulative donations from the same source within any rolling 12-month period, not merely to single transactions above the threshold. Separate donations from the same source that individually fall below the threshold but that together exceed one thousand dollars in any rolling 12-month period trigger full disclosure retroactively as of the date the cumulative total crossed the threshold. The Electoral Commission shall require tax-exempt organizations to maintain running tallies of cumulative contributions by donor and report crossing events in real time.

### 1.6 — Prohibition on LLC and partnership political spending

No limited liability company, limited partnership, general partnership, or similar pass-through entity may make a political contribution or expenditure unless it discloses the identity and proportional contribution of every human member, partner, or beneficial owner. An LLC or partnership that does not make this disclosure may not participate in political spending. This provision closes the practice of using newly formed or anonymously owned entities as vehicles to funnel political money without disclosure.

### 1.7 — Coordination defined

An expenditure is not independent of a campaign if the person or entity making the expenditure:
  (a) Shares staff, consultants, vendors, or contractors with the campaign at any point during the election cycle
  (b) Receives, uses, or relies upon non-public information, polling data, voter files, or strategic communications from the campaign
  (c) Makes the expenditure in direct response to a request, suggestion, or signal from the campaign or its agents, whether communicated publicly or privately. For purposes of this subsection, a "signal" includes: (i) public statements, messaging, or advertising by the candidate or campaign that are followed within 72 hours by matching or substantially similar independent expenditures; (ii) shared staff, consultants, vendors, data brokers, or media buyers between the outside spender and the campaign at any point within the prior 2 years; or (iii) communications through intermediaries — including former staff, mutual associates, public events, or media appearances — that reference or relay the candidate's strategic needs, messaging priorities, or targeting preferences. The Electoral Commission shall maintain a rebuttable presumption of coordination when any of these indicators is present; the burden of rebutting the presumption rests on the outside spender.
  (d) Is operated, directed, or controlled by a former senior campaign official within two years of their departure from the campaign

Expenditures that meet any of the above criteria are coordinated expenditures and are subject to the same contribution limits as direct donations to the campaign. Congress shall establish contribution limits by law.

### 1.8 — Foreign money prohibited

No foreign national, foreign government, foreign corporation, or entity owned or controlled by foreign interests may contribute to or make expenditures in connection with any federal, state, or local election in the United States. This prohibition applies regardless of the form of the expenditure, the degree of foreign ownership, or whether the funds are routed through a domestic intermediary. A domestic entity that knowingly or with willful blindness accepts and passes through foreign political funds violates this Section.

An entity is presumptively willfully blind if it does not maintain and follow written procedures adequate to verify the identity, national origin, and citizenship status of its donors before accepting their contributions. The Electoral Commission shall publish minimum verification standards specifying what procedures are necessary to avoid a finding of willful blindness. Failure to adopt and follow those standards is evidence of willful blindness in any enforcement proceeding.

### 1.9 — Corporate and organizational spending

Corporations, labor unions, trade associations, and other legal entities are not natural persons and do not possess the same constitutional rights as individual citizens with respect to political spending. Congress may regulate their political expenditures without satisfying the requirements of strict scrutiny that apply to restrictions on individual political speech.

## Section 2 — Public Financing

### 2.1 — Mandatory public financing system

Congress shall maintain a system of public financing for federal elections sufficient to enable a well-funded competitive campaign for any federal office without reliance on large private donors. Participation by candidates is voluntary. Candidates who accept public financing shall accept a spending limit as a condition of participation.

The public financing system shall be funded through a dedicated mechanism insulated from annual appropriations. Congress may not reduce funding for the public financing system below the prior year's level except by a 2/3 vote of both chambers.

The matching structure shall be set by Congress, subject to the following constitutional floor: small-dollar contributions — defined as contributions of two hundred dollars or less from individuals who are eligible voters in the relevant district or state — shall be matched at a ratio of not less than four to one. Congress may set a higher match ratio. Congress shall adjust dollar thresholds for inflation no less than every six years.

The Accountability Commission shall conduct an annual audit of the public financing system and publish a finding of whether the system is adequately funded to be genuinely competitive in each category of federal race. If the Accountability Commission finds the system is not adequately funded, the President shall submit a supplemental appropriations proposal within 90 days, and Congress shall act on it within 60 days of receipt.

### 2.2 — Electoral Commission parameter authority

The specific dollar amounts, thresholds, and qualifying requirements that govern the campaign finance system shall be set by the Electoral Commission by regulation, not by Congress directly. Congress shall establish by law the constitutional floors and ceilings within which the Electoral Commission must operate — for example, the minimum match ratio, the maximum contribution limit, and the minimum and maximum outside spending cap per race category. The Electoral Commission shall set the specific figures within those bounds.

This division of authority exists because members of Congress are candidates in the elections governed by these rules and have a direct personal interest in the specific parameters. Delegating parameter-setting to an independent body removes the conflict without removing congressional oversight of the overall framework.

The Electoral Commission shall review all dollar parameters not less than every four years. Reviews shall be conducted through a public notice-and-comment process. The Commission shall publish its findings and the basis for any changes.

### 2.3 — Automatic inflation adjustment

All dollar thresholds in this Article — including contribution limits, the small-dollar matching threshold, the anonymous contribution floor, the real-time disclosure trigger, and outside spending caps — shall be automatically adjusted for inflation by the Electoral Commission every two years using the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers. No congressional action is required to implement these adjustments. Congress may by law adjust the index used, but may not suspend or eliminate the automatic adjustment mechanism.

If the Electoral Commission fails to publish an inflation adjustment within the required period, the prior year's amounts remain in effect with the adjustment applied by operation of this Section, calculated by the Accountability Commission and published within 30 days of the deadline passing.

### 2.4 — Incumbency neutrality standard

The public financing system, the qualifying thresholds for matching funds, and all related regulations shall be designed and administered so that a well-organized challenger with demonstrated grassroots support has a realistic path to qualifying for and competing on public financing on substantially equal terms with an incumbent.

The Electoral Commission shall publish annually a finding of whether the public financing system satisfies this standard. The finding shall include data on the qualifying rates of incumbents versus challengers in each race category in the preceding election cycle.

If the Electoral Commission finds the system fails the incumbency neutrality standard, it shall publish proposed regulatory changes within 90 days and implement them before the next election cycle unless Congress acts affirmatively to modify the framework within that period. Courts may enforce this standard on petition by a candidate who demonstrates that the system's design prevented them from qualifying for public financing on terms comparable to their incumbent opponent.

### 2.5 — Cross-chamber campaign finance rules

No chamber of Congress may vote on legislation that sets specific campaign finance parameters — dollar amounts, contribution limits, outside spending caps, or public financing qualifying thresholds — that govern elections to that same chamber. Legislation governing campaign finance for elections to the House of Representatives shall be voted on by the Regional Assembly; legislation governing campaign finance for elections to the Regional Assembly shall be voted on by the House of Representatives. Legislation governing campaign finance for presidential elections shall require approval of both chambers.

Where a single piece of legislation addresses campaign finance for multiple federal offices, the provisions governing each chamber's own elections shall be severable and subject to approval by the other chamber alone before taking effect for that chamber.

### 2.6 — Candidate self-financing and the responsive match

A candidate's expenditure of personal funds in their own campaign is not subject to the outside spending cap of Section 3.1. However, when a candidate's personal spending in their own race exceeds a threshold set by the Electoral Commission, opponents of that candidate who are participating in the public financing system shall receive an enhanced matching rate on small-dollar contributions, up to a maximum set by the Electoral Commission.

The enhanced match exists to preserve the competitive viability of the public financing system against heavily self-financed opponents. It does not cap self-financing — it responds to it. The Electoral Commission shall set the threshold at which the enhanced match activates and the enhanced ratio, calibrated to ensure that a well-organized opponent using public financing can remain competitive against a self-financing candidate.

## Section 3 — Spending Limits

### 3.1 — Aggregate outside spending cap

Congress shall establish by law a cap on the total amount of outside spending — that is, all political expenditures not made by or through a candidate's authorized campaign committee — that may be made in connection with any single federal race during an election cycle. This cap applies to all outside spenders, including corporations, unions, political action committees, nonprofit organizations, political parties, and individual citizens acting independently of any campaign.

For purposes of this Section, "outside spending in connection with a federal race" means any expenditure that:
  (a) Expressly advocates for or against a specific candidate, or
  (b) Mentions or depicts a specific candidate and targets the electorate of that candidate's race, within 120 days of the relevant election

For purposes of this Article, "express advocacy" includes any communication that: (i) names, depicts, or clearly identifies a candidate; (ii) appears or is distributed within 60 days before a primary election or within 90 days before a general election in which that candidate appears; and (iii) would be understood by a reasonable viewer, listener, or reader as either supporting or opposing that candidate's election, based on the totality of its content, tone, and context. The "magic words" test — requiring use of explicit phrases such as "vote for," "elect," or "oppose" — does not apply and is insufficient to determine whether a communication constitutes express advocacy.

The Electoral Commission shall maintain a real-time public registry of outside spending by race, updated continuously as disclosures are received. An expenditure that would cause the aggregate cap for a race to be exceeded is void from the moment it is filed with the Electoral Commission — not from the moment of publication, broadcast, or distribution. A vendor, media outlet, or platform that publishes, broadcasts, or distributes a void outside expenditure is jointly and severally liable for the violation. The Electoral Commission may seek injunctive relief on an expedited basis, including emergency temporary restraining orders, to prevent the publication or continued distribution of void expenditures. Once the aggregate cap for a race is reached, further outside spending in connection with that race is prohibited. Expenditure in excess of the cap after it is reached is a criminal offense prosecutable without a statute of limitations.

The cap does not limit a candidate's own campaign spending, expenditures by candidates' authorized campaign committees, expenditures by political parties on behalf of their own candidates up to a threshold set by Congress, or bona fide news reporting, editorial commentary, and journalistic activity.

### 3.2 — Presumption of coordination for single-candidate expenditures

An outside expenditure that primarily benefits a single identifiable candidate — meaning that more than half of the spending is directed at the electorate of that candidate's race, promotes or attacks that candidate specifically, or is timed and targeted to affect that candidate's election — is presumptively a coordinated expenditure and shall be treated as a direct contribution to that candidate's campaign, subject to applicable contribution limits.

This presumption may be rebutted by the spender demonstrating all of the following to the Electoral Commission:
  (a) No communication of any kind occurred between the spender and the campaign or its agents within the preceding twelve months
  (b) No staff, consultants, vendors, data, or strategy were shared with the campaign at any point during the election cycle
  (c) The expenditure was part of a pre-existing, documented issue advocacy or political program that predates the candidate's announcement, and was not designed around the candidate's specific race

The burden of proof rests on the spender. An entity that cannot establish all three conditions shall have its expenditure treated as coordinated.

### 3.3 — Per-actor aggregate spending limit

No individual, corporation, organization, or other entity may make outside expenditures in connection with federal elections exceeding a total amount per two-year election cycle across all federal races combined. This limit applies regardless of how many separate races the spending is distributed across.

The Electoral Commission shall set the specific aggregate limit by regulation within bounds established by Congress. The per-actor aggregate limit is separate from and cumulative with the per-race cap of Section 3.1. An actor who reaches either cap first may make no further outside expenditures for the remainder of that election cycle.

### 3.4 — Acting in concert

Two or more persons or entities are acting in concert for purposes of this Article if any of the following apply:

  (a) They share a common beneficial owner, controlling person, or primary funding source that has directed or coordinated their political spending. For purposes of this subsection, coordination may be established by circumstantial evidence alone; proof of explicit communication between parties is not required. Circumstantial evidence of acting in concert includes: (i) the same or substantially similar message, advertisement, or content appearing in independent expenditures from different actors within 48 hours of candidate or campaign communications conveying a materially identical message; (ii) sequential spending patterns across multiple actors that suggest central strategic direction, such as spending in the same races in the same order at similar times; or (iii) shared vendors, consultants, data firms, or media buyers between the actors within the prior 2 years.
  (b) They have agreed, explicitly or implicitly, to divide target races, districts, or regions between them to achieve a combined political effect that neither could achieve individually within the applicable caps
  (c) They share strategic direction, targeting data, or spending schedules in a manner designed to coordinate their aggregate effect while maintaining formal separation
  (d) One entity was created, funded, or directed by another for the purpose of multiplying that entity's effective spending beyond the applicable caps

Persons or entities found to be acting in concert shall be treated as a single spender for purposes of the per-race cap and the per-actor aggregate cap. The Electoral Commission shall adjudicate acting-in-concert determinations. The burden of proving that two or more actors are not acting in concert rests on the actors once the Electoral Commission has identified a prima facie basis for the finding.

### 3.5 — Geographic concentration limit

No single outside spender — and no group of persons or entities acting in concert under Section 3.4 — may account for more than a percentage, set by the Electoral Commission, of total outside spending in any single congressional district, state, or region during a two-year election cycle. The constitutional floor for the concentration limit is 15%: the Electoral Commission may set the concentration limit at 15% or lower, but may not set it higher than 15%. "Concentration" for purposes of this Section means the percentage of total outside spending in a specific race that originates from donors, spenders, or entities domiciled in a single state.

The Electoral Commission shall:
  (a) Track the geographic distribution of outside spending in real time
  (b) Set the specific concentration percentage by regulation within the constitutional ceiling established above, with separate thresholds for congressional districts, states, and regions
  (c) Publish a public dashboard showing the geographic distribution of outside spending by actor, updated no less than weekly during the 120 days before any federal election
  (d) Issue a public notice when any actor reaches 75% of the applicable concentration threshold in any geography

The Commission shall set stricter concentration thresholds for outside spending directed at Regional Assembly elections within a single region than for House elections within a single district, reflecting the higher stakes of regional dominance.

### 3.6 — Pre-window spending

The disclosure requirements of Sections 1.2 through 1.4, the foreign money prohibition of Section 1.8, and the coordination rules of Section 1.7 apply to all political expenditures regardless of their timing relative to an election.

The outside spending cap of Section 3.1 and the geographic concentration limit of Section 3.5 apply within 120 days of a federal election. Outside those windows, the per-actor aggregate cap of Section 3.3 accumulates across the full two-year cycle. Any expenditure that mentions or depicts a candidate outside the 120-day window and is subsequently followed by substantially similar expenditures within the window shall be treated as part of a unified campaign for purposes of the coordination presumption under Section 3.2.

### 3.7 — Pre-election spending blackout

No new outside expenditure above a threshold set by the Electoral Commission may be initiated in connection with a federal race during the 72-hour period immediately preceding the opening of polls in that race. This blackout applies to first-time placements of new advertisements, new direct mail, new digital campaigns, and new voter contact programs.

The blackout does not prohibit the continued running of advertisements already placed and publicly disclosed before the blackout period begins, news reporting and editorial commentary, or candidate campaign expenditures including get-out-the-vote activities.

## Section 4 — Source Restrictions

### 4.1 — Government contractors

No entity holding an active federal contract with a total value above a threshold set by the Electoral Commission may make any political contribution or outside expenditure in connection with any federal election during the term of that contract and for two years following its completion. This prohibition applies to the contracting entity, any parent company or affiliate under common ownership, and any political action committee established or controlled by the contracting entity or its officers.

The Electoral Commission shall maintain a publicly searchable registry of all entities currently subject to this prohibition. A contractor that makes a prohibited political expenditure shall be disqualified from federal contracting for five years.

### 4.2 — Cryptocurrency and digital assets

No contribution or expenditure for the purpose of influencing a federal election may be made using cryptocurrency, digital tokens, or any blockchain-based asset unless the full identity chain from the originating wallet to an identified natural person can be verified and disclosed to the Electoral Commission in compliance with Section 1.2.

No privacy-preserving protocol, mixing service, or anonymizing layer may be used in connection with a political contribution or expenditure. Use of such a protocol to obscure the source of political funds is a criminal offense.

### 4.3 — In-kind contributions

Non-monetary contributions of value made to a candidate, campaign, political committee, or outside spender are contributions for purposes of this Article and are subject to the same limits, disclosure requirements, and source restrictions as monetary contributions. In-kind contributions include free or below-market use of aircraft, vehicles, real property, equipment, professional services, data, and hospitality. The fair market value shall be determined by the Electoral Commission and reported as a contribution at that value.

## Section 5 — Transition, Inaugural, and Post-Election

### 5.1 — Presidential transition: public funding only

The presidential transition is a governmental function and shall be funded exclusively by federal appropriation. No private contribution, donation, gift, in-kind service, or anything of value may be accepted by a presidential transition for transition operations. Campaign funds raised for the purpose of winning an election may not be converted to transition use. Remaining campaign funds after the election shall be returned to donors, donated to the United States Treasury, reserved for future permitted political activity, or donated to a registered nonprofit not controlled by the candidate.

Campaign funds reserved for "future permitted political activity" must be publicly reported to the Electoral Commission within 30 days of the election, with a statement of the total amount reserved. The amount reserved for future political activity may not exceed the candidate's average per-cycle campaign spending across the prior two election cycles in which that candidate ran. Funds reserved in excess of this cap must be returned to donors or donated to the public financing fund established under Section 2.1 within 90 days of the election. The Electoral Commission shall calculate and publish the applicable cap for each candidate within 15 days of the election.

Congress shall appropriate sufficient funds in each presidential election year to cover the full operational costs of a presidential transition. The Accountability Commission shall audit each transition and publish a finding of whether the appropriation was adequate.

The outgoing administration shall fully cooperate with the incoming transition regardless of funding. Withholding cooperation as leverage over funding decisions is a violation of Article III Section 13 and grounds for impeachment. Where such conduct uses abuse of official authority to obstruct the lawful constitutional transfer of power, it also constitutes a violation of Article VI Section 1.1.

### 5.2 — Inaugural ceremonies: regulated private fundraising

Presidential inaugural ceremonies are distinct from transition operations and may be funded through a combination of federal appropriation and private fundraising, subject to: registration of an inaugural committee with the Electoral Commission within 14 days of the election; the same disclosure, source, and contribution limit requirements that apply to campaign contributions; prohibition on foreign national, foreign government, and government contractor contributions; real-time disclosure under Section 1.4; unspent funds donated to the Treasury or a qualifying nonprofit within 90 days; and strict segregation from transition funds.

### 5.3 — Post-election debt retirement

Campaign debt incurred during a federal election campaign remains subject to the contribution limits and disclosure requirements of this Article for four years following the election. An official who retires campaign debt through contributions from entities with active matters before their office shall be subject to mandatory recusal from those matters for two years from the date of the contribution.

### 5.4 — Leadership political committees

Any political committee established, maintained, or controlled by a sitting member of Congress or the Regional Assembly shall be subject to the same contribution limits and disclosure requirements that apply to campaign committees. Funds may not be converted to the official's personal use. The Electoral Commission shall publish annual findings on the use patterns of leadership committees.

### 5.5 — Political advertising transparency

Any person or entity that purchases or places political advertising — defined as any paid communication that promotes, attacks, supports, or opposes a federal candidate, political party, or ballot measure — shall disclose to the Electoral Commission: the content of every distinct version of the advertisement; the targeting parameters used; the platform or medium; and the total expenditure and impressions reached by each version. Disclosure shall be filed within 48 hours of first appearance. The Electoral Commission shall maintain a public archive searchable for not less than ten years after each election.

## Section 6 — Enforcement

### 6.1 — Administration and prosecution

The Electoral Commission (Article IX) shall administer campaign finance disclosure and enforce the requirements of this Article. The Accountability Commission (Article IX) shall prosecute criminal violations. Willful failure to disclose, willful use of a shell entity to conceal the source of political funds, willful use of cryptocurrency to obscure a contribution's source, and willful acceptance of foreign political money are criminal offenses subject to prosecution without a statute of limitations.

### 6.2 — Tiered enforcement

Enforcement under this Article follows the tiered framework established in Article IX Section 3: minor and technical violations are handled by Electoral Commission automated civil enforcement; substantive violations are formally investigated by the Electoral Commission's Campaign Finance Enforcement Division; willful criminal violations are referred to the Accountability Commission for prosecution.

---

## Section 7 — State Elections Floor

The minimum campaign finance standards applicable to state and local elections are established in Article VII Section 1.7. This Article's full framework applies to federal elections. States are encouraged to adopt equivalent or stricter standards for their own elections and may access the federal public financing infrastructure under Article VII Section 1.8 upon doing so.
