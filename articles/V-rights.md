# Article V — Rights and Freedoms

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

---

## Scope

Enumerates and protects the civil and political rights of all persons within the United States. This Article replaces and expands the Bill of Rights and all rights-bearing amendments of the prior Constitution. Rights enumerated here are a floor — states may expand but may not reduce them. The enumeration of specific rights shall not be construed to deny or disparage others retained by the people.

Social, economic, environmental, and affirmative rights — including environmental rights, healthcare, education, housing, economic rights, and children's rights — are established in **[Article XIII — Social, Economic, and Affirmative Rights](XIII-social-economic-rights.md)**. The general principles of Section 1 of this Article apply to all rights in both articles.

---

## Section 1 — General Principles

**1.1** The rights in this Article belong to all persons within the jurisdiction of the United States, regardless of citizenship, unless a specific right is expressly limited to citizens.

**1.2** Rights are not absolute. They may be limited only where a law or government action serves a compelling public interest, is narrowly tailored to that interest, and uses the least restrictive means available. Courts shall apply strict scrutiny to any law that burdens a right enumerated in this Article.

**1.3** No right may be permanently suspended. Temporary suspension of specific rights requires a 2/3 vote of both chambers of Congress plus a judicial finding that the suspension is narrowly tailored and necessary. Any such suspension expires automatically after 90 days and may be renewed only by the same process. The required judicial finding must be issued by the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals or the Supreme Court. Any registered voter has standing to challenge such a suspension in court. Courts must rule on any such challenge within 72 hours of filing. A suspension for which no timely ruling is issued automatically lapses.

**1.4 Absolute rights.** The following rights may never be suspended under any circumstances, including declared emergencies, by any majority, at any time:
  (a) Freedom from torture, cruel treatment, and degrading punishment
  (b) Freedom from slavery and involuntary servitude
  (c) The right to habeas corpus, except as provided in Article III, Section 5.7. Habeas corpus may not be suspended in the same geographic area for more than 120 consecutive days without a new judicial finding under §1.3. Successive renewals of suspension in the same area are subject to heightened scrutiny and require clear and convincing evidence of ongoing necessity.
  (d) The right not to be punished for an act that was not a crime at the time it was committed
  (e) The right of equal protection before the law

**1.5 Rights floor.** No state law, state constitution, or state action may reduce or eliminate any right guaranteed by this Article. States may enact laws that expand rights beyond those guaranteed here.

**1.6 Unenumerated rights.** The rights listed in this Article are not exhaustive. The federal courts shall recognize and protect additional rights where they are deeply rooted in the history and traditions of a free people or are necessary to the concept of ordered liberty. The enumeration of rights in this Article shall not be used to deny or disparage unenumerated rights.

## Section 2 — Free Expression and Assembly

**2.1 Free speech.** Every person has the right to freedom of speech and expression. Congress and the states shall make no law abridging freedom of speech, except laws narrowly targeting:
  (a) Direct incitement of imminent lawless action
  (b) True threats of violence against specific individuals
  (c) Defamation — false statements of fact made with knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard for truth
  (d) Obscenity as defined by law, excluding material with serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value
  (e) Targeted harassment campaigns — coordinated conduct directed at a specific individual, based on their race, religion, sex, gender identity, sexual orientation, national origin, or disability, that is designed and reasonably likely to drive that individual from public life or to prevent them from exercising a right guaranteed by this Constitution. Coordination is established when two or more actors act pursuant to a common plan or understanding, whether explicit or implied; circumstantial evidence of coordination is sufficient. Platforms that knowingly host or amplify coordinated harassment campaigns share liability for the harm caused.
  (f) Official incitement to discriminate — speech by a government official acting in their official capacity that explicitly or implicitly directs, authorizes, or encourages the differential treatment of persons based on a protected characteristic in the exercise of government power. Official incitement includes implicit directives. A "specific directive" includes statements made in an official capacity that a reasonable person would interpret as an instruction to discriminate, even if not phrased as a direct order.

**2.2 Definitions for Section 2.1(e) and (f).**

  (i) *Targeted harassment campaign* requires: (1) conduct directed at a specific, identifiable individual rather than a group; (2) a pattern of repeated acts rather than a single expression of a view; (3) demonstrable intent to suppress that individual's participation in public life or exercise of rights; and (4) a nexus to a protected characteristic. Expressing a negative view of a group, religion, ideology, or political position — even in offensive or inflammatory terms — does not constitute a targeted harassment campaign regardless of the reaction it produces.

  (ii) *Official incitement to discriminate* requires: (1) the speaker acting in an official governmental capacity, not as a private citizen; (2) a specific directive or authorization, not merely an expressed opinion; and (3) direction toward the exercise of government power, such as enforcement, licensing, procurement, or public services. A government official expressing personal views outside their official duties retains the same speech rights as any private citizen.

  (iii) These exceptions shall be construed narrowly. Courts shall not extend them to cover: expression of offensive views about any group; religious condemnation of beliefs or practices; academic, journalistic, or artistic engagement with difficult subject matter; satire or parody; or political speech about immigration, religion, race, or any other social group, however inflammatory.

**2.3 Free press.** Freedom of the press is guaranteed. No prior restraint on publication shall be imposed by government except upon a judicial finding of immediate and grave harm to national security that cannot be addressed after publication. Journalists shall not be compelled to reveal confidential sources in any judicial, legislative, or executive proceeding, except upon a judicial finding that the information is essential to preventing imminent serious harm and cannot be obtained by other means. For purposes of this Section, "imminent serious harm" requires that (a) the harm be specific and credible, (b) no alternative means to prevent it exist, and (c) the court be satisfied by clear and convincing evidence. Vague or speculative harm does not qualify.

**2.4 Assembly and petition.** Every person has the right to peaceably assemble and to petition the government for redress of grievances. The government may impose reasonable time, place, and manner restrictions on assembly but may not restrict assembly based on the viewpoint or content of the message being expressed.

**2.5 Association.** Every person has the right to freedom of association — to form, join, or refuse to join any organization, political party, or group. No person shall be compelled to associate with any organization whose values they do not share.

**2.6 Political expression.** Political speech receives the highest level of protection under this Article. Laws targeting speech on the basis of its political content or viewpoint are void.

## Section 3 — Religion

**3.1 Free exercise.** Every person has the right to freedom of religion and belief, including the right to hold any religious belief or none, to practice religion individually or collectively, and to change their religion or belief. No person shall be persecuted, disadvantaged, or discriminated against by the government on account of their religion or absence of religion.

**3.2 No establishment.** Congress and the states shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, providing financial support that primarily benefits religious organizations, or requiring participation in or profession of any religious belief as a condition of any public benefit, office, or obligation.

**3.3 Government neutrality.** Government at all levels — federal, state, and local — must remain neutral among religions and between religion and non-religion. Government may not:

  (a) Favor, endorse, or promote any religion, denomination, or religious belief over others
  (b) Favor religion in general over non-belief, or non-belief over religion
  (c) Display religious symbols, conduct religious ceremonies, or issue religious proclamations in an official governmental capacity
  (d) Declare any religion, religious text, or religious figure as official, preferred, or representative of the nation

Neutrality does not require hostility to religion. Government may acknowledge the role of religion in history, culture, and public life in an educational and neutral manner. It may not use that acknowledgment to endorse any particular religious viewpoint.

**3.4 Religion in public schools.** In public schools and other government educational institutions:

  (a) No school official, teacher, or employee acting in an official capacity may lead, organize, or participate in prayer or religious worship
  (b) No student may be required, pressured, or given an academic incentive to participate in any religious activity, prayer, or expression of religious belief
  (c) No religious doctrine may be taught as factual or scientific truth in any subject area
  (d) Students retain the right to pray privately, form voluntary religious clubs on the same terms as other student organizations, and express their own religious views — government neutrality does not suppress student religious expression

**3.5 Religious exemptions may not harm third parties.** Where Congress or a state provides a religious exemption from a law of general applicability, that exemption may not be used in a manner that:

  (a) Denies any individual a service, right, or benefit they are legally entitled to receive
  (b) Imposes the costs of the religious belief on persons who do not share it
  (c) Allows a person or institution to discriminate against others in public accommodations, employment, or the provision of services on the basis of race, sex, sexual orientation, gender identity, religion, national origin, or disability

The religious freedom of one person ends where it becomes the imposition of that person's religion on another. A religious exemption claimed to avoid a general obligation is valid only where the burden falls solely on the person claiming the exemption, not on third parties.

**3.6 Secular basis required for legislation.** No law may be enacted, and no regulation may be adopted, whose only cognizable justification is the advancement of a religious belief, the enforcement of a religious doctrine, or compliance with a religious authority's position. A law must have an independent secular purpose — a legitimate government interest that can be explained and defended without reference to religious belief — to be constitutionally valid. The existence of religious support for a law does not invalidate it; the absence of any secular justification does.

**3.7 Conscientious objection.** Every person with a sincere religious or moral conviction against participation in armed combat has the right to conscientious objector status. Congress shall establish by law the procedures for claiming this status and the forms of alternative service that satisfy the national service obligation. Conscientious objector status may not be denied on the ground that the objection is based on moral rather than traditionally religious grounds, provided the conviction is sincere and deeply held. This Section does not exempt any person from all civic obligations — it requires that alternative means of fulfilling those obligations be available.

## Section 4 — Privacy and Personal Autonomy

**4.1 Right to privacy.** Every person has the right to privacy in their person, home, communications, data, and personal affairs. This right encompasses:
  (a) **Bodily autonomy** — the right to make decisions about one's own body, medical treatment, and reproductive choices, free from government compulsion or prohibition
  (b) **Domestic privacy** — the right to be free from government intrusion into the home without lawful process
  (c) **Communicative privacy** — the right to private communications, including electronic communications, free from surveillance without a warrant
  (d) **Data privacy** — the right to control personal information collected, stored, or processed by government or private entities, as further defined in Section 10

**4.2** No law may compel a person to undergo a medical procedure, disclose a medical condition, or make a medical decision against their will, except that laws requiring vaccination may be enacted where a compelling public health interest is demonstrated and the law is narrowly tailored. Any compulsory vaccination law must: (a) be limited to diseases with a basic reproduction number (R0) above a threshold established by Congress based on current public health science; (b) contain exemptions for persons with a documented medical contraindication and for persons with a sincere religious objection; and (c) not apply to any person who can demonstrate natural immunity through recognized diagnostic testing.

**4.3** The right of privacy in Section 4.1(a) includes the right to make decisions about marriage, family, contraception, and intimate relationships without government interference, regardless of the sex, gender, or sexual orientation of the persons involved.

## Section 5 — Security of Person and Home

**5.1 Search and seizure.** Every person has the right to be free from unreasonable searches and seizures of their person, home, papers, effects, and digital data. No warrant shall issue except upon probable cause, supported by oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the place to be searched and the persons or things to be seized.

**5.2 Digital searches.** A warrant is required to search or seize:
  (a) Electronic devices, accounts, or stored data
  (b) Communications in transit or in storage
  (c) Location data, whether real-time or historical
  (d) Metadata revealing patterns of communication, movement, or association

A warrant authorizing a digital search must specify the data to be accessed and may not authorize bulk collection of data beyond what is necessary for the stated purpose. Digital search warrants must specify individual accounts, devices, or files with particularity; category-based warrants (such as "all emails containing a given term") are void. Where a lawful warrant produces a bulk data return, the data must be filtered to the scope of the warrant before any government official reviews it, and all surplus data must be destroyed within 30 days of collection.

**5.3 Bulk surveillance.** No government agency may conduct bulk surveillance of the communications, location, or data of persons within the United States without an individualized warrant. Mass collection programs targeting the general population are prohibited. For purposes of this Section, "individualized" means a warrant specifying a named individual or a specific device linked to that individual. Population-based collection — such as collection targeting all persons within a geographic area — does not satisfy the individualized requirement.

**5.4 Property.** Private property shall not be taken for public use without just compensation. Regulatory action that effectively destroys substantially all economic value of property shall be treated as a taking requiring compensation.

## Section 6 — Due Process and Fair Treatment

**6.1 Due process.** No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. Due process requires both fair procedures and that laws themselves not be arbitrary, irrational, or fundamentally unjust.

**6.2 Equal protection.** No person shall be denied the equal protection of the laws. Government shall not discriminate on the basis of race, color, sex, gender identity, sexual orientation, religion, national origin, disability, or age in the provision of public services, the enforcement of laws, or the exercise of government power.

**6.3 Retroactive laws.** No person shall be convicted of an act that was not a crime at the time it was committed. No law shall increase the punishment for an offense after the offense was committed.

**6.4 Double jeopardy.** No person shall be tried twice for the same offense after acquittal or conviction.

**6.5 Self-incrimination.** No person shall be compelled in any criminal proceeding to be a witness against themselves.

**6.6 Jury trial — criminal.** In all serious criminal prosecutions, the accused shall have the right to a speedy and public trial by an impartial jury. A serious criminal prosecution is defined as one in which the defendant faces more than six months of imprisonment.

**6.7 Jury trial — civil.** In civil cases where the amount in controversy exceeds ten thousand dollars, adjusted periodically for inflation by act of Congress, either party may demand trial by jury.

**6.8 Right to counsel.** In any proceeding in which a person's liberty is at stake, the accused shall have the right to counsel. Where the accused cannot afford counsel, the government shall provide it. In civil matters where a person faces loss of housing, custody of children, or immigration status, the government shall provide or fund counsel where the opposing party is represented by counsel.

**6.9 Speedy trial.** A person accused of a crime shall be brought to trial within a reasonable time. No person shall be held in pretrial detention for more than 180 days without trial, except upon a judicial finding of exceptional circumstances.

**6.10 Bail.** Bail shall not be set at an amount designed to ensure detention rather than appearance. The purpose of bail is to secure appearance at trial, not to punish. A person may be detained without bail only upon a judicial finding that they pose a specific and serious risk of harm to an identified person that cannot be addressed by other conditions of release. Bail is presumptively set at an amount the defendant can pay within 72 hours given their actual financial means. Any bail set above that amount requires a specific written judicial finding that lower bail creates a genuine flight risk. A defendant's inability to pay is not itself evidence of flight risk.

## Section 7 — Equal Rights

**7.1** Women and men have equal rights throughout the United States. No law or government action shall abridge or deny equal rights on account of sex or gender.

**7.2** All persons are equal before the law regardless of race, color, national origin, ethnicity, religion, sex, gender identity, sexual orientation, disability, or age. Laws that classify persons on any of these bases are presumptively unconstitutional and must satisfy strict scrutiny.

**7.3** No person shall be subjected to discrimination by any entity receiving federal funding or exercising a public function on the basis of any characteristic listed in Section 7.2. For purposes of this Section, "federal funding" includes direct grants, contracts, tax exemptions with an annual value above $100,000, and federally guaranteed loans. Private entities receiving any such benefit are subject to the prohibition in this Section.

**7.4** Slavery and involuntary servitude are abolished and prohibited throughout the United States and in any place subject to its jurisdiction. No exception shall exist for punishment of crime.

**7.5** All citizens have the right to vote and to stand for election, as established in Article I. This right shall not be denied or abridged on any basis.

**7.6** Persons with disabilities have the right to full and equal participation in public life, including access to public facilities, transportation, employment, education, and government services. Government shall take reasonable steps to ensure this access.

## Section 8 — The Right to Keep and Bear Arms

**8.1** Every citizen has the right to keep and bear arms for the purpose of self-defense and the defense of their home and family. This right shall not be infringed except by laws that are narrowly tailored and serve a compelling public safety interest.

**8.2** Congress and the states may enact laws governing:
  (a) Background checks and waiting periods prior to the acquisition of firearms
  (b) Licensing, registration, and safety training requirements
  (c) The prohibition or regulation of specific categories of weapons whose primary design purpose is the efficient killing of large numbers of people. "Primary design purpose" is determined objectively by the weapon's mechanical capabilities, not the manufacturer's marketing claims. A weapon that is capable of firing more than one round per trigger pull, or that is capable of accepting a magazine of more than 10 rounds, is presumptively a weapon whose primary design purpose is mass-casualty use.
  (d) The carrying of firearms in sensitive locations including schools, government buildings, polling places, and places of worship
  (e) The possession of firearms by persons convicted of violent felonies or subject to domestic violence orders

**8.3** The right in Section 8.1 does not extend to weapons designed exclusively for military use, including fully automatic weapons, explosive ordnance, and crew-served weapons. Congress may by law define the boundary between protected and unprotected weapons categories.

**8.4** Nothing in this Section shall be construed to prevent the government from maintaining a professional standing military or to prevent the arming of law enforcement officers.

## Section 9 — Rights of the Accused and Punishments

**9.1 Presumption of innocence.** Every person accused of a crime is presumed innocent until proven guilty beyond a reasonable doubt.

**9.2 Right to know charges.** Every accused person has the right to be informed promptly of the charges against them in a language they understand, to have adequate time to prepare a defense, and to examine the evidence against them.

**9.3 Right to confront witnesses.** Every accused person has the right to confront the witnesses against them and to compel the attendance of witnesses in their favor.

**9.4 Cruel and unusual punishment.** No person shall be subjected to torture, cruel, unusual, or degrading punishment. Punishment shall be proportionate to the offense. The conditions of incarceration shall preserve the basic dignity of the incarcerated person.

**9.4A — Death penalty.** The death penalty is neither required nor prohibited by this Constitution. A sentence of death carried out under the strict procedural conditions of this Section is not deemed to constitute cruel, unusual, or degrading punishment under Section 9.4 or a violation of the absolute rights enumerated in Section 1.4. Congress may by law authorize or abolish capital punishment. Any law authorizing the death penalty shall:
  (a) Limit its application to a defined category of the most serious offenses
  (b) Require a unanimous jury finding that the death penalty is warranted
  (c) Provide for mandatory appellate review in every case
  (d) Include procedures specifically designed to prevent the execution of innocent persons
  (e) Be administered in a manner demonstrably free of racial, geographic, or other invidious disparity in application
  (f) Subject jury selection in capital cases to independent statistical review for racial bias. If such a review finds racial bias in the composition of a capital jury, the conviction is automatically vacated and the case must be retried.

A law authorizing capital punishment that fails to meet these requirements is unconstitutional as a violation of Section 9.4.

**9.5 Solitary confinement.** Extended solitary confinement — defined as isolation for more than fifteen consecutive days — constitutes cruel and unusual punishment and is prohibited, except where a judicial finding determines it is strictly necessary to protect the safety of other incarcerated persons or staff, for a defined period not to exceed 90 days.

**9.6 Rehabilitation.** The purpose of criminal punishment includes rehabilitation and the reintegration of persons into society. Punishments that serve no purpose beyond retribution or that permanently foreclose reintegration are disfavored.

**9.7 Rights of incarcerated persons.** Persons deprived of liberty retain all rights guaranteed by this Article that are not necessarily restricted by their lawful incarceration. Incarceration does not extinguish the right to due process, to be free from torture, to practice religion, or to communicate with legal counsel.

## Section 10 — Digital Rights

**10.1** Every person has the right to protection of their personal data. Personal data is information that identifies or is reasonably capable of identifying a specific person.

**10.2** Every person has the right to:
  (a) Know what personal data about them is collected and held by government agencies or large private entities
  (b) Access that data upon request
  (c) Correct inaccurate data
  (d) Request deletion of data that is no longer necessary for its stated purpose, subject to lawful retention requirements
  (e) Be informed when their data has been disclosed to a third party or compromised in a breach

**10.3** No government agency may purchase personal data from private entities to circumvent the warrant requirement that would apply to direct government collection of the same data. This prohibition extends to all acquisition of personal data through private intermediaries: government agencies may not purchase, receive as a voluntary tip, or otherwise obtain personal data through any private party if they could not have acquired that data directly under this Article. Data obtained through private intermediaries is treated as government-obtained data for all constitutional purposes.

**10.4 Algorithmic due process.** No significant decision affecting a person's liberty, employment, housing, credit, benefits, or immigration status may be made solely by automated means without human review. Where automated systems are used in such decisions, the person affected has the right to:
  (a) Know that an automated system was used
  (b) Receive a meaningful explanation of the basis for the decision
  (c) Have the decision reviewed by a human official upon request

"Meaningful human review" requires that the reviewing human have (a) access to all inputs and outputs of the automated system, (b) actual authority to override the decision, and (c) documented their independent judgment in the matter. Rubber-stamp review that does not meet all three requirements does not satisfy this Section.

**10.5** Surveillance of persons in public spaces using facial recognition or other biometric identification by government agencies requires a warrant based on individualized suspicion. Indiscriminate mass biometric surveillance of the general public in public spaces is prohibited. The individualized suspicion required by this Section must be established before the identification occurs; post-hoc warrant applications based on bulk biometric collection are void.

**10.6 — Right to internet access.** Every person has the right to access the public internet. The government may not deny, restrict, or throttle an individual's access to the public internet as punishment, as political coercion, or on the basis of their political views or associations. Congress shall ensure that basic internet infrastructure is available to all residents regardless of geographic location or income. This right applies to access to public internet infrastructure; it does not require any specific private provider to offer service, nor does it guarantee any particular speed or quality beyond basic access. Internet service providers operating under a federal or state franchise, license, or right-of-way agreement are subject to the same prohibition as government actors under this Section; denial or throttling of service as punishment or political coercion by any such provider is treated as state action for all constitutional purposes.

## Section 14 — Rights in Emergency

**14.1** A declaration of national emergency does not suspend any right enumerated in this Article unless Congress separately authorizes a specific suspension under Section 1.3.

**14.2** The following rights may never be suspended under any emergency:
  (a) All absolute rights under Section 1.4
  (b) The right to vote and to have votes counted
  (c) Freedom from discrimination under Section 7
  (d) The right to counsel under Section 6.8. In any declared emergency, the government shall ensure adequate public defender capacity to serve all persons subject to arrest or detention. Where the public defender system is overwhelmed by emergency-related caseloads, courts shall appoint private counsel at public expense. Delay in appointing counsel beyond 72 hours from the time of arrest or detention is a per se constitutional violation.
  (e) The right to access courts and seek judicial review

**14.3** Any suspension of rights authorized under Section 1.3 must be limited to the geographic area and population directly affected by the emergency. A suspension applicable to the entire nation requires a judicial finding that the emergency itself affects the entire nation.

## Section 16 — Conflicting Rights

**16.1** Where two or more rights enumerated in this Article conflict, courts shall seek interpretations that give maximum effect to each right. Where a full conflict cannot be avoided, courts shall consider:
  (a) The relative weight of the interests at stake for each right holder
  (b) Whether one right directly enables the exercise of another (the more foundational right is given greater weight)
  (c) The proportionality of any limitation imposed on one right to protect another

**16.2** No right in this Article shall be interpreted to permit the use of that right as a weapon to destroy the rights of others.
