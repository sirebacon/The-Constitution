# Article II — The Legislature

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

---

## Scope

Establishes the structure, composition, powers, and procedures of the federal legislature. The legislature consists of two chambers: the House of Representatives and the Regional Assembly. Together they form the Congress of the United States.

---

## Section 1 — The Congress

**1.1** All federal legislative power is vested in the Congress of the United States, which shall consist of the House of Representatives and the Regional Assembly.

**1.2** The Congress shall meet in regular session beginning on the third day of January each year. Neither chamber may adjourn for more than three days without the consent of the other, nor may either chamber adjourn to a location outside the seat of government without the consent of both chambers and the President.

**1.3** The proceedings of both chambers shall be recorded and published in full. No session of either chamber may be closed to the public record except by a 2/3 vote of the chamber for reasons of national security, and the fact of the closed session shall itself be published.

---

## Section 2 — The House of Representatives

**2.1 Composition.** The House of Representatives shall consist of 500 members elected from congressional districts as established under Article I.

**2.2 Terms.** Members of the House shall serve four-year terms, with districts staggered into two groups as provided in Article I, Section 4.3.

**2.3 Qualifications.** A member of the House must be:
  (a) At least 21 years of age at the time of taking office
  (b) A citizen of the United States for at least five years
  (c) A resident of the state from which they are elected at the time of election

**2.4 Speaker.** The House shall elect a Speaker from among its members at the beginning of each new Congress. The Speaker shall preside over the House, set the legislative calendar, and represent the House in relations with the other branches. The Speaker may be removed by a majority vote of the House at any time.

**2.5 Officers.** The House shall elect such other officers as it deems necessary. No officer of the House may simultaneously hold any other federal office.

---

## Section 3 — The Regional Assembly

**3.1 Composition.** The Regional Assembly shall consist of approximately 100 members elected from the states within each geographic region, as defined in Section 4. The precise number of seats shall be reapportioned following each census as provided in Article I, Section 7.

**3.2 Terms.** Members of the Regional Assembly shall serve six-year terms. Seats within each region shall be divided into three equal classes rotating on a two-year election cycle, as provided in Article I, Section 5.2.

**3.3 Qualifications.** A member of the Regional Assembly must be:
  (a) At least 25 years of age at the time of taking office
  (b) A citizen of the United States for at least seven years
  (c) A resident of the state from which they are elected at the time of election

**3.4 President of the Regional Assembly.** The Vice President of the United States shall preside over the Regional Assembly but shall have no vote except to break a tie. The Regional Assembly shall also elect a President Pro Tempore from among its members to preside in the Vice President's absence.

**3.5 Officers.** The Regional Assembly shall elect such other officers as it deems necessary.

---

## Section 4 — Geographic Regions

The states of the United States are organized into the following eight geographic regions for purposes of Regional Assembly apportionment. Regional boundaries may be altered only by constitutional amendment under Article VIII, Track 1.

| Region | States |
|--------|--------|
| **Northeast** | Maine, Vermont, New Hampshire, Massachusetts, Rhode Island, Connecticut, New York, New Jersey |
| **Mid-Atlantic** | Pennsylvania, Delaware, Maryland, Virginia, West Virginia |
| **Southeast** | North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia, Florida |
| **South** | Alabama, Mississippi, Tennessee, Kentucky, Arkansas, Louisiana |
| **Midwest** | Ohio, Indiana, Michigan, Wisconsin, Illinois, Minnesota, Iowa, Missouri |
| **Great Plains** | North Dakota, South Dakota, Nebraska, Kansas, Oklahoma, Texas |
| **Mountain West** | Montana, Idaho, Wyoming, Colorado, Utah, Nevada, Arizona, New Mexico |
| **Pacific** | Washington, Oregon, California, Alaska, Hawaii |

**4.1** Washington D.C. shall be treated as a state for purposes of Regional Assembly representation and shall be assigned to the Mid-Atlantic region.

**4.2** Any territory admitted as a state shall be assigned to the most geographically appropriate region by act of Congress at the time of admission.

**4.3** Seats within each region shall be allocated among the states of that region in proportion to their populations following each census, with a minimum of one seat per state.

**4.4** Each region shall be guaranteed a minimum of eight seats in the Regional Assembly regardless of population, to ensure regional coherence. Seats above this floor shall be allocated among all regions in proportion to national population.

---

## Section 5 — Disqualification and Removal

**5.1** No person shall serve in Congress who has been convicted of a felony involving corruption, fraud, or abuse of public office, unless pardoned in accordance with Article III.

**5.2** No person shall simultaneously serve as a member of Congress and hold any other federal office, state office, or position of profit under the United States.

**5.3** A member of Congress may be expelled by a 2/3 vote of their chamber. Grounds for expulsion include conviction of a felony, a finding of serious ethics violation by the Congressional Ethics Commission, or conduct that the chamber determines to be incompatible with the duties of office.

**5.3A** Members of the House of Representatives and the Regional Assembly are subject to constituent-initiated recall under Article I, Section 13. Recall is a separate mechanism from expulsion and does not require a finding of misconduct — it reflects loss of constituent confidence. The procedures, thresholds, and restrictions governing recall are set out in Article I, Section 13.

**5.4** A member under active criminal indictment for a felony involving their official duties shall be suspended from voting in their chamber pending the outcome of proceedings, but shall not lose their seat until conviction.

**5.5 — Felony conviction and removal: two categories.**

  **(a) Category 1 — Corruption and abuse of office: automatic removal.** A member of Congress who is finally convicted of any of the following offenses shall be automatically removed from office upon the exhaustion of all appeals or the expiration of the time to appeal, whichever is earlier: bribery; extortion; fraud committed in an official capacity; self-dealing or misuse of public funds; perjury or obstruction of justice in an official proceeding; abuse of official power; electoral subversion under Article VI; or any conspiracy to commit any of the foregoing. These offenses directly negate the trust placed in the office and admit no exception based on motivation or context.

  **(b) Category 2 — All other felony convictions: chamber vote required.** A member convicted of any other felony does not lose their seat automatically. The relevant chamber shall vote within 60 days of final conviction on whether to expel the member under Section 5.3. In making this determination the chamber may consider all relevant circumstances, including whether the conduct constituted principled civil disobedience, whether the prosecution appears to have been politically motivated, and whether the conduct bears on the member's fitness to serve. Expulsion under this subsection requires a 2/3 vote.

  **(c) Appeal stay.** For Category 2 offenses, removal does not take effect while a conviction is under direct appeal. A member subject to a Category 2 conviction may continue to serve pending the outcome of their appeal. If the conviction is upheld on final appeal, the chamber vote under Section 5.5(b) shall be held within 60 days of that final determination.

  **(d) Reinstatement.** If a member's conviction is overturned on appeal after they have been removed under this Section, they may stand for election at the next available opportunity but are not automatically reinstated to their seat.

---

## Section 6 — Compensation and Conflicts

**6.1** Members of Congress shall receive compensation set by law. No law increasing congressional compensation shall take effect until after an election of the House of Representatives has intervened.

**6.2** Members of Congress shall file complete financial disclosures as required by Article XV and by the Accountability Commission (Article IX).

**6.3** No member of Congress may hold a financial interest in any matter before their chamber, vote on any legislation from which they stand to derive direct personal financial benefit, or accept gifts, payments, or compensation from any person or entity seeking to influence legislation, beyond the compensation established by law.

---

## Section 7 — Exclusive Powers of the House

The House of Representatives alone shall have the power to:

**(a) Originate revenue and appropriations legislation.** All bills raising revenue or appropriating federal funds must originate in the House. The Regional Assembly may propose amendments to such bills but may not originate them.

**(b) Initiate impeachment.** The House alone may bring articles of impeachment against the President, Vice President, federal judges, members of the Regional Assembly, and other federal officers. A simple majority of the House is required to approve articles of impeachment.

**(c) Elect the President in a contingent election.** If no presidential candidate achieves a majority under ranked choice voting, the House shall elect the President from the top two candidates by vote of the full membership, with each member casting one vote.

---

## Section 8 — Exclusive Powers of the Regional Assembly

The Regional Assembly alone shall have the power to:

**(a) Try impeachments.** When the House has approved articles of impeachment, the Regional Assembly shall conduct the trial. Conviction requires a 2/3 vote. Upon conviction, the officer is removed from office and may be disqualified from future office by a subsequent majority vote. Criminal prosecution may follow separately.

**(b) Confirm appointments.** The Regional Assembly shall confirm or reject presidential nominations for: Supreme Court justices, federal appellate judges, Cabinet members, ambassadors, the heads of independent agencies, and members of the Constitutional Organs (Article IX). Confirmation requires a majority vote. A nomination not acted upon within 90 days of submission is deemed confirmed.

**(c) Ratify treaties.** The Regional Assembly shall ratify treaties negotiated by the President. Ratification requires a 2/3 vote. An executive agreement that would, if enacted as law, require legislation must be submitted as a treaty.

**(d) Confirm declarations of national emergency.** A declaration of national emergency by the President must be approved by the Regional Assembly within 30 days or it lapses, as provided in Article III.

---

## Section 9 — Joint Powers of Congress

Both chambers, acting together, shall have the power to:

**(a) Declare war.** A declaration of war requires a majority vote of both chambers in joint session. No declaration of war may be issued against a nation that has not attacked the United States or a treaty ally, except by a 2/3 vote of both chambers.

**(b) Override a presidential veto.** A bill returned by the President with objections may be enacted over the veto by a 2/3 vote of both chambers.

**(c) Propose constitutional amendments.** As provided in Article VIII.

**(d) Authorize all other federal powers** enumerated in this Constitution, including taxation, borrowing, regulation of commerce, establishment of federal courts below the Supreme Court, and all laws necessary and proper to carry out these powers.

**9.1 — Enumerated Powers.** Congress may exercise only the powers granted to it by this Constitution. The enumeration of specific powers shall not be construed to deny or disparage powers reasonably implied by those expressly granted, but Congress may not use implied powers to circumvent the explicit limitations of this Constitution.

---

## Section 10 — The Legislative Process

**10.1 Introduction.** Any member of either chamber may introduce a bill. Bills must address a single subject, stated clearly in their title.

**10.2 Committee review.** Each chamber shall establish standing committees with jurisdiction over defined subject areas. All bills shall be referred to the relevant committee within five days of introduction. Committees shall hold public hearings on any bill before reporting it to the full chamber. A bill that has not been reported out of committee within 120 days may be discharged to the full chamber by majority vote of the chamber. In addition, if a bill has not received a committee vote within 60 days of referral, any member of the chamber may file a discharge petition; if the petition is signed by a majority of the full membership of the chamber, the bill proceeds to the floor for a scheduled vote within 10 days of the petition being certified by the presiding officer.

**10.3 Floor vote.** A bill reported by committee shall be scheduled for a floor vote within 60 days. Floor debate shall be governed by rules established by each chamber. Debate may not be indefinitely extended to prevent a vote — each chamber's rules shall provide for a mechanism to close debate and proceed to a vote by majority will. No chamber rule may permit debate on any single bill to be extended beyond a cumulative total of 30 hours without an affirmative vote of 2/3 of the full membership of the chamber; a rule purporting to authorize extended debate beyond this limit is void to that extent.

**10.4 Passage.** A bill passes a chamber upon approval by a majority of members present and voting, provided a quorum is present. A quorum consists of a majority of the full membership of the chamber.

**10.5 Bicameral passage.** A bill must pass both chambers in identical form before being presented to the President. If the chambers pass different versions of the same bill, a conference committee of members from both chambers shall attempt to reconcile the differences. A reconciled bill must pass both chambers before proceeding.

**10.6 Presidential action.** The President shall sign or veto a bill within 15 days of receiving it. If the President takes no action within 15 days, the bill becomes law. There is no pocket veto — a bill presented to the President during a congressional recess becomes law after 15 days if unsigned.

**10.7 Single subject rule.** No bill may address more than one subject. A bill that addresses multiple unrelated subjects is void in its entirety. Appropriations bills may fund multiple programs within the jurisdiction of a single subject area.

**10.7A — Pre-enactment single-subject challenge.** Any member of either chamber may file a single-subject challenge with the Supreme Court at any point after a bill has passed both chambers and before the President acts on it. The Supreme Court shall rule on a single-subject challenge within 15 days. If the Court finds a violation, the bill is returned to the originating chamber for severance or redrafting before proceeding to the President. The 15-day presidential action clock under Section 10.6 is tolled during any pending single-subject challenge.

---

## Section 11 — Deadlock Resolution

**11.1** If the Regional Assembly fails to pass a bill that has passed the House, or passes a materially different version, the following process applies:

  (a) The bill returns to the House, which may accept the Regional Assembly's amendments, reject them, or request a conference committee.

  (b) If a conference committee fails to produce an agreed version within 60 days, the original House-passed version is put to a vote of the full Regional Assembly.

  (c) If the Regional Assembly votes against the original House version a second time, the bill does not proceed. It may be reintroduced in the next session.

**11.2 — 90-Day Delay Rule.** On any bill passed by the House that the Regional Assembly has not acted upon within 90 days of receipt, the House may by majority vote declare the bill delayed and request an immediate floor vote in the Regional Assembly. The Regional Assembly must hold a floor vote within 15 days of such a request. The deadlock resolution mechanism of Sections 11.1 and 11.2 may not be invoked more than twice for the same bill within a single Congress. If the mechanism has been invoked twice and the chambers have still not reached agreement, the bill is tabled for the remainder of that Congress and may not be reintroduced until the next Congress convenes.

**11.3 — Regional Assembly Primacy on Appointments and Treaties.** Sections 11.1 and 11.2 do not apply to confirmation of appointments or ratification of treaties, which are the exclusive power of the Regional Assembly and not subject to House override.

## Section 12 — The Budget Process

**12.1** The President shall submit a proposed federal budget to Congress no later than the first Monday of February each year.

**12.2** Congress shall pass appropriations legislation for the coming fiscal year by September 30. If appropriations legislation has not been enacted by October 1, a continuing resolution at the prior year's funding levels automatically takes effect and remains in force until appropriations legislation is enacted. The government may not shut down for lack of appropriations. The automatic continuing resolution is self-executing upon the conditions of this Section being met and requires no further legislative or executive action to take effect; it may not be re-interpreted, modified in application, or altered in its funding ratios by executive order, agency guidance, or any other executive action. Any attempt by the executive branch to alter funding ratios, withhold appropriated funds, or selectively apply funding levels under an automatic continuing resolution is subject to immediate judicial review on an expedited basis, with standing held by any member of Congress and by any person or entity whose funding is affected.

**12.3** The automatic continuing resolution in Section 12.2 shall fund all government operations at 98% of the prior year's enacted levels. It shall not fund new programs, expand existing programs, or carry out capital projects not already underway.

**12.4** Congress may not appropriate funds in excess of projected revenues plus authorized debt, except by a 3/5 vote of both chambers declaring a fiscal emergency. Such a declaration must state the specific reason and expected duration of the deficit spending.

**12.5** An independent Congressional Budget Office shall provide non-partisan analysis of all legislation with significant fiscal impact. No appropriations bill may be voted upon before the CBO has published its fiscal analysis, except in a declared fiscal emergency. For purposes of this Section, a "fiscal emergency" permitting bypass of CBO analysis is limited to: (i) an imminent default on an obligation of the United States government; (ii) a natural disaster requiring an immediate appropriation to protect life and property; or (iii) a declared war requiring immediate defense appropriations. A fiscal emergency under this Section must be certified in writing by the CBO Director before any bill is brought to a vote under the emergency exception; if the CBO Director declines to certify, the emergency exception is unavailable and normal CBO analysis procedures apply.

**12.6 — Abolition of the debt ceiling.** There shall be no separate legislative limit on the total debt of the United States. Congressional authorization of spending constitutes implicit authorization of whatever borrowing is necessary to fund that spending. The practice of requiring a separate vote to authorize borrowing already committed to by prior appropriation is abolished. Fiscal discipline shall be exercised through the appropriations process, not through threats of default on obligations already incurred.

**12.7 — Prohibition on off-budget spending.** All federal expenditures, obligations, and liabilities shall appear in the official federal budget regardless of how they are characterized. No spending may be designated as "emergency," "supplemental," "off-budget," or by any other label that would exclude it from the official budget totals or from the CBO analysis required by Section 12.5. All spending is on-budget spending.

**12.8 — Mandatory public publication.** The full federal budget, as submitted by the President and as enacted by Congress, shall be published in complete, line-item detail within five days of submission or enactment respectively. Publication shall be in a publicly accessible, machine-readable, and searchable format. No portion of the unclassified budget may be withheld from public publication. A summary written in plain language shall be published simultaneously.

**12.9 — Long-term fiscal projections.** The President's annual budget submission shall include ten-year and thirty-year fiscal projections prepared by the CBO. These projections shall model the fiscal impact of current policy trajectories and shall identify any projected unsustainable debt paths. Congress shall not be required to act on these projections but may not suppress or alter them before publication.

**12.10 — Performance reporting.** Every federal program receiving appropriations above a threshold set by Congress shall publish an annual performance report within 90 days of the close of the fiscal year. Performance reports shall state the program's stated objectives, the metrics used to measure progress, the results achieved, and the cost per unit of outcome where measurable. Performance reports shall be published publicly alongside the subsequent year's budget request. Congress shall not appropriate funds to a program that has failed to file a required performance report.

## Section 12A — Classified Spending

**12A.1 — Default of transparency.** Transparency in government spending is the constitutional default. Classified spending is a narrow, justified exception to that default — not a separate parallel system of finance beyond democratic oversight.

**12A.2 — Congressional oversight is absolute.** No classified spending program shall be withheld from the relevant oversight committees of Congress. The executive branch may not create, fund, or operate any program whose existence, budget, or activities are concealed from the congressional committees designated by law to oversee them. A program whose budget cannot be disclosed to Congress may not be funded. Concealment of a classified spending program from authorized congressional oversight committees is grounds for impeachment of the responsible official. The Inspector General of the relevant agency shall report any known concealment, or credible allegation of concealment, to the Accountability Commission within 30 days of becoming aware of it; failure to report is itself a violation of this Section.

**12A.3 — Independent audit.** The Accountability Commission (Article IX) shall maintain a division of auditors holding appropriate security clearances. This division shall conduct annual audits of all classified spending programs and shall certify to Congress and the public that classified funds were spent lawfully, within authorized purposes, and without material waste or fraud. The audit findings shall be published in classified form to the relevant congressional committees and in unclassified summary form to the public.

**12A.4 — Public aggregate disclosure.** The total dollar amount appropriated for classified programs in each fiscal year shall be publicly disclosed, even if individual program line items remain classified. Citizens have the right to know the total scale of classified spending as a share of the federal budget. No mechanism may be used to conceal the aggregate classified total from public knowledge.

**12A.5 — Prohibition on domestic political use.** No classified appropriation may be used to:
  (a) Conduct surveillance, influence operations, or intelligence activities targeting domestic political organizations, candidates, elected officials, journalists, or citizens on the basis of their political views or associations
  (b) Fund activities designed to influence domestic elections or public opinion on domestic political matters
  (c) Circumvent the requirements of any law applicable to unclassified government activities

Any classified program found to be operating in violation of this Section shall be immediately terminated, and its responsible officers shall be referred to the Accountability Commission for prosecution.

**12A.6 — Sunset and reauthorization.** No classified spending program may operate for more than five years without explicit reauthorization by Congress. Reauthorization requires a formal review by the relevant oversight committees and a finding that the program remains necessary, lawful, and operating within its authorized scope. A program that has not been reauthorized within five years of its last authorization lapses automatically.

**12A.7 — Declassification review.** Every classified program shall undergo a mandatory declassification review every five years. The review shall be conducted by the Accountability Commission in consultation with the relevant agency. At the conclusion of each review, the maximum amount of information that can be declassified without genuine harm to national security shall be released publicly. Continued classification must be justified anew at each review — prior classification does not automatically justify continued classification.

**12A.8 — Whistleblower protection.** No person shall be prosecuted, penalized, or retaliated against for disclosing to the Accountability Commission, to a congressional oversight committee, or to a federal court evidence that a classified program is operating illegally, unconstitutionally, or in violation of Section 12A.5. Protection under this Section applies to the disclosure of illegal activity within classified programs, not to the disclosure of lawfully classified operational details. Congress shall establish by law a secure channel through which such disclosures may be made.

## Section 13 — Supermajority Requirements

The following actions require supermajority votes as specified:

| Action | Threshold |
|--------|-----------|
| Override presidential veto | 2/3 of both chambers |
| Impeachment conviction (Regional Assembly) | 2/3 of Regional Assembly |
| Expel a member | 2/3 of the relevant chamber |
| Ratify a treaty | 2/3 of Regional Assembly |
| Declare war against a non-aggressor | 2/3 of both chambers |
| Propose a constitutional amendment | 2/3 of both chambers |
| Authorize deficit spending | 3/5 of both chambers |
| Override an Electoral Commission ruling | Not permitted |
| Remove a Constitutional Organ commissioner | 2/3 of both chambers plus judicial finding of cause |

## Section 14 — Congressional Ethics

**14.1** Each chamber shall maintain an Ethics Committee responsible for investigating alleged violations of ethics rules by members of that chamber.

**14.2** The Accountability Commission (Article IX) shall have independent jurisdiction to investigate federal officials, including members of Congress, for corruption, bribery, and abuse of office. The Accountability Commission's investigations supersede the jurisdiction of the relevant chamber's Ethics Committee in cases involving criminal conduct.

**14.3** Members of Congress shall be subject to the financial disclosure, blind trust, and revolving door provisions of Article VI.

**14.4** No member of Congress shall receive compensation for any service rendered, speech given, or article written in their capacity as a public official, beyond the compensation established by law.

---

## Section 15 — Minimum Session Requirement

**15.1** Congress shall be in session for not fewer than 120 days per calendar year. Days in session are defined as days on which either chamber holds a recorded floor vote or a committee hearing open to the public record. Pro forma sessions in which no business is conducted do not count toward this requirement.

**15.2** Failure to meet the 120-day requirement does not invalidate any legislation passed during the year, but shall be published by the Electoral Commission as a matter of public record and shall constitute a reportable failure in each member's official attendance record.

**15.3** The 120-day floor may be raised but not lowered by statute.

---

## Section 16 — Congressional Oversight

### 16.1 — The Oversight Power

Congress possesses the power to investigate any matter within the scope of its legislative, appropriations, and confirmation authority, including the conduct of the executive branch, the administration of federal law, the expenditure of federal funds, and the activities of any person or entity subject to federal jurisdiction. The oversight power is an inherent and necessary attribute of the legislative function. No act of Congress, executive order, or claim of executive privilege may extinguish it.

Oversight may be conducted through committee hearings, subpoenas, requests for documents, depositions, and any other lawful investigative means. The oversight power extends to past conduct as well as current operations.

### 16.2 — Subpoena Power and Expedited Judicial Enforcement

Each chamber of Congress and each standing committee, acting by majority vote, may issue subpoenas compelling the testimony of witnesses and the production of documents, records, and other materials in connection with a lawful oversight inquiry.

A subpoena issued under this Section is enforceable by federal courts. The following expedited process applies:

  (a) A committee that has issued a subpoena that has not been complied with within 30 days of service may apply to the United States District Court for the District of Columbia for an enforcement order
  (b) The court shall rule on the application within 30 days of filing. No continuance may extend this deadline except by order of the Chief Judge upon a finding of extraordinary circumstances
  (c) A person who fails to comply with a court enforcement order within 15 days is in contempt of court and subject to civil detention and daily fines until compliance
  (d) Civil detention under this Section is enforced by the United States Marshals Service and does not require action by the Department of Justice

The expedited enforcement process of this Section may not be delayed, stayed, or extended by interlocutory appeal except upon a showing to the appellate court that the subpoena is facially unconstitutional. A claim of executive privilege does not automatically stay enforcement — it must be adjudicated under Section 16.6 on the same expedited timeline. Enforcement orders issued under subsection (b) are self-executing upon issuance; the United States Marshals Service shall enforce them without awaiting further direction from the Department of Justice or any other executive agency. A Marshal who refuses or fails to enforce a valid subpoena enforcement order without lawful justification is subject to removal from office. If the Department of Justice refuses to support enforcement, the Accountability Commission (Article IX) is independently empowered to enforce congressional subpoenas through the same process, including by direct application to any United States District Court.

### 16.3 — Affirmative Duty to Testify

The following officers of the executive branch have an affirmative constitutional duty to testify before Congress on matters within their official responsibilities when summoned by either chamber or by a committee with jurisdiction over those responsibilities:

  (a) All Cabinet secretaries and heads of executive departments
  (b) All heads of independent agencies and regulatory bodies
  (c) The Director of National Intelligence, the Director of the CIA, the Director of the FBI, and equivalent senior intelligence and law enforcement officials
  (d) Any other Senate-confirmed officer of the United States

The duty to testify is distinct from the obligation to answer every question — a witness subject to this duty must appear, must answer questions not subject to a valid privilege claim under Section 16.6, and must do so truthfully. Refusal to appear without a valid legal basis, and refusal to answer questions not subject to a valid privilege claim, are each independent violations of this Section. A response of "I do not recall" or equivalent that a court subsequently finds was made in bad faith — that is, where the witness did in fact recall or had the means to recall with reasonable inquiry — constitutes perjury under Section 16.4. Witnesses subject to this duty have an affirmative obligation of reasonable inquiry before claiming lack of recall; a claim of non-recall made without reasonable inquiry is deemed knowing if the information was available to the witness through records, staff, or ordinary professional practice.

A violation of the duty to testify shall be referred by the relevant committee to the Accountability Commission for prosecution. An official who refuses to appear is subject to removal from office in addition to criminal penalties.

### 16.4 — Perjury and False Statements Before Congress

No person appearing before either chamber of Congress, before any committee or subcommittee, or in any deposition conducted pursuant to a congressional subpoena, may knowingly make a false statement of material fact, knowingly deny material information, or knowingly present false documents.

**16.4(a) — Jurisdiction.** Perjury before Congress and knowing false statements in congressional proceedings shall be prosecuted exclusively by the Accountability Commission (Article IX). The Department of Justice has no jurisdiction over these offenses. This allocation exists because the executive branch controls the Department of Justice and has a structural conflict of interest in prosecuting those who lie to Congress about executive branch conduct.

**16.4(b) — Penalties.** A person convicted of perjury or making a knowing false statement before Congress shall be subject to:
  (i) Criminal penalties established by law
  (ii) If a current federal official: automatic removal from office upon conviction, without requiring a separate impeachment or chamber vote
  (iii) Permanent disqualification from holding any federal elected or appointed office
  (iv) No pardon — the President, and no other official with pardon authority, may pardon or commute a sentence for a violation of this Section. A pardon issued in violation of this prohibition is void.

**16.4(c) — No statute of limitations.** There is no statute of limitations for perjury before Congress or for knowing false statements in congressional proceedings. The passage of time does not extinguish the Accountability Commission's authority to prosecute.

**16.4(d) — Scope.** This Section applies regardless of whether the witness was placed under oath, provided that the witness was informed that truthful testimony was required. The formal oath requirement is a procedural safeguard, not a prerequisite for the obligation of truthfulness.

### 16.5 — Contempt of Congress

A person who willfully refuses to comply with a valid congressional subpoena — whether to testify, to produce documents, or to appear — is in contempt of Congress.

**16.5(a) — Referral.** Contempt citations shall be referred by the relevant chamber to the Accountability Commission for criminal prosecution. The Department of Justice has no role in prosecuting contempt of Congress. This allocation applies for the same reason as Section 16.4(a) — the executive branch may not be the enforcer of congressional authority against executive branch witnesses.

**16.5(b) — Penalties.** Criminal contempt of Congress carries criminal penalties established by law and, for current federal officials, constitutes grounds for removal from office. The Accountability Commission shall prosecute within 90 days of referral unless the Commission publishes specific written findings that the evidence is insufficient to support prosecution; a general statement of insufficient resources or prosecutorial discretion without specific findings as to the evidence does not satisfy this requirement. After the Commission has published three written explanations for declining to act on the same matter, the matter is automatically referred to the Supreme Court for the appointment of a special prosecutor, who shall have all the powers of the Accountability Commission with respect to that matter.

**16.5(c) — Civil contempt.** In addition to criminal referral, a chamber may seek civil enforcement of its subpoenas directly in federal court under Section 16.2. Civil and criminal contempt proceedings may proceed simultaneously.

### 16.6 — Executive Privilege: Definition, Scope, and Limits

Executive privilege is a limited constitutional protection for specific categories of executive branch communications. It is not a general power to withhold information from Congress. The following rules govern its scope and exercise:

**16.6(a) — Covered communications.** Executive privilege protects:
  (i) Communications directly between the President and senior advisers regarding the President's own deliberative process on specific pending decisions, where disclosure would chill frank advice on that specific decision
  (ii) Information whose disclosure would compromise an active, ongoing criminal investigation or intelligence operation, upon a specific showing that disclosure would cause concrete harm to the investigation. An "active investigation" claimed as the basis for privilege under this subsection must be registered with the Accountability Commission within 72 hours of the privilege being first invoked on that basis; the registration shall identify the investigation by subject matter and supervising authority without disclosing operational details. Registered investigations are subject to quarterly review by the Accountability Commission to confirm continued active status. An investigation that has not resulted in charges, indictments, or a formal written finding of no prosecutable conduct within two years of initial privilege registration loses its privileged status under this subsection; the privilege may not be re-registered for the same investigation without a showing of extraordinary circumstances approved by the Accountability Commission
  (iii) Genuine military operational secrets whose disclosure would directly endanger active operations or personnel

**16.6(b) — Not covered.** Executive privilege does not protect:
  (i) Evidence of criminal conduct, violations of this Constitution, or abuse of office by any official
  (ii) Information about actions that have already been taken and that Congress is investigating after the fact
  (iii) Communications that were not genuinely confidential at the time they occurred
  (iv) Information about the expenditure of appropriated funds
  (v) The identity of persons making or implementing decisions that Congress has authority to investigate
  (vi) Any communication that a court finds was made in furtherance of a crime or fraud

**16.6(c) — How privilege is invoked.** A claim of executive privilege must:
  (i) Be made in writing by the President personally, not by a subordinate official or agency counsel
  (ii) Identify with specificity each document or communication withheld and the basis for the claim
  (iii) Be filed with both the relevant committee and the Accountability Commission simultaneously

A blanket claim of privilege over a category of documents without specific identification is void.

**16.6(d) — Judicial review.** A privilege claim is subject to judicial review under the expedited process of Section 16.2. A court shall examine the withheld materials in camera and rule within 30 days on whether each specific document falls within the covered categories of Section 16.6(a). A privilege claim covering documents that fall outside those categories shall be denied as to those documents.

**16.6(e) — Congressional override.** A 2/3 vote of the relevant committee may override a privilege claim and compel disclosure of specific materials where the committee finds that the public interest in disclosure substantially outweighs the executive interest in confidentiality. A congressional override is subject to judicial review, but the court shall give substantial deference to the committee's finding of public interest. In addition, the minority of a committee — comprising at least 1/3 of the committee's total membership — may force a floor vote on a privilege override by filing a written motion signed by a majority of all minority-party members serving on the committee; upon such filing, the full chamber shall schedule a floor vote on whether to override the privilege claim, and that vote shall be held within 15 days of the filing being certified by the presiding officer of the chamber. A floor vote on privilege override requires the same 2/3 threshold applicable to committee overrides.

### 16.7 — Adverse Inference for Bad-Faith Privilege Claims

Where a court finds that a claim of executive privilege was asserted without a reasonable basis — that is, where the withheld materials plainly fall outside the categories of Section 16.6(a) or where the privilege was invoked primarily to delay or obstruct a congressional inquiry — the following consequences apply:

  (a) The relevant committee may draw an adverse inference from the withholding: it may treat the withheld materials as supporting the committee's inquiry in the most adverse manner consistent with other available evidence
  (b) The adverse inference is admissible in any subsequent congressional proceeding, report, or resolution
  (c) The official who asserted the bad-faith privilege claim is referred to the Accountability Commission for investigation of obstruction of Congress

The adverse inference provision creates a real cost for overclaiming privilege. An official who withholds documents knowing they are not privileged gains nothing — the committee may treat the withheld documents as confirming whatever the committee suspected, and the official faces obstruction proceedings.

### 16.8 — Protection for Congressional Witnesses

Any federal official or employee who testifies truthfully before Congress — including testimony that is adverse to the interests of their superiors, their agency, or the administration in power — is protected from any form of retaliation. This protection applies from the date the official is subpoenaed or voluntarily agrees to testify.

Protected against retaliation includes:
  (a) Termination, demotion, suspension, or reassignment motivated by the testimony
  (b) Reduction in responsibilities, access, or security clearances motivated by the testimony
  (c) Threats, intimidation, or pressure to alter or withhold testimony
  (d) Any adverse action taken against the official's family members in connection with the testimony

An official who is retaliated against for congressional testimony may seek immediate injunctive relief in any federal court. The Accountability Commission shall investigate and prosecute officials who retaliate against congressional witnesses. A finding of retaliation constitutes grounds for removal of the retaliating official from office.

The protection of this Section applies to truthful testimony. It does not protect false testimony, which remains subject to Section 16.4.

### 16.9 — Grants of Immunity

Either chamber, or a committee by majority vote with approval of the full chamber, may grant use immunity to a witness compelling their testimony before Congress. A grant of use immunity means:

  (a) The witness must answer all questions put to them, notwithstanding any Fifth Amendment claim
  (b) The Accountability Commission may not use the immunized testimony, or any evidence derived directly from it, as evidence in a criminal prosecution of the witness
  (c) The Accountability Commission may independently investigate and prosecute the witness using evidence gathered independently of the immunized testimony

A grant of immunity does not prevent prosecution — it prevents only the use of the immunized testimony itself as evidence. The purpose is to enable Congress to get truthful information while preserving the possibility of independent criminal accountability.

### 16.10 — No Interference with Oversight

No executive order, presidential directive, agency rule, or congressional leadership decision may direct a federal official to withhold truthful information from a congressional oversight inquiry, to refuse to appear before Congress when lawfully summoned, or to provide Congress with information the official knows to be false or misleading.

An order directing any of these acts is void. An official who follows such an order as justification for withholding truthful information is not thereby relieved of liability under this Section — compliance with an unlawful order to obstruct Congress is itself a violation. An official who receives such an order shall report it to the Accountability Commission within 72 hours.
