# Article VII — Federalism and the States

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

---

## Scope

Establishes the division of authority between the federal government and the states. The United States is a federal republic in which the national government and state governments each possess defined sovereign authority. Neither is a mere creation of the other. This Article replaces the prior Constitution's Articles IV and VI and the Tenth Amendment.

---

## Section 1 — Principles of Federalism

**1.1** The powers of the federal government are those granted by this Constitution, expressly or by necessary implication. All other powers are reserved to the states or to the people.

**1.2** State governments are sovereign within their reserved domains. The federal government may not commandeer state legislatures, state executives, or state courts to implement federal programs or enforce federal law against state will, except where this Constitution expressly provides otherwise.

**1.3** The federal government and the states are partners in governance, not adversaries. Both are obligated to act in good faith toward the citizens they serve and toward each other.

**1.4 Rights floor.** The rights guaranteed by Article V are a minimum floor. States may enact laws that expand rights beyond the federal floor. No state may enact laws that reduce rights below the federal floor. Where a state law provides greater protection for a right than Article V requires, the state standard governs within that state.

**1.5 Democratic floor.** Every state shall maintain a republican form of government including free and fair elections, an independent judiciary, and separation of executive and legislative power. The federal government shall guarantee this form of government to every state and shall protect each state against invasion and, upon application of the state legislature or executive, against domestic violence.

**1.6 — Enforcement of the democratic floor.** Where a state persistently and materially violates the democratic floor of Section 1.5, the following enforcement mechanism applies:

  (a) **Finding.** The Supreme Court, on petition by the United States or by the legislature or executive of any other state, may find that a state is in persistent and material violation of Section 1.5. The finding must identify the constitutional violations and explain why ordinary judicial relief has proved insufficient.

  (b) **Congressional remedy.** Upon a finding under Section 1.6(a), Congress shall within 180 days enact a remedial measure reasonably directed to restoring constitutional government in the state. Such measures may include federal administration of federal elections in the state, conditions on federal grants related to the violation, or other lawful means consistent with this Constitution.

  (c) **Mandatory suspension vote after remedial failure.** If Congress fails to enact a remedial measure within the 180-day period of Section 1.6(b), the House and the Regional Assembly shall each hold a recorded vote within the next 30 days on whether to suspend the state's representation until compliance is restored. Members from the affected state may not vote on that question, and the denominator shall be reduced accordingly.

  (d) **Suspension of representation.** If the state remains in material violation one year after a Supreme Court finding under Section 1.6(a), the state's representation in the House and Regional Assembly is suspended automatically until the Supreme Court certifies compliance, unless Congress has already imposed suspension earlier under Section 1.6(c) or has enacted a remedial measure that the Supreme Court finds is producing substantial and timely progress toward compliance. Congress may extend that progress determination once for no more than 90 days by a 2/3 vote of both chambers. Suspension does not affect the constitutional rights of the state's residents, does not expel the state from the Union, and does not create a permanent blocking minority in the amendment process under Article VIII.

  (e) **Restoration.** Representation shall be restored immediately upon Supreme Court certification that the state has returned to compliance with Section 1.5.

**1.7 — Electoral integrity floor for state elections.** Free and fair elections require, at minimum, that the identity of those funding candidates and campaigns be known to voters, that foreign money be excluded, and that the political spending of corporations and similar entities not be treated as equivalent to the political expression of citizens. These requirements are conditions of the democratic floor of Section 1.5. Every state shall maintain the following minimum standards for all elections conducted within its borders, including state and local elections:

  **(a) Disclosure.** All contributions and expenditures made for the purpose of influencing a state or local election shall be publicly disclosed. Disclosure shall identify the original human source of funds through all intermediaries. No entity may be used as a conduit to conceal the identity of the original human donor. A state may set stricter disclosure requirements than this floor but may not set weaker ones.

  **(b) Anonymous contributions.** No anonymous contribution above two hundred dollars may be made to any candidate, political committee, or entity making expenditures to influence a state or local election. States may lower but not raise this threshold.

  **(c) Foreign money.** No foreign national, foreign government, foreign corporation, or entity owned or controlled by foreign interests may contribute to or make expenditures in connection with any state or local election. This prohibition applies regardless of whether funds are routed through a domestic intermediary.

  **(d) Corporate and organizational spending.** Corporations, labor unions, trade associations, and other legal entities are not natural persons and do not possess the same constitutional rights as individual citizens with respect to political spending in state and local elections. States may regulate or prohibit their political expenditures without satisfying strict scrutiny.

  **(e) Government contractors.** No entity holding an active state or local government contract above a threshold set by state law may make political contributions or expenditures in elections for offices that exercise regulatory or contracting authority over that entity. States shall set the specific threshold and coverage by law but may not exempt state contractors from this principle entirely.

  **(f) Cryptocurrency and digital assets.** No contribution or expenditure to influence a state or local election may be made through a cryptocurrency or digital asset unless the full identity chain from the originating wallet to an identified natural person can be verified and disclosed in compliance with the disclosure requirement of Section 1.7(a).

  **(g) Public financing option.** Every state shall make available at least one public financing option for candidates for statewide office. The design, funding level, and matching structure are matters of state law; the obligation to provide an option is a condition of the democratic floor.

  **(h) Enforcement.** A state that persistently and materially fails to maintain the standards of this Section is in violation of the democratic floor of Section 1.5 and subject to the enforcement mechanism of Section 1.6. The Accountability Commission may refer evidence of systematic violations to the Supreme Court.

**1.8 — Federal incentive for full electoral integrity compliance.** A state that voluntarily adopts campaign finance standards equivalent to or stricter than the full framework of Article XII — including outside spending caps, per-actor aggregate limits, and the geographic concentration limit — shall be eligible to participate in the federal public financing matching infrastructure administered by the Electoral Commission. Participation allows state candidates to access federal matching fund infrastructure for qualifying small-dollar contributions in state races, at the state's option and cost. This creates a positive incentive for states to go beyond the floor without requiring them to do so.

## Section 2 — Powers of the Federal Government

Congress shall have the power to:

**(a) Defense and security.** Raise, maintain, and regulate the armed forces; declare war; provide for the common defense; establish rules for the conduct of warfare; enter into alliances and mutual defense treaties.

**(b) Foreign affairs.** Conduct foreign policy; negotiate and ratify treaties; regulate relations with foreign governments and international organizations; define and punish offenses against the law of nations.

**(c) Commerce.** Regulate commerce with foreign nations, among the states, and with Indian nations. This power includes the authority to regulate activities that substantially affect interstate commerce, establish rules for interstate transactions, and prevent states from erecting barriers to interstate trade. It does not include the power to compel individuals to engage in commerce.

**(d) Taxation and spending.** Lay and collect taxes, duties, and imposts; borrow money on the credit of the United States; spend for the general welfare of the United States. Federal spending may attach conditions to grants to states, provided those conditions are clearly stated, directly related to the purpose of the grant, and not so coercive as to leave states no meaningful choice.

**(e) Currency and financial system.** Coin money, regulate its value, and maintain the integrity of the monetary system; charter and regulate banks and financial institutions engaged in interstate activity; establish bankruptcy laws.

**(f) Immigration and naturalization.** Establish uniform rules for immigration and the naturalization of citizens. States may not enact immigration enforcement regimes that conflict with federal immigration law, but may enact laws that extend benefits or protections to immigrants beyond the federal floor.

**(g) Civil rights.** Enforce the rights guaranteed by this Constitution against infringement by state governments or private actors, through appropriate legislation.

**(h) Environment and natural resources.** Regulate pollution, protect navigable waters, manage federal lands, and address environmental harms that cross state boundaries or affect the global commons. States may enact environmental standards stricter than federal standards.

**(i) Public health.** Respond to public health emergencies that cross state lines; regulate the safety of food, drugs, and medical devices in interstate commerce; fund and coordinate national public health systems.

**(j) Infrastructure.** Build and maintain infrastructure of national significance, including interstate highways, rail networks, air traffic systems, and communications infrastructure.

**(k) Science and knowledge.** Fund scientific research; establish standards of weights and measures; grant patents and copyrights for limited terms to promote innovation and the useful arts.

**(l) Federal courts.** Establish courts inferior to the Supreme Court, define their jurisdiction, and fund their operation.

**(m) Federal lands and territories.** Exercise exclusive jurisdiction over federal lands, installations, and territories not admitted as states.

**(n) Postal and communications.** Establish postal services and regulate interstate communications networks.

**(o) Necessary and proper.** Make all laws necessary and proper for carrying into execution the foregoing powers and all other powers vested in the federal government by this Constitution. This power authorizes means reasonably adapted to enumerated ends; it does not create independent powers beyond those enumerated. The Necessary and Proper Clause may not be invoked to regulate activity that is (i) purely intrastate, (ii) not economic in nature, and (iii) has no substantial effect on interstate commerce; all three conditions must be present for this limitation to apply. Where invocation of the Clause would expand an enumerated power beyond its historical scope, courts shall apply heightened scrutiny, requiring the government to demonstrate a close and direct relationship between the means chosen and an enumerated end.

## Section 3 — Powers Reserved to the States

**3.1** Powers not granted to the federal government by this Constitution, and not prohibited to the states, are reserved to the states or to the people.

**3.2** The reserved powers of the states include, without limitation:

  (a) Criminal law — defining and punishing offenses against state law, operating police and corrections systems, subject to the rights guaranteed by Article V
  (b) Education — operating and funding public education above the federal floor established by Article XIII, Section 2.2
  (c) Land use and zoning — regulating the use of land within the state, including housing, development, and environmental conservation beyond federal minimums
  (d) Family law — regulating marriage, divorce, adoption, and child welfare, within the rights guaranteed by Article V
  (e) Professional licensing — establishing standards for occupations practiced within the state, subject to interstate recognition requirements of Section 5
  (f) State taxation — levying taxes on residents and activity within the state, subject to the prohibition on burdening interstate commerce
  (g) Public health and safety — regulating health, safety, and welfare within the state, subject to federal preemption where applicable
  (h) Local government — creating, organizing, and empowering municipal governments, subject to the local government protections of Section 8

**3.3 Laboratories of democracy.** States are encouraged to experiment with innovative policies within their reserved domains. The federal government shall not preempt state policy experimentation except where the experiment creates demonstrable harm to residents of other states or violates the requirements of this Constitution. Where a state law causes demonstrable harm to residents of another state — including environmental contamination, financial fraud, or public health risks — the affected state has standing to sue the offending state in federal court without further jurisdictional prerequisites. Courts adjudicating such suits shall apply a rebuttable presumption that cross-border harms of this nature are preemptable by the affected state's interests and by federal law; the offending state bears the burden of rebutting that presumption.

## Section 4 — Federal Preemption

**4.1 Express preemption.** Where Congress explicitly states that federal law preempts state law on a subject, federal law governs. Preemption clauses shall be construed narrowly — a statement that federal law governs a subject does not preempt state laws that supplement or exceed federal standards unless Congress explicitly says so.

**4.2 Conflict preemption.** Where compliance with both federal and state law is physically impossible, or where state law directly obstructs the achievement of a federal statutory purpose, federal law governs to the extent of the conflict. State law that merely exceeds federal standards does not conflict with federal law.

**4.3 No implied field preemption.** Federal regulation of a subject does not automatically preempt state regulation of the same subject. States may regulate concurrently with the federal government in most domains. Implied field preemption — the doctrine that federal regulation of an area implicitly occupies the entire field — is abolished except in the following domains where uniform national policy is constitutionally essential:
  (a) Foreign affairs and relations with foreign governments
  (b) National security and the conduct of warfare
  (c) Currency and monetary policy
  (d) Immigration status and naturalization

**4.4 The concurrent regulation principle.** In all areas not subject to implied field preemption under Section 4.3, states may regulate more strictly than federal standards but may not regulate less strictly. A state environmental standard more stringent than federal law is valid. A state environmental standard less stringent than federal law is invalid to the extent of the conflict.

**4.5 Anti-circumvention.** Congress may not use conditional grants, regulatory mandates, or other indirect means to effectively commandeer state governments to implement federal programs when direct federal action would be constitutionally impermissible.

## Section 5 — Interstate Relations

**5.1 Full faith and credit.** Each state shall give full faith and credit to the public acts, records, and judicial proceedings of every other state. In particular:

  (a) A marriage or civil union lawfully entered into in any state shall be recognized as valid in every other state, regardless of whether the receiving state would have authorized such a marriage under its own law

  (b) A final judgment of a court of any state shall be recognized and enforced in every other state, subject to the requirement that the rendering court had proper jurisdiction

  (c) A professional license issued by any state, upon meeting minimum federal qualification standards established by Congress, shall be recognized as valid in every other state for the practice of that profession. States may impose additional requirements for practice within the state but may not refuse to recognize the base qualification

**5.2 Extradition.** A person charged with a crime in one state who is found in another state shall be delivered to the charging state upon the demand of the executive authority of that state. The governor of the receiving state shall issue the warrant of extradition without inquiry into the merits of the charges, subject to the following. Extradition requests are subject to judicial review in the sending (receiving) state: a court of competent jurisdiction in the state where the person is found may deny extradition if it finds, by clear and convincing evidence, that the requesting state's charges are pretextual, politically motivated, or facially unconstitutional. A denial under this provision is subject to immediate appeal; the court must issue its ruling within 30 days of the extradition request being contested.

**5.3 Interstate compacts.** Two or more states may enter into compacts with each other on any matter within their reserved powers. Compacts that affect the powers of the federal government or the rights of states not party to the compact require the consent of Congress.

**5.4 Prohibition on interstate trade barriers.** No state shall impose tariffs, taxes, or discriminatory regulations on goods, services, or persons moving in interstate commerce. States may regulate commerce within their borders for legitimate health, safety, and welfare purposes, provided such regulations do not discriminate against out-of-state commerce and do not impose an undue burden on interstate trade.

**5.5 Cross-border reach of state law.** No state may enforce its laws against conduct that occurred entirely within another state, or against a person for actions taken in another state where those actions were lawful. A state's criminal and civil law applies to conduct within its borders; it does not apply to lawful conduct by its residents in other states. For purposes of digital activity, conduct is deemed to occur where the actor is physically located at the time of the conduct, not where the effects of that conduct are felt. A state may not assert jurisdiction over digital conduct that originates outside its borders solely on the basis of the conduct's audience reach within the state or its downstream effects on persons located within the state; additional nexus to the state beyond mere audience or effect is required to support jurisdiction.

## Section 6 — Washington D.C. and the National Capital

**6.1** The seat of the federal government shall be a defined area designated the National Capital District, comprising the United States Capitol building and grounds, the White House and Executive Office complex, the Supreme Court building, and such federal facilities in close proximity as Congress designates. The National Capital District shall be governed exclusively by Congress.

**6.2** All territory of the current District of Columbia not included within the National Capital District under Section 6.1 shall be admitted as a state, to be known as the State of Washington, and shall be represented in the House of Representatives, the Regional Assembly, and all other federal institutions on the same basis as any other state.

**6.3** Residents of the National Capital District who do not reside in the State of Washington shall be treated as residents of the State of Washington for purposes of federal elections.

## Section 7 — Territories and Admission of New States

**7.1 Citizenship.** All persons born in any territory, possession, or commonwealth of the United States are citizens of the United States with full constitutional rights, regardless of whether their place of birth has been admitted as a state.

**7.2 Right to petition for statehood.** Any territory, possession, or commonwealth of the United States with a resident population of at least 100,000 citizens may petition Congress for admission as a state. Congress shall act on any such petition within five years of its submission, either by admitting the territory as a state, by offering a schedule of admission, or by providing written reasons for declining and specifying what conditions, if any, would need to be met for admission.

**7.3 Right to self-determination.** The residents of any territory, possession, or commonwealth may vote in a referendum on their preferred political status, including statehood, independence, free association, or continuation of current status. Congress shall recognize the result of any such referendum and shall take action consistent with it within a reasonable period.

**7.4 Admission of new states.** New states may be admitted by Congress. No new state shall be formed within the jurisdiction of any existing state, or by the junction of two or more states, without the consent of the legislatures of the states concerned as well as Congress.

**7.5 Federal territories.** Congress shall exercise authority over all territories not admitted as states. Residents of territories shall have the right to local self-government and to all rights guaranteed by Article V.

## Section 8 — Local Government

**8.1 Right to local self-government.** Every city, town, or municipality with a resident population of at least 10,000 citizens, and every county with a resident population of at least 50,000 citizens, has the right to local self-government, including the right to elect its own governing body, levy local taxes within state-set limits, and enact ordinances governing local matters. Special districts and single-purpose administrative bodies are not general-purpose governments and do not possess the rights established by this Section; their structure and autonomy are governed by state law.

**8.2 Protection from state override.** A state may not override, preempt, or nullify a local ordinance on a matter of purely local concern — defined as a matter whose effects are substantially confined to the jurisdiction of the local government — unless the state demonstrates a compelling statewide interest that cannot be served by less intrusive means. In any proceeding to enforce this Section, the state must demonstrate by clear and convincing evidence that the local ordinance conflicts with a specific and articulable statewide interest; speculative, generalized, or post-hoc rationalizations do not qualify as sufficient statewide interests. Courts shall apply heightened scrutiny to state preemption of local housing, labor, and environmental regulations, requiring the state to identify the precise conflict between the local ordinance and the statewide interest and to show that preemption is the least restrictive means of resolving that conflict.

**8.3 Matters of statewide concern.** Where a matter has significant effects beyond a single municipality — including land use decisions affecting regional housing availability, environmental regulation, and infrastructure — states may establish minimum standards that localities must meet. Localities may exceed but not fall below those standards.

**8.4 No abolition.** A state may restructure, merge, or reorganize local governments for legitimate governmental purposes. A state may not abolish all forms of local self-government within any populated area, or so thoroughly preempt local authority as to render local government meaningless.

## Section 9 — Federal Supremacy

**9.1** This Constitution and the laws and treaties of the United States made pursuant to it are the supreme law of the land. Judges in every state shall be bound thereby, anything in any state constitution or law to the contrary notwithstanding.

**9.2** Federal supremacy applies only to federal actions taken within the scope of powers granted by this Constitution. An unconstitutional federal act does not override state law by virtue of the Supremacy Clause — federal supremacy flows from constitutional authority, not from the mere fact of federal action.

**9.3** No state or local official is required to enforce federal law. The federal government may enforce its own laws through its own officers and courts. A state's refusal to assist federal enforcement does not nullify federal law within that state but does not expose the state to penalty solely on account of that refusal.
