# Article XIII — Social, Economic, and Affirmative Rights

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

---

## Scope

Establishes social, economic, environmental, and affirmative rights of all persons within the United States. These rights impose positive duties on government — to build and maintain systems, provide access, and protect against conditions that prevent a person from living a minimally dignified life. They complement the civil and political rights in Article V. The general principles of Article V Section 1 — including the rights floor, the prohibition on permanent suspension, absolute rights, and the rule that enumeration does not deny unenumerated rights — apply equally to the rights in this Article.

---

## Section 1 — Environmental Rights

**1.1** Every person has the right to a healthy environment, including clean air, safe drinking water, and freedom from toxic contamination.

**1.2** Every person has the right to a stable climate. The government has a duty to take reasonable measures, consistent with the best available scientific evidence, to prevent dangerous interference with the climate system and to protect present and future generations from the consequences of climate change. When a court finds climate inaction unconstitutional, it shall order Congress to pass legislation meeting a minimum standard defined by the court; if Congress fails to act within 12 months, the court shall impose the least restrictive constitutional remedy available, which may include ordering funding levels, mandating regulatory action under existing authority, or requiring executive agencies to use existing powers; courts may not design policy but may enforce minimum adequacy floors.

**1.3** In all decisions affecting the environment, the government shall give weight to the interests of future generations who cannot participate in current political processes.

**1.4** The rights in this Section create enforceable duties on government. A court may find that government inaction or action has violated these rights and may order Congress or the executive to take remedial action within a defined period. Courts shall not prescribe specific policy solutions but may require that adequate measures be taken. "Enforceable duty" means that courts may (a) declare a specific funding level or program insufficient, (b) order Congress to act within a defined period, (c) appoint a special master to oversee compliance, and (d) hold officials in contempt for non-compliance; courts may not prescribe the specific policy instrument but may prescribe the minimum outcome that any compliant policy must achieve.

## Section 2 — Affirmative Rights

**2.1 Healthcare.** Every person has the right to access basic healthcare and emergency medical treatment. No person shall be denied emergency medical treatment due to inability to pay. Congress shall establish and maintain a system that ensures no person is denied necessary medical care due to financial circumstances.

**2.2 Education.** Every person has the right to free public education through the completion of secondary school. Public education shall be of sufficient quality to enable participation in civic and economic life. No state may provide substantially inferior education to any group of students on the basis of their race, income, disability, or geographic location.

**2.3 Housing security.** Every person has the right to protection against arbitrary homelessness. The government shall maintain a safety net ensuring that no person is left without access to basic shelter due to circumstances beyond their control. This right does not guarantee any specific housing but imposes a duty on government to prevent destitution.

**2.4 Enforcement of affirmative rights.** The rights in this Section impose duties on government that are judicially enforceable. A court finding a violation of an affirmative right may:
  (a) Declare that a duty has not been met
  (b) Order Congress to enact legislation remedying the violation within a defined period of not less than one year
  (c) Order the executive to take available administrative action pending legislative remedy

Courts may not prescribe the specific policy design of legislative remedies. The choice of how to fulfill an affirmative duty belongs to Congress. Courts may, however:
  (i) Declare that a specific legislative approach is constitutionally insufficient if it demonstrably fails to address the violation
  (ii) Order that a minimum funding level be maintained pending a legislative remedy, where the violation is caused by a complete absence of funding
  (iii) Appoint a special master to monitor compliance with a remedial order and report to the court
  (iv) Hold the executive branch in contempt for failing to implement available administrative measures ordered under Section 2.4(c)

Courts may not: specify the design of any program, override legislative policy choices among constitutionally sufficient alternatives, or order tax increases or new appropriations as primary remedies. "Constitutionally sufficient alternative" means an alternative that a court has certified will achieve the minimum outcome required by this Article; a court may not simply accept a legislative claim of sufficiency — it must independently verify that the chosen alternative meets the constitutional floor before deferring to legislative choice.

## Section 3 — Economic Rights

**3.1 Right to work.** Every person has the right to work and to pursue a lawful occupation free from arbitrary government restriction. Licensing requirements for occupations must be rationally related to the protection of public health, safety, or welfare.

**3.2 Right to organize.** Every worker has the right to form, join, and be represented by a labor union or other collective bargaining organization, and to engage in collective bargaining. No law shall prohibit collective bargaining or penalize workers for organizing.

**3.3 Economic security.** Congress shall maintain programs ensuring a basic standard of living for persons unable to support themselves due to age, disability, illness, or economic hardship. Such programs may not be eliminated without replacement by programs providing equivalent or greater protection.

**3.4 Fair compensation.** Every worker has the right to a living wage — compensation sufficient to meet basic needs including food, housing, healthcare, and transportation in the region where they work. Congress shall establish a minimum wage floor meeting this standard, set the specific amount by law, and review and adjust it not less than every five years to reflect changes in the cost of living. States may set higher minimums. A minimum wage that has not been reviewed within five years of its last adjustment shall be automatically increased by the cumulative change in the Consumer Price Index for that period, until Congress acts. The automatic CPI adjustment is self-executing and takes effect on January 1 of each year; Congress may not freeze, delay, or reduce the automatic adjustment by statute; Congress may only act to set the minimum wage above the adjusted floor, not below it; any statute purporting to override the automatic adjustment is void.

## Section 4 — Rights of Children

**4.1 — Children as rights holders.** Children are rights holders under this Constitution. The rights enumerated in this Article and in Article V apply to children as well as adults. Where rights require modification in their application to children by reason of age and developing capacity, such modification shall be made in the child's best interests, not for the convenience of the state or other adults.

**4.2 — Right to education.** Every child has the right to free public education of sufficient quality to develop their intellectual and civic capacities. No child may be denied education on the basis of their race, national origin, disability, immigration status, gender, or the circumstances of their parents.

**4.3 — Protection from abuse, neglect, and exploitation.** Every child has the right to protection from physical abuse, sexual abuse, emotional abuse, neglect, and economic exploitation. The state has an affirmative duty to protect children from these harms, including harms inflicted by parents, guardians, and institutions.

**4.4 — Protection from child labor.** No child under sixteen years of age may be employed in work that interferes with their education, endangers their health, or is hazardous in nature. Congress shall define and enforce standards protecting children from exploitative labor. Light, non-hazardous work by older children that does not interfere with education is not prohibited.

**4.5 — Right to be heard.** In any legal proceeding that directly affects a child's welfare — including custody, adoption, guardianship, and child protective proceedings — the child's views shall be given weight appropriate to their age and maturity. A child shall have the right to legal representation in any proceeding in which their custody or placement is at issue. In proceedings affecting a child's welfare, the court shall appoint an independent guardian ad litem to represent the child's interests; the guardian's recommendation on the weight to give the child's expressed views is entitled to strong deference; the standard for disregarding a child's expressed preference is clear and convincing evidence that following it would harm the child.

**4.6 — Best interests standard.** In all actions by government affecting children, the best interests of the child shall be a primary consideration. This standard applies to legislative, executive, and judicial action.

**4.7 — Children of incarcerated parents.** The incarceration of a parent does not, by itself, constitute grounds for the permanent termination of parental rights. The state shall make reasonable efforts to maintain family bonds where doing so is in the child's best interests.
