# Article XV — Government Ethics

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

---

## Scope

Establishes the ethics rules that govern how federal officials behave in office. These provisions address corruption, financial conflicts of interest, civil service merit protection, lobbying, and the integrity of financial markets. Enforcement is primarily through the Accountability Commission (Article IX). Democratic integrity provisions — anti-subversion, foreign interference in elections, and media pluralism — are in Article VI.

---

## Section 1 — Corruption, Conflicts of Interest, and Self-Dealing

### 1.1 — Financial disclosure

All federal officials — elected, appointed, and senior civil servants — shall file complete annual financial disclosures covering all assets, liabilities, income sources, and financial interests. Financial disclosures must be filed within 48 hours of taking office and within 48 hours of any material change; Congress may set shorter deadlines. Disclosures shall be published publicly within 48 hours of filing. Members of Congress are subject to the same 48-hour publication requirement. Failure to file, or filing a materially false disclosure, is grounds for removal from office and criminal prosecution.

### 1.2 — Blind trusts

Any federal official whose financial holdings create a conflict of interest with the duties of their office shall place those holdings in a blind trust managed by an independent trustee. The official shall have no communication with the trustee regarding investment decisions during their service.

### 1.3 — Emoluments

No federal official shall accept any gift, payment, benefit, or thing of value from a foreign government or its agents, or from any domestic entity with a direct financial interest in the official's decisions, beyond reasonable compensation for actual services rendered in a non-official capacity and disclosed as required by Section 1.1. Accepting a prohibited emolument is grounds for removal and criminal prosecution.

### 1.4 — Bribery and self-dealing

No federal official shall take any official action — vote, decision, appointment, contract, regulation, or enforcement — in exchange for anything of value given to themselves, their family, or any entity in which they have a financial interest. Self-dealing shall be defined broadly to include: awarding contracts to entities in which the official holds interests; making decisions that benefit relatives who have not been confirmed to their positions through normal processes; and using official information for personal financial gain.

### 1.5 — Revolving door

No former federal official may, for a period of five years following the conclusion of their service, work for, consult for, lobby on behalf of, or accept compensation from any private entity that was directly regulated or substantially affected by decisions they made or policies they supervised during their service. Congress may extend but not reduce this period by statute.

### 1.6 — Nepotism

No federal official may appoint, hire, promote, or advocate for the appointment of a family member to any position within the same agency, department, or branch of government in which the official serves.

### 1.7 — Automatic removal

A federal official convicted of bribery, self-dealing, or an emoluments violation shall be automatically removed from office upon conviction. Removal does not require a separate impeachment proceeding for officials ordinarily subject to impeachment, though impeachment may proceed concurrently.

### 1.8 — Permitted investments: the index fund rule

No federal official, and no spouse, domestic partner, or dependent child of a federal official, may hold, purchase, or sell any of the following while the official is in office:

  (a) Individual stocks or equity securities of any publicly or privately traded company
  (b) Options, futures, warrants, or derivatives on any individual security or commodity
  (c) Sector-specific or industry-specific exchange-traded funds or mutual funds
  (d) Individual corporate bonds or debt instruments
  (e) Investments in private companies that conduct business with, or are subject to regulation by, the federal government
  (f) Cryptocurrency or digital assets other than through a broadly diversified fund as defined in Section 1.9

The purpose of this Section is to ensure that federal officials have no personal financial incentive to favor any company, sector, or industry over another in the exercise of their official duties.

### 1.9 — Definition of permitted investments

Federal officials and their covered family members may hold:

  (a) **Broadly diversified index funds** — funds that track a market index comprising at least one hundred securities, in which no single holding constitutes more than five percent of the fund's total value, and which are not limited to a single economic sector or industry. This includes total market funds, S&P 500 index funds, international equity index funds, and comparable broad-market instruments.

  (b) **Government bonds** — bonds issued by the United States government, any state, or any municipality, whether held directly or through a broadly diversified bond fund.

  (c) **The official's primary residence** — one property used as the primary personal residence. Investment properties and real estate held for rental income must be disclosed under Section 1.1 and trigger mandatory recusal from legislation that specifically and directly affects real estate markets.

  (d) **Pre-existing business interests** — a business owned or operated by the official prior to taking office, provided it is fully disclosed under Section 1.1, the official recuses from all matters directly affecting that business, and no significant new investment in or expansion of the business occurs during the official's term.

  (e) **Retirement accounts** — accounts holding only the investments permitted by this Section.

  (f) **Illiquid assets under Commission-assigned management** — assets that cannot practicably be converted to permitted investments, including private business interests that predate the official's service, investment real estate, complex inherited assets, and illiquid partnership interests, may be retained only under the conditions of Section 1.9A. Liquid securities — stocks, bonds, options, ETFs, and publicly traded instruments of any kind — are never illiquid for purposes of this exception and must be divested regardless. Commission-assigned management applies only to assets that are genuinely illiquid, defined as assets that cannot be sold on a public market within 90 days; the Accountability Commission must certify illiquidity before approving the exception; the official may not communicate with the assigned manager directly or indirectly; annual audits of the managed assets are public; the exception does not exempt the official from recusal on matters affecting the managed assets.

### 1.9A — Commission-assigned management for illiquid assets

Where an official holds assets that qualify as illiquid under Section 1.9(f) and cannot practicably be divested within the required period, those assets shall be managed under the following conditions:

  **(i) No manager of the official's choosing.** The official may not select, retain, or have any prior or ongoing relationship with the manager of their illiquid assets. The Accountability Commission shall maintain a rotating panel of qualified independent asset managers. Upon taking office, the official shall be assigned a manager from this panel by the Commission. The official has no role in this selection.

  **(ii) Strict separation.** Once assigned, the official shall have no communication with the manager regarding investment decisions, asset valuations, disposition strategies, or any matter relating to the management of the assets. All communication between the official and the manager shall be conducted in writing through the Accountability Commission, which shall log and publish all such communications. Communications between an official and their Commission-assigned manager are logged by the Commission and reviewed for compliance; any attempt to communicate outside this channel — whether through family members, staff, or other intermediaries — constitutes a violation of this Article; the Commission shall promulgate rules identifying prohibited indirect communication channels.

  **(iii) Post-service restriction on manager.** The assigned manager may not accept employment, compensation, investment, or any business relationship from the official or their immediate family for a period of five years following the conclusion of the official's service.

  **(iv) Annual audit.** The Accountability Commission shall conduct an annual audit of all assets held under Commission-assigned management, verifying that the manager has acted independently and that no prohibited communications occurred. Audit findings shall be published publicly.

  **(v) Recusal remains mandatory.** Commission-assigned management does not eliminate the official's duty to recuse. An official whose illiquid assets include a business in a particular industry must still recuse from all legislative, regulatory, and oversight matters that specifically and directly affect that industry, regardless of who manages those assets.

  **(vi) Conversion encouraged.** Where illiquid assets become liquid — through a company going public, a sale, a distribution, or any other event — the proceeds must be converted to permitted investments within ninety days. The tax deferral provisions of Section 1.10 apply to such conversions.

  **(vii) This exception is narrow.** The Commission-assigned management option exists solely for assets that genuinely cannot be divested or converted without extraordinary financial hardship. It is not a general alternative to the index fund rule. Any official who uses this exception for assets that could practicably be converted to permitted investments violates this Section.

### 1.10 — Divestiture upon taking office

A federal official who holds prohibited investments at the time of taking office shall divest those holdings within six months. An official who acquires a prohibited investment through inheritance, gift, or corporate action shall divest within ninety days of acquisition.

To remove the financial barrier to divestiture: the gain realized on the forced sale of a prohibited investment into a permitted investment shall not be recognized as taxable income at the time of sale. Tax on that gain is deferred until the permitted investment is ultimately sold. This deferral applies only to divestitures required by this Section and does not apply to voluntary sales.

### 1.11 — Real-time disclosure of all trades

All purchases, sales, and transfers of any investment by a federal official or their covered family members shall be disclosed to the Accountability Commission within 24 hours of execution. Disclosures shall be published immediately in a publicly searchable database, updated in real-time upon filing. The 24-hour deadline may not be extended by Congress. Trades filed late are flagged publicly. The Accountability Commission shall audit for front-running and may refer violations to the SEC. SEC investigation of an official's trading triggers automatic recusal from all matters affecting the relevant securities. Failure to disclose within 24 hours is a violation subject to the penalties of Section 1.12 regardless of whether the trade itself was permitted.

### 1.12 — Penalties for investment violations

A federal official or covered family member who holds a prohibited investment or executes a prohibited trade shall be subject to:

  (a) **Disgorgement** — full surrender of all profits realized from the prohibited trade or holding, paid into the federal treasury

  (b) **Civil fine** — a fine of not less than three times the profit realized or the loss avoided through the prohibited trade, assessed by the Accountability Commission

  (c) **Criminal prosecution** — willful violation of this Section, or willful failure to disclose within 48 hours, is a federal criminal offense prosecutable by the Accountability Commission. No statute of limitations applies to willful violations.

  (d) **Removal from office** — a pattern of violations, defined as two or more violations within a single term, constitutes grounds for removal. The Accountability Commission shall refer such cases to Congress for impeachment proceedings or to the relevant chamber for expulsion.

  (e) **No profit from public service** — in no circumstance shall a federal official or covered family member retain financial profit derived from information obtained through the official's public position. Courts shall order disgorgement even where the trade was technically permitted if the profit is traced to non-public official information. Profit is presumed to be traced to non-public information if (a) the trade occurred within 30 days of a material non-public decision by the official's agency or committee, (b) the trade was profitable, and (c) no credible alternative explanation is provided; the burden shifts to the official to rebut the presumption by clear and convincing evidence.

### 1.13 — Scope of application

The investment rules of Sections 1.8 through 1.12 apply to:

  - All members of Congress
  - The President, Vice President, and all Cabinet members
  - All federal judges at every level
  - All senior executive branch officials confirmed by the Regional Assembly
  - Senior congressional staff, defined as chiefs of staff, staff directors of committees, and legislative directors, who have access to non-public legislative information
  - Spouses, domestic partners, and dependent children of all of the above while the official is serving

The rules apply from the date the official takes office or, for staff, from the date of appointment to a covered position. They cease to apply one year after the official leaves office, except that the disgorgement remedy remains available permanently for trades made during service.

### 1.14 — Prohibition on use of government resources for campaign purposes

No federal official and no federal employee may use government staff, facilities, equipment, communications systems, or funds paid for by taxpayers for any activity whose primary purpose is to support or oppose a candidate, political party, or ballot measure. This prohibition applies to:

  (a) Directing government employees to perform campaign work during official hours
  (b) Using government offices, vehicles, aircraft, or equipment for campaign events or travel
  (c) Using official communications channels — email, social media accounts, letterhead, or mailing lists — to distribute campaign materials
  (d) Pressuring subordinate employees to donate to, volunteer for, or support any candidate or party

Incidental, unavoidable overlap between official duties and public political activity — such as a President giving a speech that is also newsworthy — does not violate this Section. The test is whether the primary purpose of the activity is governmental or electoral.

Violations shall be investigated and prosecuted by the Accountability Commission. A pattern of violations constitutes grounds for removal from office.

## Section 2 — Financial Market Integrity

### 2.1 — Purpose

Financial markets are infrastructure of national importance. Fraud, manipulation, and insider trading in financial markets harm ordinary investors, undermine public trust, concentrate wealth illegitimately, and — at sufficient scale — threaten economic stability. The federal financial market regulator shall have the independence, funding, and enforcement powers necessary to deter and punish violations effectively. Toothless penalties and political interference in enforcement are incompatible with market integrity.

### 2.2 — Independence of the financial market regulator

The primary federal financial market regulator established by Congress shall be structured as an independent agency whose commissioners serve fixed terms and may be removed only for cause. "Cause" means criminal conviction, a finding of serious ethics violation by the Accountability Commission, or incapacity certified by a panel of three physicians; "good cause" does not include disagreement with regulatory decisions, enforcement priorities, or legal interpretations made in good faith. Commissioners may not be removed for their enforcement decisions, regulatory rulings, or investigation choices. The President may not direct, delay, or terminate any specific enforcement action or investigation.

### 2.3 — Independent funding

The financial market regulator shall be funded through fees assessed on regulated entities and market transactions, not through annual congressional appropriations. Its budget shall be set by its own governing body, subject to a maximum established by law. No congressional action may reduce its funding below the prior year's level except by a 2/3 vote of both chambers. This provision prevents the use of budget pressure as a tool to weaken enforcement against politically connected financial interests.

### 2.4 — Penalty floors

Civil penalties for securities fraud, insider trading, and market manipulation shall be set at levels that actually deter. Congress shall establish civil penalty maximums of not less than:

  (a) Three times the profit gained or loss avoided through the violation, plus full disgorgement of all profits
  (b) No absolute dollar cap that renders penalties inconsequential relative to the scale of the violation or the financial resources of the defendant

A fine that costs less than the profit of the fraud is not a penalty — it is a licensing fee for fraud. Congress may not set penalties so low as to make violation financially rational. In cases of large-scale fraud, the minimum penalty is the greater of 3× profits or $50 million; no settlement below this floor is valid. In cases involving systemic fraud affecting more than 1,000 victims, criminal prosecution is mandatory and may not be settled.

### 2.5 — No settlement without admission for major fraud

In any enforcement action for securities fraud, market manipulation, or insider trading where the alleged profits or investor losses exceed a threshold set by law, settlement on a "neither admit nor deny" basis is prohibited. A defendant must either:

  (a) Admit the conduct and accept the consequences, or
  (b) Proceed to a full adjudication before a court or administrative tribunal

The practice of large financial institutions paying fines while denying wrongdoing deprives markets of deterrence, deprives victims of acknowledgment, and prevents the public record from reflecting what actually occurred. It is prohibited in significant cases.

### 2.6 — Criminal referral and prosecution

When the financial market regulator's enforcement division determines that criminal conduct has occurred — including willful securities fraud, willful insider trading, and market manipulation — it shall refer the matter to the Accountability Commission (Article IX) for criminal prosecution within 30 days of that determination.

The Accountability Commission shall act on such referrals within 180 days of receipt or publish a written public explanation of why criminal prosecution is not being pursued. After one written explanation for delay, subsequent failures to act trigger automatic appointment of a special prosecutor by the D.C. Circuit; the Commission's explanation does not bind the special prosecutor; the special prosecutor has full Commission authority for the referred matter. The decision not to prosecute a financial crime referral may not be made for political reasons and is subject to review by the Judicial Conduct Board of the Accountability Commission.

No financial institution shall be deemed "too large to prosecute." The scale of an institution's operations is not a lawful basis for declining criminal prosecution of fraud committed by that institution or its officers.

### 2.7 — Emergency asset freeze

The financial market regulator shall have authority to seek an emergency judicial order freezing the assets of a person or entity upon a showing of probable cause that fraud, market manipulation, or insider trading is occurring or is about to occur. A court shall rule on such an application within 72 hours. The freeze shall preserve assets pending full adjudication and shall prevent the dissipation of funds that would otherwise be subject to disgorgement.

### 2.8 — Executive clawback

Congress shall provide the financial market regulator with authority to claw back executive compensation — including bonuses, equity awards, and deferred compensation — paid to officers and directors of financial institutions where subsequent investigation reveals that:

  (a) The compensation was calculated based on fraudulent financial reporting, or
  (b) The officer or director was directly responsible for or knowingly participated in the fraud or violation that gave rise to enforcement action

The clawback period shall extend not less than seven years from the date of payment. Clawback authority applies regardless of whether the officer has left the institution.

### 2.9 — Whistleblower rewards

Congress shall maintain a whistleblower reward program for financial market violations. The reward for a whistleblower whose information leads to a successful enforcement action shall be not less than 10% and not more than 30% of the sanctions collected. Whistleblowers shall be protected from retaliation under Section 3.4 of this Article. Retaliation against a financial market whistleblower by any person — including private employers — is a federal offense prosecutable by the Accountability Commission.

### 2.10 — Revolving Door: Comprehensive Restrictions and Protections

**2.10.1 — Purpose.** The revolving door between the financial market regulator and the financial industry corrupts enforcement in two directions: industry insiders placed in regulatory positions protect their former employers; regulators who expect to join the industry pull their punches to preserve future employment. Both patterns must be eliminated.

**2.10.2 — Post-service cooling-off periods.** Cooling-off period requirements apply to all personnel with access to confidential supervisory information, enforcement plans, or non-public regulatory data, regardless of seniority title; "senior personnel" means any person with such access, not merely persons with formal senior titles. All such personnel of the financial market regulator may not, following their departure from service, work for, advise, consult for, represent before the regulator, or accept compensation from any financial institution or entity subject to the regulator's jurisdiction. Cooling-off periods shall be:

  (a) Not less than one year for junior and mid-level personnel
  (b) Not less than three years for division directors, senior attorneys, senior economists, examiners with supervisory authority, and equivalent senior positions
  (c) Not less than five years for commissioners and the head of the agency

Congress shall establish the full schedule of covered positions and periods by law. Congress may extend but not reduce these minimum periods.

**2.10.3 — Scope of post-service restrictions.** During any applicable cooling-off period, a former official may not:

  (a) Accept employment, compensation, or equity in any regulated financial institution or entity subject to the regulator's jurisdiction
  (b) Advise, consult for, or provide strategic guidance to any such entity, whether compensated or not
  (c) Appear before, communicate with, or advocate to the regulator on behalf of any private party
  (d) Provide to any private party non-public information obtained during service

A former official may, without restriction, work in academia, publish, teach, work in industries not subject to the regulator's jurisdiction, or provide services to other government agencies.

**2.10.4 — Transition stipend.** The cooling-off period shall not impose financial hardship on officials who have served the public. Every former official subject to a cooling-off period of more than one year shall receive a transition stipend during that period. The transition stipend shall be funded through the regulator's fee revenues. Congress may not eliminate the transition stipend or reduce it below forty percent of the official's final salary at any vesting tier above two years of service.

**2.10.5 — Vesting of transition stipend.** The transition stipend vests with length of service:

  (a) Service of less than two years: no stipend; a one-year cooling-off period still applies
  (b) Service of two or more years: stipend at not less than forty percent of final salary during the applicable cooling-off period

Congress shall establish by law a graduated vesting schedule under which the stipend rate increases with years of service, up to a maximum set by law. That maximum may not be less than sixty percent of final salary for officials with ten or more years of service. Congress may increase but not reduce these floors.

Commissioners and the agency head shall receive the full stipend regardless of tenure. The cooling-off period for these positions reflects the influence of the role, not the duration of service alone.

**2.10.6 — Compensation during service.** Below-market government pay is the structural cause of the revolving door. Congress shall set compensation for senior regulatory personnel at levels sufficient to attract and retain qualified experts in competition with the private sector. The financial market regulator shall publish annually a comparison of its compensation levels against comparable private sector roles. Where the comparison shows a gap exceeding thirty percent for senior positions, the President shall submit a compensation adjustment proposal in the following budget cycle, and Congress shall act on it.

**2.10.7 — Pre-service restrictions: industry hires.** A person appointed to a senior regulatory position from a regulated financial institution shall:

  (a) Be recused from all matters directly involving their former employer for not less than two years from the date of their departure from that employer
  (b) Disclose at the time of appointment all financial interests, equity compensation, deferred compensation, unvested awards, and contractual obligations from their former employer
  (c) Not retain unvested equity, deferred compensation, or contingent payments from a regulated entity that would create a continuing financial incentive to favor that entity's interests while in regulatory service. Arrangements that condition future payment on regulatory outcomes are void as against public policy.

**2.10.8 — No golden parachute conditioning.** No regulated financial institution may offer or provide to a departing employee, in connection with that employee entering regulatory service, any payment or financial benefit that is contingent upon the employee's future regulatory decisions, that rewards favorable treatment of the institution, or that would create a financial incentive to protect the institution's interests while in office. Any such arrangement is void. Its existence is grounds for disciplinary action against the employee and civil penalties against the institution under Section 2.4.

**2.10.9 — Enforcement.** Violations of this Section by former officials shall be prosecuted by the Accountability Commission. Violations by regulated institutions — including hiring officials in violation of this Section or structuring golden parachute arrangements — are subject to the civil penalty regime of Section 2.4. The Accountability Commission shall maintain and publish a public registry of all departing senior officials, their cooling-off status, and their disclosed post-service employment for the duration of their cooling-off period.

### 2.11 — Jurisdiction over new financial instruments

The financial market regulator's jurisdiction extends to all instruments that function as investments, securities, or derivatives, regardless of their technological form, the label applied by their issuers, or the novelty of the underlying asset. No financial instrument escapes regulatory oversight solely because it is new, digital, or structured to avoid existing definitional categories. Congress shall define the outer boundaries of this jurisdiction by law, but may not exempt a class of investment instruments from oversight entirely.

### 2.12 — Market surveillance authority

The financial market regulator shall maintain a real-time market surveillance system capable of detecting patterns of trading that indicate fraud, manipulation, or insider trading. Congress shall fund this system adequately. Data collected through market surveillance shall be used solely for regulatory and enforcement purposes and shall not be shared with any foreign government, private entity, or government agency outside its regulatory mandate without a judicial order.

## Section 3 — The Civil Service

### 3.1 — Merit principle

Federal employment in the executive branch shall be based on merit — qualifications, demonstrated competence, and performance. No person shall be hired, promoted, assigned, transferred, or removed from federal civil service employment on the basis of their political affiliation, political activity, or the political preferences of their appointing official, except for positions lawfully designated as policy-making appointments.

### 3.2 — Protected positions

The vast majority of federal employees occupy career civil service positions. These positions are protected from politically motivated removal. A career civil servant may be removed only for:
  (a) Demonstrated unsatisfactory performance following notice and an opportunity to improve
  (b) Misconduct
  (c) Elimination of the position for legitimate operational reasons, not as a pretext for political removal

### 3.3 — Policy appointments

A defined number of senior positions in each agency may be designated as political appointments subject to the President's direction, reflecting the legitimate need for the executive to have senior officials aligned with its policy direction. Congress shall set by law the maximum number of such positions per agency, subject to a constitutional cap of 1% of total agency positions; Congress may lower the cap but not raise it above 1%. Political appointees serve at the pleasure of the appointing official. No reclassification of career positions to political appointment status may exceed the statutory limits; reclassification of career positions as political appointments above the 1% cap is void ab initio and self-executing.

### 3.4 — Whistleblower protections

Federal employees who report to the Accountability Commission, to Congress, or to a supervisor evidence of: illegal conduct; waste, fraud, or abuse; violations of this Constitution; or dangers to public health or safety — are protected from retaliation. Retaliation against a federal whistleblower by any official is a criminal offense prosecutable by the Accountability Commission.

### 3.5 — Enforcement

The Accountability Commission (Article IX) shall receive and investigate complaints of civil service violations, including politically motivated removals and retaliation against whistleblowers.

## Section 4 — Lobbying and Access

### 4.1 — Mandatory registration

All persons who communicate with federal officials for the purpose of influencing legislation, regulation, or government decisions on behalf of another party for compensation shall register as lobbyists with the Accountability Commission. Registration shall include disclosure of clients, subject matters, and compensation received.

### 4.2 — Disclosure of contacts

Lobbyists shall file quarterly reports disclosing all contacts with federal officials, the subject matter of each contact, and the positions advocated. Federal officials shall maintain and publish logs of all substantive contacts with registered lobbyists.

### 4.3 — Gifts and hospitality

No lobbyist may provide and no federal official may accept gifts, meals, travel, entertainment, or anything of value beyond a nominal threshold established by law. This prohibition applies regardless of whether the gift is connected to a specific official action.

### 4.4 — Revolving door — lobbyists to officials

No registered lobbyist may assume a federal position with direct regulatory authority over matters on which they lobbied within the preceding three years, except upon a specific written waiver by the Accountability Commission finding no conflict of interest.

### 4.5 — Enforcement

The Accountability Commission shall maintain the lobbyist registry, enforce disclosure requirements, and investigate violations. Violations are subject to civil penalties, criminal prosecution for willful violations, and deregistration.

## Section 5 — Enforcement

**5.1** The provisions of this Article are enforced by the Accountability Commission (Article IX). The Commission's authority over the conduct covered by this Article — corruption, financial conflicts, civil service violations, and lobbying violations — cannot be overridden, transferred, or terminated by the President, Congress, or any other official.

**5.2** The financial market regulator established under Section 2 of this Article operates as an independent enforcement body for civil securities violations. Criminal referrals from the financial market regulator are acted upon by the Accountability Commission under Section 2.6. The two bodies shall coordinate and may not duplicate proceedings against the same conduct.

**5.3** Any federal official — including the President, any member of Congress, or any federal judge — is subject to investigation and prosecution under this Article. No office confers immunity from these provisions.

**5.4** The Accountability Commission's authority to investigate and prosecute violations of this Article is part of the unamendable core of Article VIII. It cannot be reduced, constrained, or eliminated by constitutional amendment. If the Accountability Commission becomes systemically captured or fails to perform its core functions for more than 2 consecutive years, a majority of the Supreme Court may declare it in institutional failure; upon such declaration, a reconstitution panel appointed by the Chief Justices of the 50 states shall appoint new Commissioners; existing Commissioners are removed upon reconstitution; this provision is the sole mechanism for institutional correction and does not require constitutional amendment.
