# Article XIV — Foreign Policy and National Security

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

---

## Scope

Establishes the constitutional framework for economic security, foreign influence defense, critical infrastructure protection, foreign intelligence collection, gray zone response authority, alliance obligations, and technology security. These provisions address the full spectrum of modern threats that fall below or alongside conventional military conflict — including economic coercion, information warfare, cyber attacks on civilian systems, and supply chain vulnerabilities. Complementary to Article XI (War Powers), which governs the use of military force. The war-making power and military response authorities in Article XI remain the exclusive framework for conventional military operations.

---

## Section 1 — Economic Security and Countermeasures

**1.1 — Presidential authority.** The President may, in response to a foreign nation's or actor's actions that constitute a threat to the national security or economic security of the United States, impose the following economic countermeasures without prior congressional authorization:
  (a) Targeted sanctions against named foreign persons, entities, and government officials
  (b) Asset freezes against foreign government-controlled entities operating in the United States
  (c) Restrictions on specific categories of exports to identified foreign adversaries
  (d) Emergency restrictions on foreign investment in critical sectors

**1.2 — Congressional review.** Economic countermeasures imposed under Section 1.1 shall be reported to Congress within 48 hours of imposition. Congress may by majority vote of both chambers terminate any countermeasure within 60 days of imposition. Countermeasures not terminated by Congress within 60 days remain in effect until the President lifts them or Congress acts. After Congress terminates an economic countermeasure, the President may reimpose it only (a) after a 30-day waiting period, (b) based on materially changed circumstances documented in writing, and (c) subject to congressional termination again by the same process; the same countermeasure may not be reimposed more than once without new statutory authorization.

**1.3 — National Security Investment Review.** Congress shall establish a permanent National Security Investment Review body to screen foreign acquisitions of United States businesses, real property, and assets in sectors critical to national security. The Review body shall operate independently of political direction. Its decisions to block or condition acquisitions shall be based on objective national security criteria established by law. The President may not override a blocking decision of the Review body without a formal written national security determination published within 30 days. The President's override must be transmitted to Congress within 24 hours; Congress may nullify the override by majority vote within 30 days; if Congress nullifies the override, the transaction is automatically blocked; the President may not reimpose the override for the same transaction.

**1.4 — Critical sectors defined.** Congress shall define by law the sectors subject to investment review under Section 1.3. At minimum, the following shall be designated critical: defense and aerospace; semiconductors and advanced computing; telecommunications infrastructure; artificial intelligence; biotechnology and pharmaceuticals; energy production and distribution; water systems; and financial market infrastructure.

**1.5 — Economic coercion as national security threat.** Systematic foreign government efforts to create economic dependence — including through state-subsidized market manipulation, predatory investment, and infrastructure financing designed to establish leverage — shall be recognized as a national security threat. The President shall report to Congress annually on identified economic coercion campaigns and the countermeasures taken or proposed.

## Section 2 — Foreign Influence Operations

**2.1 — Scope.** Foreign adversary influence operations extend beyond election interference to include ongoing campaigns to shape domestic political opinion, policy, and institutions through covert or undisclosed foreign government direction. This Section addresses those operations without limiting protected speech or legitimate foreign engagement.

**2.2 — Mandatory disclosure of foreign government funding.** Any organization operating within the United States — including nonprofit organizations, think tanks, academic institutions, media organizations, cultural programs, and advocacy groups — that receives funding from a foreign government or a foreign government-controlled entity shall register with the Accountability Commission and publicly disclose:
  (a) The identity of the foreign government or entity providing funds
  (b) The amount received, updated quarterly
  (c) The purposes for which the funds are used

This requirement applies regardless of whether the organization engages in political activity. Disclosure is required for any foreign government funding above a threshold set by Congress. The constitutional floor for foreign government funding registration is $10,000 per year; Congress may set lower thresholds but not higher. Non-disclosure is a criminal offense. The Accountability Commission is the enforcement authority.

**2.3 — State-sponsored foreign media.** Any media organization that is editorially directed, substantially funded, or operationally controlled by a foreign government shall be registered as a foreign government media outlet with the Accountability Commission. All content produced by a registered foreign government media outlet distributed within the United States shall be labeled as such. Registration does not prohibit distribution — it requires transparency. Failure to register is a criminal offense. In addition to mandatory registration, state-sponsored media must display a prominent, clear, and standardized disclosure label on all content; the label must identify the sponsoring government and the relationship between the media outlet and that government; platforms that host state-sponsored media are required to enforce the labeling requirement.

**2.4 — Academic and research institutions.** Academic and research institutions receiving foreign government funding above a threshold set by Congress for research programs, academic chairs, or institutional programs shall disclose that funding publicly. Institutions may not accept foreign government funding subject to conditions that restrict research conclusions, limit publication, or require access to research results by the foreign government. Accepting such conditions voids the institution's eligibility for federal funding.

**2.5 — Enforcement.** The Accountability Commission shall maintain a public registry of all organizations registered under this Section. The Commission shall investigate suspected undisclosed foreign government funding operations and refer criminal violations for prosecution. The Commission shall publish an annual report on identified foreign influence operations and their sponsors.

## Section 3 — Critical Infrastructure Protection

**3.1 — Federal responsibility.** The federal government bears primary responsibility for protecting critical infrastructure — as defined in Section 1.4 of this Article — from attack, sabotage, and degradation by foreign governments, foreign-controlled entities, and terrorist organizations. This responsibility cannot be fully delegated to private owners of critical infrastructure.

**3.2 — Minimum security standards.** Congress shall establish by law minimum security standards for all critical infrastructure, whether publicly or privately owned. The standards shall address physical security, cybersecurity, supply chain integrity, and resilience to attack or disruption. Owners and operators of critical infrastructure shall comply with these standards as a condition of operating in the United States. Congress shall review and update these standards not less than every five years.

**3.3 — Federal assessment authority.** The executive branch shall conduct regular security assessments of all designated critical infrastructure. Owners and operators of critical infrastructure shall cooperate with federal security assessments. The existence of known foreign government-implanted malware, surveillance tools, or access mechanisms in critical infrastructure systems shall be reported to Congress within 24 hours of discovery; the President must simultaneously notify the Speaker of the House, the Majority Leader of the Regional Assembly, and the chairs of both intelligence committees; failure to report within 24 hours is grounds for impeachment.

**3.4 — Foreign ownership of critical infrastructure prohibited.** No foreign government, foreign government-controlled entity, or entity with a foreign government beneficial owner holding more than a threshold set by Congress may own, operate, or hold a controlling interest in any designated critical infrastructure within the United States. Existing holdings in violation of this Section shall be divested within two years of ratification or within two years of an asset being designated critical infrastructure, whichever is later. The 2-year divestiture window may not be extended; during the divestiture period, the foreign owner's operational control is suspended immediately upon the order; an independent trustee appointed by the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court shall manage the asset; the foreign owner retains financial interest but no operational control during divestiture.

**3.5 — Attack on critical infrastructure as act of war.** A foreign government-directed attack on United States critical infrastructure that causes significant physical damage, significant disruption to essential services, or loss of life shall be treated as an act of war for purposes of the response authorities in Article XI. The President may invoke the defensive response authorities of Article XI Section 1.4 against the attacking state or actor.

## Section 4 — Foreign Intelligence Collection

**4.1 — Purpose and balance.** The collection of foreign intelligence — information about the activities, intentions, and capabilities of foreign governments, foreign organizations, and foreign nationals — is a necessary function of national security. This Section establishes a constitutional framework for foreign intelligence collection that preserves civil liberties while enabling effective defense against foreign threats.

**4.2 — Foreign Intelligence Court.** Congress shall establish a specialized Foreign Intelligence Court composed of federal judges serving renewable three-year assignments, drawn from the Article IV judiciary. The Court shall:
  (a) Issue warrants for the collection of foreign intelligence within the United States
  (b) Review the legality and scope of intelligence collection programs
  (c) Publish annual statistics on warrants issued, denied, and modified, in unclassified form
  (d) Appoint independent counsel to argue against government warrant applications in significant cases

**4.3 — Warrant requirements for collection.** Collection of foreign intelligence that involves the interception of communications, the search of premises, or the collection of data about specific identified persons within the United States requires a warrant from the Foreign Intelligence Court upon a showing of:
  (a) Probable cause that the target is an agent of a foreign power or a foreign intelligence service
  (b) That the information sought is foreign intelligence information
  (c) That the collection will be conducted using minimization procedures that limit the retention and dissemination of information about United States persons

"Agent of a foreign power" means a person who (a) acts at the direction or control of a foreign government or intelligence service, (b) engages in clandestine intelligence activities on behalf of a foreign power, or (c) knowingly engages in sabotage, terrorism, or the unauthorized gathering of defense information for a foreign power; journalists, academics, and advocates engaged in lawful activity do not qualify regardless of foreign contact.

**4.4 — United States persons — heightened protection.** Collection of foreign intelligence that targets a United States citizen or permanent resident requires a higher showing: probable cause that the person is knowingly engaged in clandestine intelligence activities, sabotage, or terrorism on behalf of a foreign power. Incidental collection of United States persons' communications during foreign-targeted collection shall be minimized; communications of United States persons may not be searched without a separate warrant under Section 4.3.

**4.5 — Bulk collection limitations.** Bulk collection of communications — collection that is not targeted at specific identified persons — is permitted only for foreign-to-foreign communications transiting United States infrastructure. Bulk collection of domestic communications, or of communications where one end is a United States person, is prohibited without individual warrants under Section 4.3. The foreign-to-foreign carve-out applies only to communications where both parties are confirmed to be outside the United States at the time of communication; communications where either party is in the United States are subject to full warrant requirements; the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court shall certify compliance with this requirement annually.

**4.6 — Congressional oversight.** All foreign intelligence collection programs shall be disclosed to the relevant congressional oversight committees. No collection program whose existence is concealed from Congress is lawful. The Accountability Commission's classified oversight division under Article IX Section 3 shall audit compliance with this Section annually.

**4.7 — No domestic political use.** Foreign intelligence collection authorities may not be used to collect information on domestic political organizations, candidates, elected officials, journalists, or citizens on the basis of their political views or associations. Use of foreign intelligence authorities for domestic political surveillance is a criminal offense prosecutable by the Accountability Commission without a statute of limitations.

## Section 5 — Gray Zone Response Authority

**5.1 — Definition.** Gray zone operations are hostile actions by foreign states or state-directed actors that fall below the threshold of conventional military attack but are designed to harm United States interests, undermine democratic institutions, or coerce United States policy. They include: state-sponsored disinformation campaigns targeting domestic political processes; foreign government-directed sabotage of infrastructure; economic coercion through market manipulation or weaponized financial leverage; and hybrid operations combining cyber, information, and proxy forces.

**5.2 — Presidential response authority.** The President may respond proportionately to identified gray zone operations without triggering the full war powers authorization requirements of Article XI Section 1.2, subject to the following conditions:
  (a) The President must determine in writing that the operation constitutes a gray zone attack by an identified foreign state or state-directed actor
  (b) The response must be proportionate to the nature and scale of the gray zone operation
  (c) Congressional notification must occur before gray zone operations begin; the President may waive prior notification only if (1) the operation must begin within hours to be effective and (2) delay would cause irreparable harm; in that case, notification must occur within 6 hours of commencement; failure to notify is grounds for impeachment

**5.3 — Scope of gray zone response.** Permissible proportionate responses to gray zone operations include: counter-disinformation programs; economic countermeasures under Section 1 of this Article; defensive and proportionate offensive cyber responses; diplomatic measures; and targeted sanctions. Proportionate gray zone response does not authorize sustained military operations, which require authorization under Article XI Section 1.2.

**5.4 — Attribution standard.** A gray zone determination under Section 5.2 requires a written intelligence assessment attributing the operation to a foreign state or state-directed actor with at least high confidence. The written determination must be reviewed by the Director of National Intelligence and the Attorney General before the response is authorized; the determination is provided to congressional intelligence committees simultaneously with authorization. Responses based on lower-confidence attributions require congressional notification before the response is executed.

## Section 6 — Alliance Reliability

**6.1 — Treaty commitments are binding.** The President shall faithfully execute all mutual defense and collective security treaty obligations ratified by the Regional Assembly. The President may not selectively honor, condition, or withhold treaty obligations based on personal, political, financial, or extraneous considerations.

**6.2 — No conditioning of alliance obligations.** A President who conditions the fulfillment of a treaty alliance commitment — including military assistance, intelligence sharing, or collective defense obligations — on any consideration other than the treaty's terms and the requirements of this Constitution violates the oath of office and is subject to prosecution by the Accountability Commission under Article VI Section 1 and to impeachment under Article III Section 10.

**6.3 — Congressional affirmation.** Either chamber of Congress may by resolution independently affirm the United States' commitment to any treaty alliance. Such a resolution does not bind the President to take specific military action but creates a public record of congressional intent and may be considered in any proceeding under Section 6.2.

**6.4 — Withdrawal from collective security treaties.** The President may not withdraw the United States from any mutual defense or collective security treaty without the approval of a 2/3 vote of the Regional Assembly. The 2/3 requirement for collective security treaties is higher than the majority required for other treaty withdrawal under Article III Section 9.4, reflecting the particular destabilizing effect of withdrawing from collective defense arrangements.

## Section 7 — Technology and Supply Chain Security

**7.1 — Strategic technology protection.** The federal government has a responsibility to prevent foreign adversaries from acquiring, through investment, theft, academic transfer, or market manipulation, technology that would provide them a decisive military, intelligence, or economic advantage over the United States.

**7.2 — Export controls.** Congress shall maintain a system of export controls on technologies with significant national security implications. The executive branch shall administer export controls through an independent body whose decisions are based on objective security criteria. Export control decisions shall be reviewed annually and updated to reflect technological developments. Allies and partners may be granted preferential treatment in export licensing consistent with treaty obligations.

**7.3 — Technology transfer through academic and research institutions.** The executive branch shall maintain a program to identify and mitigate foreign adversary efforts to acquire sensitive technology through academic partnerships, research collaborations, and talent recruitment programs. Federal funding for research in sensitive technology areas shall be conditioned on recipient institutions maintaining appropriate controls on foreign participation. This Section shall be implemented in a manner that does not restrict legitimate international scientific collaboration or discriminate against researchers on the basis of national origin.

**7.4 — Supply chain security.** The federal government shall assess and mitigate national security risks in the supply chains for critical technology, defense systems, and critical infrastructure. Congress shall by law establish requirements for supply chain security assessment as a condition of federal procurement. Equipment from foreign adversary manufacturers shall not be used in federal systems or designated critical infrastructure where the Accountability Commission's technical review finds a material security risk.

**7.5 — No targeting of persons by national origin.** Nothing in this Article authorizes surveillance, investigation, restriction of employment, or any adverse action against any person solely on the basis of their national origin, ethnicity, or the country of their birth. Technology security measures must be based on specific, articulable security concerns, not on demographic characteristics. Racial or ethnic profiling in the implementation of this Article is prohibited and prosecutable. Technology security measures based on national origin without specific articulable individualized security concerns are subject to strict scrutiny; the Accountability Commission shall review all such measures annually and publish findings; measures that the Commission finds pretextual are automatically suspended pending judicial review.
