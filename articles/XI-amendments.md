# Article XI — Amendments

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

---

## Scope

Establishes the processes by which this Constitution may be amended, the provisions that may never be amended, and the judicial mechanisms that enforce both.

---

## Section 1 — General Principle

This Constitution is a living framework intended to endure across generations. It must be capable of legitimate change while remaining resistant to capture, dismantlement, or abuse by any temporary majority. These amendment processes are designed to make necessary change achievable and democratic destruction impossible.

---

## Section 2 — Two-Track Amendment Process

### Track 1 — Structural Amendments

Applies to changes affecting the structure or powers of the Legislative, Executive, or Judicial branches, the relationship between federal and state governments, or the composition or mandate of Constitutional Organs.

**Process:**
1. Proposed by 2/3 vote of both the House of Representatives and the Regional Assembly, in separate votes held no less than 90 days apart
2. Ratified by legislatures of 3/4 of the states within 7 years of proposal
3. Submitted to the Supreme Court for pre-clearance review before ratification votes begin (see Section 4)
4. Takes effect 1 year after certification of ratification, unless the amendment specifies otherwise

**Rationale:** Structural changes alter the balance of power between institutions. They require broad consensus and time for deliberation. The 90-day gap between votes prevents a single political moment from driving irreversible structural change.

---

### Track 2 — Rights Amendments

Applies to the expansion of rights enumerated in Article V, or the addition of new rights not currently enumerated. May not be used to reduce, restrict, or remove any existing right.

**Process:**
1. Proposed by 2/3 vote of both the House of Representatives and the Regional Assembly
2. Ratified by legislatures of a majority of states within 5 years of proposal
3. Confirmed by a national referendum administered by the Electoral Commission, requiring a majority of votes cast, with participation from at least 50% of registered voters
4. Submitted to the Supreme Court for pre-clearance review before ratification votes begin (see Section 4)
5. Takes effect 6 months after certification of ratification

**Rationale:** Rights expansion should be more accessible than structural change — a democratic majority should be able to extend protections without requiring near-unanimity. Rights reduction is prohibited under this track entirely; any amendment that would reduce rights must be rejected at pre-clearance.

---

### Shared Rules for Both Tracks

**No amendment during emergency.** No amendment under either track may be proposed, voted upon, or take effect during a declared national emergency. The amendment process resumes when the emergency lapses or is terminated. For purposes of this Article, "declared national emergency" means only an emergency declared under Article III §5 with full congressional approval as required by Article III §5.4. A unilateral presidential emergency declaration that has not received congressional approval under Article III §5.4 does not constitute a declared national emergency for purposes of this prohibition, and does not suspend, delay, or bar the amendment process.

**No retaliatory amendments.** No amendment may be designed to target a specific individual, party, or group for differential treatment. The Supreme Court shall reject any such amendment at pre-clearance.

**Rescission.** A state may rescind its ratification of a proposed amendment at any time before the amendment is certified as fully ratified.

**Expiration.** A proposed amendment that fails to achieve full ratification within the time limits specified above expires and may not be re-proposed in identical form for 4 years.

## Section 3 — The Unamendable Core

### 3.1 — Enumerated Unamendable Provisions

The following provisions of this Constitution may not be amended, repealed, suspended, or superseded under any process, by any majority, at any time:

1. **Universal suffrage.** The right of adult citizens to vote in free, fair, and regular elections, as established in Article I.

2. **Free and regular elections.** The requirement that federal elections be held on the schedules established by this Constitution and that their results be binding, as established in Article I.

   **Substance-over-label protection (applies to provisions 1 and 2).** The unamendable protections of provisions 1 and 2 apply to the substance of the rights they protect, not merely to their labels or formal designations. Any amendment that redefines "federal election," "eligible voter," "vote," or "election result" in a manner that narrows the practical exercise of the rights protected by provisions 1 or 2 — regardless of whether the amendment purports to amend those provisions directly — is subject to pre-clearance review under Section 4 as if it amended an enumerated unamendable provision. The Supreme Court shall look through the form of such an amendment to its practical effect on the franchise and on election integrity.

3. **Peaceful transfer of power.** The requirement that power transfer to the lawfully elected successor following any federal election, as established in Articles II and III.

4. **Anti-subversion.** The prohibition on attempting to subvert a lawful election or constitutional transfer of power, and the penalties therefor, as established in Article VI.

5. **Electoral Commission independence.** The independence, mandate, and protection from political direction of the Electoral Commission, as established in Article XII.

6. **Accountability Commission independence.** The independence, mandate, and protection from political direction of the Accountability Commission, as established in Article XII. Both Constitutional Organs are equally essential to democratic integrity; the independence of one cannot be preserved while the other is dismantled.

7. **Judicial independence.** The protections against politically motivated removal of federal judges and the independence of the federal judiciary, as established in Article IV.

8. **Separation of powers.** The division of governmental authority among the Legislative, Executive, and Judicial branches such that no single branch may exercise the full powers of another.

9. **Freedom of political expression.** The freedoms of speech, press, and peaceful assembly as they apply to political activity, as established in Article V.

10. **Due process and habeas corpus.** The right to due process of law and the protection against arbitrary detention or imprisonment, as established in Article V.

11. **This Section.** Section 3 of this Article, in its entirety, may not be amended, repealed, or altered.

### 3.2 — Principled Backstop

No amendment may permanently concentrate unchecked governmental power in a single person, political party, or institution. No amendment may eliminate or materially impair the capacity of citizens to alter their government through free and regular elections and lawful transfer of power. The Supreme Court shall enforce this Section against evasive or functionally equivalent changes as well as against amendments that directly contradict Section 3.1.

An amendment materially impairs that capacity if, in substance or practical effect, it:
  (a) meaningfully narrows the electorate on arbitrary, partisan, hereditary, or discriminatory grounds;
  (b) prevents federal elections from remaining free, regular, and binding;
  (c) disables a meaningful avenue of peaceful political change;
  (d) strips a branch, electorate, or constitutionally independent institution of the minimum powers necessary to check entrenched rule; or
  (e) entrenches a person, party, or governing bloc against electoral replacement beyond ordinary constitutional accountability.

### 3.3 — Why These Provisions Are Unamendable

Each provision in Section 3.1 satisfies the following test: *its removal would destroy the legitimacy of any future amendment, because it is a prerequisite for democratic consent itself.* Provisions that do not satisfy this test — however important — belong on Track 1, not in the unamendable core.

The unamendable core is not an entrenchment of any political faction's preferences. It is the minimum structural floor below which self-governance becomes impossible. Future generations retain full authority to amend everything else.

## Section 4 — Judicial Pre-Clearance

### 4.1 — Mandatory Review

Before any proposed amendment under Track 1 or Track 2 is submitted to states for ratification, it must be submitted to the Supreme Court for pre-clearance review. Ratification votes may not begin until the Court has issued its ruling.

### 4.2 — Review Standard

The Supreme Court shall review the proposed amendment solely to determine:

(a) Whether it conflicts with any provision of Section 3.1 (enumerated unamendable provisions)

(b) Whether it violates the principled backstop of Section 3.2

(c) Whether it is properly classified under Track 1 or Track 2 — the Court shall determine the correct track if there is a dispute, and may reclassify an amendment to the appropriate track

(d) Whether it targets a specific individual, party, or group in violation of Section 2

The Court may not review the wisdom, desirability, or policy merits of a proposed amendment. Its jurisdiction at this stage is limited to the four questions above.

### 4.3 — Timeline

For **Track 1 structural amendments**, the Supreme Court must issue its pre-clearance ruling within 90 days of submission. For **Track 2 rights amendments**, the Court must issue its ruling within 60 days. If no ruling is issued within the applicable deadline, pre-clearance is deemed granted and ratification may proceed. Any period during which the Court is demonstrably prevented from acting by executive interference does not count toward the deadline; the Clerk of the Court shall publicly certify any such period with specificity.

### 4.4 — Outcomes

- **Cleared:** Ratification may proceed under the applicable track.
- **Rejected:** The amendment may not proceed to ratification. When the Supreme Court rejects an amendment at pre-clearance, it must publish a written opinion stating with specificity the constitutional ground of rejection. Congress may revise and resubmit after a 90-day waiting period.
- **Reclassified:** The amendment proceeds under the correct track as determined by the Court.

### 4.5 — Standing

Any member of Congress, any state legislature, or the President may petition the Supreme Court for pre-clearance review. Citizens may submit amicus briefs during the review period.

## Section 5 — Post-Ratification Review

### 5.1 — Challenge Window

Any ratified amendment may be challenged in federal court as violating Section 3 of this Article within 2 years of certification of ratification.

### 5.2 — Standing to Challenge

Standing to bring a post-ratification challenge is held by:
- Any member of Congress
- The legislature of any state
- The President
- The Accountability Commission (Article XII)
- Any citizen whose rights are directly impaired by the amendment

Any citizen has standing to challenge a ratified amendment that violates the unamendable core of Section 3, regardless of whether that citizen can demonstrate particularized injury beyond the impairment of the constitutional order protected by that Section. A challenge by any citizen under this paragraph must be filed within 2 years of the certification of ratification.

### 5.3 — Adjudication

Post-ratification challenges are heard directly by the Supreme Court, which has original and exclusive jurisdiction. The Court must issue a ruling within 180 days of the challenge being filed.

### 5.4 — Remedy

If the Supreme Court finds that a ratified amendment violates Section 3, the amendment is void in its entirety from the date of the Court's ruling. The Constitution is restored to its prior state. Congress may not re-propose the voided amendment for 10 years.

### 5.5 — Finality

After the 2-year challenge window closes, a ratified amendment is immune from challenge on Section 3 grounds and becomes a permanent part of this Constitution. This finality provision does not apply to amendments that themselves violate Section 3 — a void amendment is void regardless of when the violation is raised.

---

## Section 6 — Transition at Ratification

**6.1** Ratification transition, carried-forward law, supersession of prior constitutional provisions, and first-cycle implementation are governed by Article XIX.

**6.2** The permanent amendment rules of this Article apply subject to the first-cycle limitations and harmonizing rules established in Article XIX.
