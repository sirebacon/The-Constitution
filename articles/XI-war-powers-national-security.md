# Article XI — War Powers and National Security

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

---

## Scope

Establishes the constitutional framework for the use of military force and the conduct of war. The war-making power belongs to Congress. The President directs authorized operations and responds to immediate threats. This Article defines both authorities and their limits.

Economic security, foreign influence defense, critical infrastructure protection, foreign intelligence collection, gray zone response authority, alliance reliability, and technology/supply chain security are established in **[Article XIV — Foreign Policy and National Security](XIV-foreign-policy-national-security.md)**.

---

## Section 1 — Commander in Chief and War Powers Authority

**1.1 — Commander in Chief.** The President is commander in chief of the armed forces of the United States and of the state National Guards when called into federal service. This authority is exercised within the limits established by this Constitution. The power to initiate war belongs to Congress. The President's role as commander in chief is the authority to direct and conduct military operations once authorized — it is not independent authority to begin them.

**1.2 — Congressional Authorization Required.** The President may not commit the armed forces, cyber forces, intelligence personnel acting in a paramilitary capacity, or private military contractors in a combat role to any military operation abroad — offensive or defensive beyond immediate response — without either:

  (a) A formal declaration of war against a named state or state actor, passed by majority vote of both chambers of Congress; or
  (b) An Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF) meeting the requirements of Section 1.6

The distinction between "offensive" and "defensive" does not determine whether authorization is required. The duration, scale, and nature of the operation determine whether it constitutes a commitment of forces requiring authorization. A long-running operation that began defensively requires authorization once it extends beyond the 30-day window of Section 1.5.

**1.3 — Definition of Military Force.** For purposes of this Article, "use of military force" includes:

  (a) Deployment of armed forces in a combat or combat-support role in a foreign country
  (b) Offensive cyber operations — defined as operations designed to cause physical destruction, significant disruption to critical infrastructure, casualty-producing effects, or the degradation of a foreign nation's military or governmental capacity
  (c) Lethal drone or aerial strikes against targets in a foreign country
  (d) Deployment of private military contractors in a combat role or in direct support of combat operations
  (e) Covert paramilitary operations conducted by intelligence agencies involving lethal force against foreign nationals
  (f) Naval blockades or maritime operations designed to coerce a foreign government

Defensive cyber operations — including monitoring, detecting, and neutralizing attacks on United States systems — and intelligence collection operations that do not involve lethal force are not "use of military force" for purposes of this Article and do not require authorization under Section 1.2.

**1.4 — Defensive Action Without Prior Authorization.** The President may, without prior congressional authorization, order military action to:

  (a) Repel an attack on United States territory, United States armed forces, or treaty allies under active attack pursuant to Section 6
  (b) Protect United States military forces under imminent and specific threat
  (c) Execute a time-critical operation to rescue United States citizens in immediate physical danger
  (d) Respond to an offensive cyber attack against United States critical infrastructure by neutralizing the attacking capability — defined as disabling the specific systems, tools, or infrastructure used to execute the attack in progress. "Neutralizing the attacking capability" does not authorize destruction of command infrastructure, broader offensive cyber campaigns, targeting of civilian systems, or any action against systems not directly involved in the specific attack being responded to. Any action beyond disabling the specific systems used in the attack requires a formal Authorization for Use of Military Force under Section 1.6.

Defensive action under this Section must be proportionate to the threat, limited in scope to the specific defensive objective, and reported to Congress under Section 1.5. Defensive action does not authorize a broader military campaign, the occupation of foreign territory, or offensive operations beyond the immediate defensive objective.

**1.5 — Reporting, Authorization, and Enforcement.**

  **(a) Reporting.** Within 24 hours of ordering any military action under Section 1.4 or any other use of military force, the President shall submit a written report to Congress describing: the nature and scope of the action; the legal basis for the action; the anticipated duration; the forces involved; and, in the case of cyber operations, the target systems and expected effects.

  **(b) Authorization window.** Military action taken under Section 1.4 must receive congressional authorization within 30 days or forces must be withdrawn. Congress shall vote on authorization within 30 days of receiving the report under Section 1.5(a). If Congress fails to vote within 30 days, the action is automatically unauthorized — the failure to vote has the same legal effect as a vote against authorization and does not require any further action or finding.

  **(c) Enforcement — automatic withdrawal order.** If authorization is not granted within 30 days, the Chief Justice of the Supreme Court shall issue a withdrawal order on day 35 — that is, exactly 5 days after the 30-day authorization deadline passes. The Chief Justice's withdrawal order is self-executing: it does not require any party to bring suit, does not require any court finding, and takes effect automatically upon issuance. The withdrawal order is enforceable by any federal court. The Accountability Commission shall independently monitor compliance with every withdrawal order and publish findings. The Secretary of Defense is personally liable for compliance with the withdrawal order and shall ensure that United States forces are withdrawn within 15 days of the order's issuance. Refusal by the Secretary of Defense to comply is grounds for court-martial and removal from office. If the President defies the withdrawal order, the Secretary of Defense has an affirmative constitutional duty to comply with the order regardless of any contrary presidential directive; presidential defiance of the order is an absolute no-immunity category under Article III Section 15.

  **(d) Continued operations after withdrawal order.** Ordering or maintaining military operations in violation of a withdrawal order issued under Section 1.5(c) is:
    (i) A violation of this Constitution and grounds for impeachment under Article III Section 10
    (ii) A criminal offense prosecutable by the Accountability Commission under Article VI Section 1 as an abuse of official power
    (iii) An order that members of the armed forces are not required to follow under Section 8

  **(e) No self-authorization.** The President may not satisfy the authorization requirement of this Article by executive order, national security directive, or any unilateral executive action. Only an act of Congress constitutes authorization.

**1.6 — Requirements for an Authorization for Use of Military Force.** An Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF) passed by Congress must:

  (a) Name the enemy — identify specifically the state, non-state actor, or organization against whom force is authorized, using the entity's proper name or recognized legal designation. An AUMF may not authorize force against unnamed or unspecified future enemies. "Associated forces," "entities with ties to," "successor organizations," or similar open-ended language is void as an authorization unless each associated force, successor, or affiliated entity is separately and specifically named in the AUMF and individually authorized by a majority vote of both chambers of Congress. Expansion of an existing AUMF to cover a force not named in the original authorization requires a new AUMF; it may not be accomplished by executive determination, subsequent finding, or reinterpretation of existing authorization.
  (b) Define the objective — state the specific military or strategic objective the authorization is designed to achieve
  (c) Define the geographic scope — identify the specific countries, regions, or territories where force is authorized. An AUMF does not authorize operations in territories not named.
  (d) Define the scope of force — specify whether the authorization covers ground forces, air operations, cyber operations, special operations, or other categories. An AUMF does not authorize categories of force not named.
  (e) Include a sunset clause — every AUMF shall expire not more than two years from the date of passage unless renewed by Congress. An AUMF may be renewed for successive two-year periods by majority vote of both chambers.
  (f) Include reporting requirements — every AUMF shall require the President to report to Congress not less than every 90 days on operations conducted under the authorization

An AUMF that does not meet these requirements is void. Congress may not delegate open-ended war-making authority to the President through vague or unlimited authorizations.

**1.7 — Sunset and Renewal of Existing AUMFs.** All Authorizations for Use of Military Force enacted prior to the ratification of this Constitution are void one year after ratification unless Congress enacts a renewed authorization meeting the requirements of Section 1.6. The President shall submit to Congress, within 90 days of ratification, a report identifying all military operations then being conducted under pre-existing AUMFs, the countries and forces involved, and the objectives being pursued. Congress shall review each such authorization within one year.

This Section does not terminate ongoing military operations immediately — it requires that they be brought within the constitutional framework within one year. Operations that cannot be authorized under Section 1.6 must be concluded within that period.

## Section 2 — Cyber Warfare

**2.1 — Offensive cyber operations require authorization.** Offensive cyber operations as defined in Section 1.3(b) require the same congressional authorization as conventional military force. The President may not order an offensive cyber operation against a foreign nation or actor without a declaration of war, an AUMF covering that operation, or defensive authorization under Section 1.4(d).

**2.2 — Proportionality.** Offensive cyber operations must be proportionate to the threat or objective. Cyber operations designed to cause civilian casualties, destroy civilian infrastructure unrelated to military objectives, or produce effects equivalent to a conventional military attack require authorization commensurate with that level of force.

For purposes of this Section, "proportionate" means that a cyber response may not cause greater damage than the attack that triggered it. Proportionality is measured across three dimensions: (a) economic harm — the monetary cost of damage caused, including direct costs and reasonably foreseeable downstream economic disruption; (b) civilian harm — the number of civilians affected and the severity of effects on civilian life, health, and safety; and (c) infrastructure disruption — the scope and duration of damage to critical infrastructure systems. A response that exceeds the triggering attack on any of these three dimensions is disproportionate and requires a formal Authorization for Use of Military Force under Section 1.6. The proportionality determination is reviewable by the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court on application by any member of the congressional oversight committees.

**2.3 — Disclosure to Congress.** All offensive cyber operations conducted under any authorization shall be disclosed to the relevant congressional oversight committees within 48 hours of execution. Classification of the specific operational details is permitted; the existence and general nature of the operation may not be withheld from oversight committees.

**2.4 — Defensive cyber operations.** The President may order defensive cyber operations — including active defense measures that operate on foreign systems to neutralize an ongoing attack — without prior congressional authorization. Defensive cyber operations must be reported to Congress within 24 hours under Section 1.5(a).

## Section 3 — Nuclear Weapons

**3.1 — No unilateral first use.** The President may not order the first use of nuclear weapons — defined as the use of nuclear weapons against a nation or actor that has not used nuclear weapons against the United States or its treaty allies — without:
  (a) Confirmation by both the Secretary of Defense and the Secretary of State that the order is lawful and warranted; and
  (b) A written finding by the Director of National Intelligence that no non-nuclear response is sufficient to address the threat; and
  (c) A 6-hour waiting period following the issuance of the Director of National Intelligence's written finding, during which the order may not be executed.

If either the Secretary of Defense or the Secretary of State declines to confirm the order, it does not proceed. The 6-hour waiting period may be waived only if a nuclear strike against the United States is confirmed as incoming by independent verification systems; in that circumstance, the response procedures of Section 3.3 apply. The written DNI finding shall be preserved as a permanent record and provided to the Accountability Commission within 24 hours of its issuance.

**3.2 — Congressional notification and approval for first use.** If both Secretaries confirm a first-use order and the Director of National Intelligence issues the required finding under Section 3.1(b), the Speaker of the House and the Majority Leader of the Regional Assembly shall be notified immediately. The President must receive formal non-objection from both the Speaker of the House and the Majority Leader of the Regional Assembly before executing a first-use order. If either leader formally objects, the order may not proceed unless a 2/3 vote of both chambers overrides the objection. This requirement is binding — it is not a notification requirement only and does not merely create a record. The minority leaders and all relevant committee chairs shall also be notified for the record. This approval requirement does not apply if a nuclear strike against the United States is confirmed as incoming, in which case the response procedures of Section 3.3 apply.

**3.3 — Response to confirmed nuclear attack.** If the United States or a treaty ally is under confirmed nuclear attack, the President may order a nuclear response without the confirmation requirement of Section 3.1. The response must be proportionate to the attack. Congressional leadership shall be notified as rapidly as the operational circumstances permit.

**3.4 — Launch authority delegation.** The President may not permanently or preemptively delegate nuclear launch authority to any other person. Temporary delegation during presidential incapacity follows the succession provisions of Article III Section 12.

**3.5 — No autonomous launch.** No automated system, artificial intelligence, or algorithm may be authorized to launch nuclear weapons without a direct human order from an authorized person. Autonomous or semi-autonomous nuclear launch systems are prohibited.

## Section 4 — Private Military Contractors

**4.1 — Authorization required.** Private military contractors operating in a combat role or in direct support of combat operations abroad are subject to all war powers requirements of this Article. Their deployment must be reported to Congress under Section 1.5(a) as part of any military operation. They may not be used to circumvent the authorization requirements of Section 1.2.

**4.2 — Laws of war.** Private military contractors deployed by or on behalf of the United States government are subject to the laws of war and the Uniform Code of Military Justice. Violations are prosecutable in federal courts and by the Accountability Commission.

**4.3 — Annual disclosure.** The President shall report to Congress annually the total number of private military contractors deployed abroad, their locations, their operational roles, and the total expenditure on their contracts. This report shall be unclassified to the maximum extent consistent with operational security.

Classification of any portion of the annual PMC report as required for operational security purposes requires a written determination by the Director of National Intelligence specifying the basis for classification. Such a determination expires after 180 days and must be renewed by a new written DNI determination to remain in effect. Regardless of any classification determination, the following information shall be disclosed to the congressional intelligence committees and may not be withheld: the total contract value for each PMC engagement; the general operational area (by country or region); and the mission category of each engagement (combat, training, or logistics). No PMC expenditure or contract may be classified in its entirety — at minimum, the existence of the contract, its general mission category, and its total value shall be disclosed to the oversight committees.

**4.4 — No substitution.** Private military contractors may not be used as a substitute for authorized military force to avoid reporting requirements, to conduct operations in countries where military deployment would require authorization, or to conceal the scale of United States military engagement in a conflict.

## Section 5 — Covert Lethal Operations

**5.1 — Authorization required.** Covert operations conducted by intelligence agencies or special operations forces involving lethal force against foreign nationals require either:
  (a) An existing AUMF that covers the target, the territory, and the category of force; or
  (b) Notification of the congressional Gang of Eight — the majority and minority leaders of both chambers and the chairs and ranking members of the intelligence committees — within 48 hours of the operation

**5.2 — No operations against US citizens without warrant.** No covert lethal operation, whether conducted by military or intelligence personnel, may target a United States citizen anywhere in the world without a judicial warrant. The warrant must be issued by the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court upon a finding of probable cause that the citizen poses an imminent and specific threat to the United States and that capture is not feasible. The warrant must be obtained before the operation is executed unless exigent circumstances make prior authorization impossible — for example, where the operational window is so narrow that obtaining prior authorization would cause the threat to materialize. In exigent circumstances, a warrant application must be submitted to the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court within 72 hours of the operation. The warrant requirement is not waived by the location of the citizen or by the operational difficulty of capture.

The warrant application and the court's ruling shall be unsealed and made available to the public within 10 years of the date of issuance, unless the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court renews a specific classification determination for the application and ruling within that period. Renewed classification must be supported by a written finding that disclosure would cause specific, articulable harm; it may be renewed in successive 5-year periods on the same showing.

**5.3 — Accountability Commission oversight.** The Accountability Commission (Article IX) shall maintain a classified division with appropriate security clearances to audit covert lethal operations for compliance with this Section. The division shall report annually to the relevant congressional oversight committees in classified form and publish an unclassified summary.

## Section 6 — Treaty-Based Defense Obligations

A mutual defense treaty ratified by the Regional Assembly under Article III Section 9.2 constitutes advance congressional authorization for strictly defensive military action required to fulfill that specific treaty obligation. This authorization is limited to:
  (a) Defensive operations to repel an attack on the treaty ally
  (b) Operations specifically required by the terms of the ratified treaty
  (c) The scope and duration necessary to fulfill the immediate defensive obligation

Any military action beyond strict treaty-based defense — including offensive operations, operations in territories not covered by the treaty, or operations that extend beyond repelling the specific attack — requires separate congressional authorization under Section 1.2. The existence of a treaty obligation does not authorize an open-ended military campaign.

## Section 7 — Domestic Deployment

The armed forces, National Guard units in federal service, and all paramilitary forces under federal authority may not be deployed against civilians on domestic soil except:

  (a) To suppress an armed rebellion upon a declaration by Congress identifying the specific rebellion and authorizing specific military action
  (b) To provide disaster relief and humanitarian assistance, at the request of a state governor or by congressional authorization, in a non-combat capacity
  (c) To protect specific federal facilities, personnel, or infrastructure against imminent physical attack, limited in scope to the specific protective mission

For purposes of this Section, "armed rebellion" means the organized use of deadly force by a group of 100 or more persons acting in concert to: overthrow a federal or state government; seize and hold territory by force against the lawful authority of the United States; or prevent federal law enforcement from operating in a defined geographic area through the use or credible threat of deadly force. Political protest, civil disobedience, and uncoordinated riots — even if they involve violence — do not constitute armed rebellion under this Section. A presidential declaration of armed rebellion must be certified by the Accountability Commission within 72 hours of issuance; if the Accountability Commission declines to certify, the declaration is void and all military deployments authorized under it must cease immediately.

The President may not declare a domestic insurrection or rebellion for the purpose of deploying military force against peaceful protest, civil unrest, or political opposition. A deployment of armed forces against domestic civilians that is not authorized by this Section is void, and members of the armed forces are not required to carry it out under Section 8.

---

## Section 8 — Unlawful Orders

No member of the armed forces, intelligence service, or any paramilitary force under federal authority may be ordered to carry out, and no such person is required to obey, a command that:

  (a) Violates this Constitution
  (b) Violates a law of the United States
  (c) Violates the laws of war as recognized under international treaty ratified by the United States
  (d) Orders the use of nuclear weapons in violation of Section 3
  (e) Orders continued military operations in violation of a withdrawal order issued under Section 1.5(c)
  (f) Orders the deployment of armed forces against domestic civilians in violation of Section 7

A member of the armed forces who refuses to carry out an unlawful order under this Section is protected from punishment for that refusal. A superior who punishes a subordinate for refusing an unlawful order is subject to prosecution by the Accountability Commission. A member of the armed forces who carries out an unlawful order is not relieved of personal liability for the consequences of that order.
