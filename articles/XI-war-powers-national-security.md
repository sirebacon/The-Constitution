# Article XI — War Powers and National Security

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

---

## Scope

Establishes the constitutional framework for the use of military force and the conduct of war. The war-making power belongs to Congress. The President directs authorized operations and responds to immediate threats. This Article governs armed conflict, combat operations, and other uses of military force.

Economic security, foreign influence defense, critical infrastructure protection, foreign intelligence collection, gray-zone response short of sustained armed conflict, alliance reliability, and technology and supply-chain security are established in **[Article XIV — Foreign Policy and National Security](XIV-foreign-policy-national-security.md)**.

---

## Section 1 — Commander in Chief and War Powers Authority

**1.1 — Commander in Chief.** The President is commander in chief of the armed forces of the United States and of the state National Guards when called into federal service. This authority is exercised within the limits established by this Constitution. The power to initiate war belongs to Congress. The President's role as commander in chief is the authority to direct and conduct military operations once authorized — it is not independent authority to begin them.

**1.2 — Congressional Authorization Required.** The President may not commit the armed forces, cyber forces, intelligence personnel acting in a paramilitary capacity, or private military contractors in a combat role to any military operation abroad — offensive or defensive beyond immediate response — without either:

  (a) A formal declaration of war against a named state or state actor, passed by majority vote of both chambers of Congress; or
  (b) An Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF) meeting the requirements of Section 1.6

The distinction between "offensive" and "defensive" does not determine whether authorization is required. The duration, scale, and nature of the operation determine whether it constitutes a commitment of forces requiring authorization. A long-running operation that began defensively requires authorization once it extends beyond the 30-day window of Section 1.5.

**1.3 — Definition of Military Force.** For purposes of this Article, "use of military force" includes:

  (a) Deployment of armed forces in a combat or combat-support role in a foreign country
  (b) Offensive cyber operations — defined as operations designed to cause physical destruction, significant disruption to critical infrastructure, casualty-producing effects, or the degradation of a foreign nation's military or governmental capacity
  (c) Lethal drone or aerial strikes against targets in a foreign country
  (d) Deployment of private military contractors in a combat role or in direct support of combat operations
  (e) Covert paramilitary operations conducted by intelligence agencies involving lethal force against foreign nationals
  (f) Naval blockades or maritime operations designed to coerce a foreign government

Defensive cyber operations, intelligence collection operations that do not involve lethal force, sanctions, diplomatic action, and other non-military countermeasures governed by Article XIV are not "use of military force" for purposes of this Article.

**1.4 — Defensive Action Without Prior Authorization.** The President may, without prior congressional authorization, order military action to:

  (a) Repel an attack on United States territory, United States armed forces, or treaty allies under active attack pursuant to Section 6
  (b) Protect United States military forces under imminent and specific threat
  (c) Execute a time-critical operation to rescue United States citizens in immediate physical danger
  (d) Respond to an offensive cyber attack against United States critical infrastructure by neutralizing the attacking capability, limited to disabling the specific systems, tools, or infrastructure used in the attack in progress

Defensive action under this Section must be proportionate to the threat, limited in scope to the specific defensive objective, and reported to Congress under Section 1.5. It does not authorize a broader military campaign, occupation of foreign territory, or sustained hostilities.

**1.5 — Reporting, Authorization, and Enforcement.**

  **(a) Reporting.** Within 24 hours of ordering military action under Section 1.4 or any other use of military force, the President shall submit a written report to Congress describing the action, its legal basis, its anticipated duration, and the forces involved.

  **(b) Authorization window.** Military action taken under Section 1.4 must receive congressional authorization within 30 days or forces must be withdrawn. Failure of Congress to authorize within that period has the same legal effect as a vote against authorization.

  **(c) Enforcement — withdrawal order.** If authorization is not granted within 30 days, the Chief Justice of the Supreme Court shall issue a binding withdrawal order enforceable by the federal courts. The Secretary of Defense shall ensure compliance within the period fixed by law, which may not exceed 15 days. Presidential defiance of a withdrawal order is a grave constitutional violation and an absolute no-immunity category under Article III Section 15.

  **(d) Continued operations after withdrawal order.** Ordering or maintaining military operations in violation of a withdrawal order is a violation of this Constitution, grounds for impeachment, a criminal abuse of official power, and an unlawful order under Section 8. Upon issuance of the withdrawal order, no funds may be obligated or expended for continued unlawful operations except as strictly necessary to protect forces during disengagement. If the President purports to continue the operation after the withdrawal order takes effect, lawful operational authority is limited to terminating, withdrawing, or defending withdrawing forces; no officer is required or permitted to continue the unlawful operation on the President's command.

  **(e) Expedited impeachment vote.** A presidential order maintaining operations in violation of a withdrawal order shall trigger the expedited impeachment timetable of Article III Section 10.2A.

  **(f) No self-authorization.** Only an act of Congress constitutes authorization under this Article.

**1.6 — Requirements for an Authorization for Use of Military Force.** An Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF) passed by Congress must:

  (a) Name the enemy specifically; open-ended references to associated or future forces are void
  (b) State the objective of the authorization
  (c) Define the geographic scope
  (d) Define the categories of force authorized
  (e) Expire not more than two years after passage unless renewed by Congress
  (f) Require periodic reporting to Congress

An AUMF that does not meet these requirements is void. Congress may not delegate open-ended war-making authority to the President through vague or unlimited authorizations.

**1.7 — Sunset and Renewal of Existing AUMFs.** All Authorizations for Use of Military Force enacted before ratification are void one year after ratification unless renewed in conformity with Section 1.6. The President shall report to Congress within 90 days on all operations then being conducted under pre-existing authorizations.

## Section 2 — Cyber Warfare

**2.1 — Offensive cyber operations require authorization.** Offensive cyber operations as defined in Section 1.3(b) require the same congressional authorization as conventional military force. The President may not order an offensive cyber operation against a foreign nation or actor without a declaration of war, an AUMF covering that operation, or defensive authorization under Section 1.4(d).

**2.2 — Proportionality.** Offensive cyber operations must be proportionate to the threat or objective. Cyber operations designed to cause civilian casualties, destroy civilian infrastructure unrelated to military objectives, or produce effects equivalent to a conventional military attack require authorization commensurate with that level of force.

Proportionality under this Section shall be judged by the scale of harm, the degree of civilian risk, and the relationship of the response to the triggering attack.

**2.3 — Disclosure to Congress.** All offensive cyber operations conducted under any authorization shall be disclosed to the relevant congressional oversight committees within 48 hours of execution. Classification of the specific operational details is permitted; the existence and general nature of the operation may not be withheld from oversight committees.

**2.4 — Defensive cyber operations.** The President may order defensive cyber operations — including active defense measures that operate on foreign systems to neutralize an ongoing attack — without prior congressional authorization. Defensive cyber operations must be reported to Congress within 24 hours under Section 1.5(a).

## Section 3 — Nuclear Weapons

**3.1 — No unilateral first use.** The President may not order the first use of nuclear weapons without confirmation by the Secretary of Defense and the Secretary of State that the order is lawful and warranted, a written finding by the Director of National Intelligence that no non-nuclear response is sufficient, and immediate notification to congressional leadership. If either Secretary declines to confirm the order, it does not proceed.

**3.2 — Congressional objection to first use.** A first-use order may not proceed if the Speaker of the House or the Majority Leader of the Regional Assembly formally objects, unless Congress overrides the objection by a 2/3 vote of both chambers. These requirements do not apply to a response to a confirmed nuclear attack governed by Section 3.3.

**3.3 — Response to confirmed nuclear attack.** If the United States or a treaty ally is under confirmed nuclear attack, the President may order a nuclear response without the confirmation requirement of Section 3.1. The response must be proportionate to the attack. Congressional leadership shall be notified as rapidly as the operational circumstances permit.

**3.4 — Launch authority delegation.** The President may not permanently or preemptively delegate nuclear launch authority to any other person. Temporary delegation during presidential incapacity follows the succession provisions of Article III Section 12.

**3.5 — No autonomous launch.** No automated system, artificial intelligence, or algorithm may be authorized to launch nuclear weapons without a direct human order from an authorized person. Autonomous or semi-autonomous nuclear launch systems are prohibited.

## Section 4 — Private Military Contractors

**4.1 — Authorization required.** Private military contractors operating in a combat role or in direct support of combat operations abroad are subject to all war powers requirements of this Article. Their deployment must be reported to Congress under Section 1.5(a) as part of any military operation. They may not be used to circumvent the authorization requirements of Section 1.2.

**4.2 — Laws of war.** Private military contractors deployed by or on behalf of the United States government are subject to the laws of war and the Uniform Code of Military Justice. Violations are prosecutable in federal courts and by the Accountability Commission.

**4.3 — Annual disclosure.** The President shall report to Congress annually the number, locations, operational roles, and total cost of private military contractors deployed abroad. Operational details may be classified as necessary, but the existence, general location, mission category, and total cost of such deployments shall always be disclosed to the relevant congressional oversight committees.

**4.4 — No substitution.** Private military contractors may not be used as a substitute for authorized military force to avoid reporting requirements, to conduct operations in countries where military deployment would require authorization, or to conceal the scale of United States military engagement in a conflict.

## Section 5 — Covert Lethal Operations

**5.1 — Authorization required.** Covert operations conducted by intelligence agencies or special operations forces involving lethal force against foreign nationals require either:
  (a) An existing AUMF that covers the target, the territory, and the category of force; or
  (b) A lawful defensive justification under Section 1.4 together with prompt notification to the congressional intelligence leadership

**5.2 — No operations against US citizens without warrant.** No covert lethal operation may target a United States citizen anywhere in the world without a judicial warrant issued upon probable cause that the citizen poses an imminent and specific threat to the United States and that capture is not feasible. In exigent circumstances, a warrant application must be submitted within 72 hours after the operation.

The warrant application and the court's ruling shall be made public within a period set by law unless a court makes a renewed finding that disclosure would cause specific and articulable harm to national security.

**5.3 — Accountability Commission oversight.** The Accountability Commission (Article IX) shall maintain a classified division with appropriate security clearances to audit covert lethal operations for compliance with this Section. The division shall report annually to the relevant congressional oversight committees in classified form and publish an unclassified summary.

## Section 6 — Treaty-Based Defense Obligations

A mutual defense treaty ratified by the Regional Assembly under Article III Section 9.2 constitutes advance congressional authorization for strictly defensive military action required to fulfill that specific treaty obligation. This authorization is limited to:
  (a) Defensive operations to repel an attack on the treaty ally
  (b) Operations specifically required by the terms of the ratified treaty
  (c) The scope and duration necessary to fulfill the immediate defensive obligation

Any military action beyond strict treaty-based defense — including offensive operations, operations in territories not covered by the treaty, or operations that extend beyond repelling the specific attack — requires separate congressional authorization under Section 1.2. The existence of a treaty obligation does not authorize an open-ended military campaign.

## Section 7 — Domestic Deployment

The armed forces, National Guard units in federal service, and all paramilitary forces under federal authority may not be deployed against civilians on domestic soil except:

  (a) To suppress an armed rebellion upon a declaration by Congress identifying the specific rebellion and authorizing specific military action
  (b) To provide disaster relief and humanitarian assistance, at the request of a state governor or by congressional authorization, in a non-combat capacity
  (c) To protect specific federal facilities, personnel, or infrastructure against imminent physical attack, limited in scope to the specific protective mission

For purposes of this Section, "armed rebellion" means the organized use of deadly force to overthrow government, seize and hold territory, or prevent federal law enforcement from operating in a defined area. Political protest, civil disobedience, and uncoordinated riots do not constitute armed rebellion under this Section. A presidential declaration of armed rebellion must be promptly reviewed by the Accountability Commission; if the Commission rejects the declaration, the deployment authority lapses.

The President may not declare a domestic insurrection or rebellion for the purpose of deploying military force against peaceful protest, civil unrest, or political opposition. A deployment of armed forces against domestic civilians that is not authorized by this Section is void, and members of the armed forces are not required to carry it out under Section 8.

---

## Section 8 — Unlawful Orders

No member of the armed forces, intelligence service, or any paramilitary force under federal authority may be ordered to carry out, and no such person is required to obey, a command that:

  (a) Violates this Constitution
  (b) Violates a law of the United States
  (c) Violates the laws of war as recognized under international treaty ratified by the United States
  (d) Orders the use of nuclear weapons in violation of Section 3
  (e) Orders continued military operations in violation of a withdrawal order issued under Section 1.5(c)
  (f) Orders the deployment of armed forces against domestic civilians in violation of Section 7

A member of the armed forces who refuses to carry out an unlawful order under this Section is protected from punishment for that refusal. A superior who punishes a subordinate for refusing an unlawful order is subject to prosecution by the Accountability Commission. A member of the armed forces who carries out an unlawful order is not relieved of personal liability for the consequences of that order.
