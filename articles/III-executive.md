# Article III — The Executive

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

---

## Scope

Establishes the structure, powers, and limits of the executive branch. The President is both head of state and head of government. Executive power is derived from this Constitution and may not be exercised beyond its limits regardless of congressional authorization.

---

## Section 1 — The President

**1.1** The executive power of the United States is vested in a President of the United States of America.

**1.2** The President shall:
  (a) Faithfully execute the laws of the United States
  (b) Serve as commander in chief of the armed forces
  (c) Represent the United States in foreign affairs
  (d) Nominate officers of the United States as provided in this Constitution
  (e) Report to Congress on the state of the nation no less than once per year
  (f) Convene either or both chambers of Congress in extraordinary session when necessary

**1.3** The President shall take the following oath before assuming office, administered by the Chief Justice of the Supreme Court:

> *"I do solemnly affirm that I will faithfully execute the office of President of the United States, will to the best of my ability preserve, protect, and defend the Constitution of the United States, and will ensure that the laws of the United States are faithfully carried out."*

---

## Section 2 — Eligibility and Term

**2.1 Qualifications.** A person is eligible to serve as President if they:
  (a) Are at least 35 years of age at the time of taking office
  (b) Have been a citizen of the United States for at least 14 years
  (c) Have been a resident of the United States for at least 7 of the 14 years preceding election

**2.2** The requirement of natural-born citizenship is abolished. No distinction shall be drawn between citizens by birth and citizens by naturalization for purposes of eligibility for the presidency. The general principles of equal citizenship and equal eligibility for office are governed by Article IX.

**2.2A — Sole citizenship required.** The President must hold only United States citizenship while in office. A person who holds citizenship of any other nation at the time of election must formally renounce that citizenship before taking the oath of office. A President who acquires or accepts citizenship of a foreign nation during their term is immediately disqualified and the succession provisions of Section 12 apply. This requirement applies to the Vice President on the same terms.

**2.2B — Prior foreign government service.** No person who has served as an officer, official, elected representative, or employee of a foreign government, foreign military, or foreign intelligence service within the twenty years preceding their election may serve as President or Vice President. Service as a private citizen in a foreign country, employment by a foreign private company, or academic or cultural exchange do not constitute foreign government service for purposes of this Section. The Electoral Commission shall make an initial administrative determination of compliance as part of the candidate qualification process under Section 2.2C, subject to expedited judicial review.

**2.2C — Enhanced foreign ties disclosure.** Every candidate for President or Vice President shall file a complete foreign ties disclosure with the Electoral Commission no later than the date they qualify for ballot access. The disclosure shall include:

  (a) All citizenship or nationality held currently or within the preceding 20 years, and any renunciations made
  (b) All income received from foreign governments, foreign state-owned enterprises, or entities controlled by foreign governments within the preceding 10 years
  (c) All business interests, equity holdings, contracts, or debts involving foreign governments or entities owned or controlled by foreign governments within the preceding 10 years
  (d) All prior service with any foreign government, military, or intelligence service, regardless of how long ago it occurred
  (e) All immediate family members who currently hold positions in a foreign government or military

The Electoral Commission shall publish all disclosures publicly within 5 days of receipt. A candidate who files a materially false or incomplete disclosure is disqualified from the ballot and subject to criminal prosecution under Article VI; such false or materially incomplete disclosure is an absolute bar to certification, not merely a post-election remedy. The Electoral Commission shall verify disclosures against available federal intelligence and law enforcement records before ballot qualification is granted, not merely before final certification of election results. Any candidate aggrieved by an administrative disqualification under this Section has the right to expedited review in the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit, which shall issue a ruling within 14 days.

**2.3 Term.** The President shall serve a term of four years, beginning on the twentieth day of January following their election.

**2.4 Term limits.** No person may be elected President more than twice. Terms need not be consecutive — a person who has served two terms may seek the presidency again after sitting out at least one full term. No person who has served more than two years of another President's term may be elected more than once thereafter.

**2.4A — Vice Presidential terms count toward the limit.** Any full term served as Vice President counts as one term toward the two-term presidential limit. A person who has served two full terms as Vice President may not be elected President. A person who has served one full term as Vice President may be elected President once. A partial Vice Presidential term of fewer than two years does not count. This provision prevents the use of the Vice Presidency as a device to extend executive influence beyond the two-term limit intended by this Section.

**2.5** The President shall receive a fixed compensation set by law at the beginning of each presidential term. This compensation may not be increased or decreased during the term for which it was set. The President may not receive any other emolument, payment, gift, or benefit from the United States or any state, foreign government, or private entity during their term, except compensation established by law.

## Section 3 — The Vice President

**3.1** The Vice President shall be elected jointly with the President on the same ticket, as provided in Article I. The Vice President assumes the powers and duties of the President upon the President's death, resignation, removal, or incapacity, as provided in Sections 11 and 12 of this Article.

**3.2** The Vice President shall preside over the Regional Assembly but shall cast no vote except to break a tie.

**3.3** The Vice President shall serve as a member of the Cabinet and shall assume such responsibilities as the President delegates.

**3.4** The Vice President must meet the same eligibility requirements as the President.

**3.5** If the Vice Presidency becomes vacant, the President shall nominate a new Vice President, subject to confirmation by a majority vote of both chambers of Congress in joint session. The nominee must be confirmed within 60 days.

---

## Section 4 — The Cabinet and Executive Departments

**4.1** The President may establish executive departments and agencies to carry out the functions of the executive branch. The heads of executive departments shall form the Cabinet.

**4.2 Confirmation required.** All Cabinet members, heads of independent agencies, and senior officers of the United States must be confirmed by the Regional Assembly before assuming their duties. No person may exercise the powers of a position requiring confirmation without having been confirmed, except as provided in Section 4.3.

**4.3 Acting officials.** When a confirmed position becomes vacant, the President may designate an acting official to serve temporarily. An acting official may not serve for more than 45 days without a confirmed successor being nominated and submitted to the Regional Assembly. A nomination not acted upon by the Regional Assembly within 90 days of submission is deemed confirmed. No person who has been rejected by the Regional Assembly may serve as acting official in the same position.

**4.4** Cabinet members serve at the pleasure of the President and may be removed at any time without cause, except that removal during an active investigation by the Accountability Commission into the President or Cabinet members requires a written statement of reasons published within 48 hours of removal.

**4.5 Independent agencies.** The heads of independent regulatory agencies whose mandates require insulation from political direction — including but not limited to financial regulators, consumer protection agencies, and communications regulators — may be removed by the President only for cause. Cause shall mean demonstrated neglect of duty, malfeasance, incapacity, or conviction of a crime. Congress shall designate by law which agencies are independent for purposes of this Section.

## Section 5 — Emergency Powers

**5.1 Declaration.** The President may declare a national emergency in response to an imminent threat to public safety, national security, or critical national infrastructure that requires executive action faster than the ordinary legislative process permits.

**5.2 Immediate powers.** Upon a valid declaration of emergency, the President may:
  (a) Direct federal agencies to use existing appropriated funds within their lawful mandates to address the emergency
  (b) Mobilize the National Guard and federal emergency services
  (c) Suspend specific regulatory requirements that materially impede emergency response, for the duration of the emergency only
  (d) Exercise any additional emergency powers specifically authorized by law

**5.3 Limits.** No emergency declaration may:
  (a) Suspend or restrict the right to vote, delay or cancel elections, or alter the electoral process
  (b) Suspend habeas corpus except as provided in Section 5.7
  (c) Direct the armed forces against citizens on domestic soil without congressional authorization
  (d) Authorize surveillance of citizens without judicial warrant
  (e) Appropriate funds not previously authorized by Congress
  (f) Override any provision of Article V (Rights and Freedoms) except as permitted by Article V itself

**5.4 Congressional approval.** An emergency declaration must be submitted to the Regional Assembly within 48 hours. The Regional Assembly must approve or reject the declaration within 30 days. The Regional Assembly approval vote is a constitutionally required vote subject to the mandatory scheduling and continuity rules of Article II Section 15A. Recess, adjournment, or failure of internal chamber procedure does not suspend that deadline. If approval is not granted within the 30-day period, the declaration lapses automatically at the end of the thirtieth day without need for further action by any branch. All powers, directives, suspensions, deployments, or restrictions that depend on the declaration become void upon lapse unless independently authorized by ordinary law. No internal executive memorandum, certification, unilateral finding, or legislative inaction may continue an emergency after lapse. Upon lapse for nonapproval, the Chief Justice, or the judicial officer designated under Article IV Section 2.7, shall immediately issue a public certificate stating that the declaration has expired and that all measures dependent on it are void unless separately authorized by ordinary law. Failure to issue the certificate does not preserve the declaration or any measure dependent on it. Any person subject to continued enforcement after lapse has immediate standing to seek relief in federal court. Congress shall ensure that the Regional Assembly can assemble promptly for this purpose when in recess. No national emergency based on substantially the same facts or circumstances as a lapsed declaration may be re-declared within 60 days of that lapse unless the President certifies in writing that material new circumstances have arisen that were not present at the time of the original declaration. Any such certification is subject to expedited judicial review in the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit, which shall rule within 5 days of any challenge filed by Congress, the Accountability Commission, or any affected party. A court finding that the certification lacks a reasonable factual basis voids the re-declaration.

**5.5 Renewal.** The Regional Assembly may renew an approved emergency declaration for successive 30-day periods. No emergency may remain in effect for more than 180 days in total without a 2/3 vote of both chambers of Congress authorizing continuation.

**5.6 Termination.** An emergency declaration terminates upon expiration of its authorized period, upon a majority vote of either chamber of Congress, or upon the President's written declaration that the emergency has ended. All emergency measures lapse upon termination unless independently authorized by ordinary law.

**5.7 Suspension of habeas corpus.** The privilege of the writ of habeas corpus may be suspended only by Congress, not by the President alone, only in cases of rebellion or invasion where public safety requires it, and only for defined geographic areas and time periods not to exceed 60 days without renewal.

## Section 6 — Executive Orders

**6.1** The President may issue executive orders to direct the operations of the executive branch, implement existing law, and exercise powers vested in the President by this Constitution.

**6.2 Limits.** An executive order may not:
  (a) Create new legal obligations on private citizens or entities that have not been authorized by statute
  (b) Appropriate or redirect funds contrary to an act of Congress
  (c) Contradict, override, or effectively repeal any act of Congress
  (d) Purport to interpret this Constitution in a manner binding on the other branches

**6.3 Publication.** All executive orders must be published in a public federal register within 48 hours of signing and may not take effect until 15 days after publication, except in a declared emergency. An emergency declaration issued solely to accelerate the effective date of an executive order is void.

**6.4 Congressional override.** Either chamber of Congress may pass a resolution of disapproval of any executive order within 60 days of its publication. An executive order disapproved by a majority of both chambers is void. The President may not reissue a disapproved order in substantially the same form without statutory authorization.

**6.5 Expiration.** An executive order expires at the end of the presidential term in which it was issued unless reissued by the incumbent or successor President. Reissuance requires the same publication and waiting period as original issuance.

## Section 7 — The Pardon Power

**7.1** The President may grant pardons, commutations, and reprieves for offenses against the United States, except as provided in this Section.

**7.2 Prohibited pardons.** The President may not pardon:
  (a) Themselves
  (b) Any person for conduct that formed the basis of impeachment proceedings against the President or that involved the President as a co-conspirator
  (c) Any person convicted under the anti-subversion provisions of Article VI
  (d) Any person for crimes committed against the electoral process under Article I

**7.3 Publication and justification.** Every pardon must be published within 48 hours of being granted, accompanied by a written statement of the reasons for the pardon, including the underlying conduct and the public interest served. Pardons issued without publication and justification are void.

**7.4 Timing restriction.** No pardon may be issued in the 60 days preceding any federal general election, regardless of whether the incumbent President is a candidate in that election. No pardon may be issued after articles of impeachment against the President have been voted out of committee in the House of Representatives, and this prohibition continues until the conclusion of the Regional Assembly's trial or the dismissal of the articles, whichever occurs first.

**7.5** The pardon power does not extend to civil liability, state criminal charges, or administrative penalties. A federal pardon extinguishes only federal criminal liability.

## Section 9 — Foreign Affairs and Treaties

**9.1** The President shall conduct the foreign policy of the United States, negotiate treaties and international agreements, and appoint ambassadors subject to confirmation by the Regional Assembly.

**9.2 Treaties.** A treaty negotiated by the President becomes binding upon ratification by a 2/3 vote of the Regional Assembly. A treaty that has not been ratified by the Regional Assembly within two years of submission is deemed rejected.

**9.3 Executive agreements.** The President may enter into executive agreements with foreign governments without Regional Assembly ratification for matters within the President's existing constitutional or statutory authority. An executive agreement that would, if enacted domestically, require legislation may not be implemented without congressional authorization.

**9.4 Withdrawal from treaties.** The President may not withdraw the United States from a ratified treaty without either: (a) the consent of the Regional Assembly by majority vote, or (b) a 90-day notice period during which Congress may by majority vote of both chambers prohibit withdrawal.

## Section 10 — Impeachment and Removal

**10.1** The President, Vice President, and all civil officers of the United States may be impeached and removed for treason, bribery, corruption, abuse of office, willful violation of this Constitution, or other serious crimes and misdemeanors.

**10.2 Process.** Impeachment proceedings originate in the House of Representatives. The House Judiciary Committee shall conduct an investigation and hold public hearings before articles of impeachment are put to a floor vote. A majority of the full House membership must approve articles of impeachment.

**10.2A Expedited impeachment timetable for specified presidential violations.** Where the President is alleged to have:
  (a) Ordered or maintained military operations in violation of a withdrawal order under Article XVI Section 1.5(c),
  (b) Interfered with an Accountability Commission investigation into the President's own conduct under Section 15.8 of this Article, or
  (c) Committed electoral subversion within the meaning of Article VI Section 1,

the House Judiciary Committee shall report articles of impeachment, or a written statement declining to recommend them, within 14 days after the violation is formally certified by the Accountability Commission, the Supreme Court, or another body expressly designated by this Constitution. The House shall hold a recorded floor vote within 7 days after that report. If the Committee fails to report within the 14-day period, any filed impeachment resolution based on the certified violation is automatically discharged to the floor for a recorded vote within the next 7 days. If the House fails to hold the required recorded vote within the time fixed by this Section, the certified charge and supporting record are automatically transmitted to the Regional Assembly as articles of impeachment limited to the certified violation, and the Regional Assembly shall proceed under Section 10.3. This Section requires a vote; it does not predetermine the result in either chamber.

**10.3 Trial.** Upon the House approving articles of impeachment, the Regional Assembly shall conduct a trial. The Chief Justice of the Supreme Court, or the judicial officer designated under Article IV Section 2.7, shall preside when the President is tried. The accused shall have the right to present a defense, call witnesses, and be represented by counsel. A 2/3 vote of the full Regional Assembly is required to convict. The presiding judicial officer's procedural rulings are binding, subject to judicial review as provided by law.

**10.4 Consequences.** Conviction results in removal from office. A subsequent majority vote of the Regional Assembly may disqualify the convicted officer from holding future federal office. Criminal prosecution for the underlying conduct may proceed separately and is not barred by acquittal in impeachment proceedings.

**10.5** The President or other official under impeachment trial may not exercise the pardon power with respect to any person implicated in the proceedings giving rise to the impeachment.

## Section 11 — Presidential Incapacity

**11.1 Voluntary declaration.** The President may at any time transmit a written declaration to the Speaker of the House and the President Pro Tempore of the Regional Assembly stating that they are unable to discharge their duties. The Vice President shall immediately become Acting President. The President resumes their duties upon transmitting a written declaration of recovery to the same officers.

**11.2 Involuntary determination.** If the President is unable or unwilling to declare their own incapacity, the Vice President, together with a majority of the Cabinet, may transmit a written declaration to the Speaker and President Pro Tempore that the President is unable to discharge their duties. The Vice President shall immediately become Acting President.

**11.3 Presidential contest.** The President may contest a determination under Section 11.2 by transmitting a written declaration to the Speaker and President Pro Tempore within 4 days. If the Vice President and Cabinet majority reaffirm the declaration within 2 days, Congress shall resolve the question within 21 days by a 2/3 vote of both chambers. If Congress does not sustain the incapacity determination within that period, the President resumes the powers of office. During a contest, the President may not remove Cabinet members whose votes are necessary to the determination.

**11.4 Medical panel.** In any contested incapacity proceeding, either the President or Congress may convene an independent medical panel appointed under procedures established by law to assess the President's capacity and report its findings to Congress.

## Section 12 — Succession

**12.1** If the President dies, resigns, is removed from office, or is permanently incapacitated, the Vice President shall become President for the remainder of the term.

**12.2** If both the President and Vice President are simultaneously unable to serve, the line of succession shall be as follows, in order:
  1. Speaker of the House of Representatives
  2. President Pro Tempore of the Regional Assembly
  3. Secretary of State
  4. Secretary of Defense
  5. Attorney General
  6. Secretary of the Treasury
  7. Remaining Cabinet members in order of the establishment of their department

**12.3** Any person in the succession line must meet the constitutional qualifications for the presidency to assume the office. A person who does not meet those qualifications is skipped in the line.

**12.4** A successor under Section 12.2 who was not elected to the presidency may not exercise the pardon power, withdraw from ratified treaties, declare a national emergency, remove Senate-confirmed officials without lawful cause, or make recess appointments without the approval of a majority of both chambers of Congress. This limitation does not apply if the successor was the elected Vice President. Actions taken in violation of this Section are void.

## Section 13 — Presidential Transition

**13.1** The outgoing President shall cooperate fully with the President-elect in a transition of power beginning on the day after the election is certified. Cooperation shall include access to security briefings, agency records necessary for transition planning, and office space and staff.

**13.2** Federal agencies shall provide transition teams of the President-elect with access to information necessary to prepare for governance, subject to existing classification requirements.

**13.3** The outgoing President may not during the transition period:
  (a) Enter into treaties or executive agreements with foreign governments beyond routine matters
  (b) Issue extraordinary waves of pardons designed to evade political accountability
  (c) Make significant senior appointments without the incoming President's written concurrence, except where immediate action is necessary for ongoing national security operations

**13.4** The President-elect shall take office on the twentieth day of January. If that day falls on a Sunday, the oath shall be administered on the following Monday.

**13.5** An outgoing President who refuses to cooperate with the transition or who takes actions prohibited by Section 13.3 is subject to the anti-subversion provisions of Article VI.

## Section 14 — Public Recall of the President

**14.1 Purpose.** Impeachment addresses criminal conduct and abuse of office. This Section addresses a distinct failure mode: a President who has catastrophically lost the confidence of the nation through incompetence, gross policy failure, or conduct that is harmful but not criminal. It provides the public a direct and lawful means of shortening a presidency between elections, subject to strict safeguards against partisan abuse.

**14.2 Eligibility window.** A recall petition may only be initiated during the second or third year of a presidential term. No recall petition may be initiated during the first year of a term or during the final year before a scheduled presidential election. This window ensures the President has had time to govern before facing recall, and prevents recall from being used as a substitute for the ordinary electoral process.

**14.3 Petition requirements.** A recall process is initiated by a public petition:
  (a) Signed by at least 20% of registered voters
  (b) With signatures drawn from at least 35 states, with no fewer than 1% of registered voters in each of those states signing
  (c) Collected within a 90-day window
  (d) Verified by the Electoral Commission within 45 days of submission

A petition that does not meet these requirements is void and may not be resubmitted in the same term.

**14.4 One attempt per term.** Only one recall petition may be pursued per presidential term. If a recall referendum is held and the President is retained, no further recall petition may be filed for the remainder of that term, regardless of subsequent events. Conduct arising after a failed recall that constitutes criminal or impeachable offense remains subject to impeachment proceedings under Section 10.

**14.5 The recall referendum.** Upon the Electoral Commission certifying that a petition meets the requirements of Section 14.3, a national recall referendum shall be held within 90 days. The referendum shall be administered by the Electoral Commission under the same standards as a presidential election, including early voting, accessibility requirements, and paper-verified ballots.

**14.6 Referendum question.** The ballot shall pose a single question:

> *"Shall [Name], President of the United States, be removed from office before the end of their current term?"*

Voters shall answer Yes or No. No alternative candidate shall appear on the recall ballot.

**14.7 Threshold for removal.** The President is removed from office only if:
  (a) At least 60% of all registered voters participate in the referendum, and
  (b) At least 60% of votes cast are in favor of removal

The participation threshold for removing the President is higher than for removing a member of Congress, reflecting that the President is the sole nationally elected executive and removal requires broader national consensus. Coordinated suppression of turnout for the purpose of defeating the recall process violates Article VI.

**14.8 Succession upon recall.** If the President is removed by recall referendum, the Vice President shall immediately become President for the remainder of the term. No special election shall be held. The Vice President was elected on the same ticket and carries a democratic mandate to serve.

**14.9 Scope and limits.** A recall under this Section is not a finding of criminal wrongdoing and does not bar subsequent criminal prosecution or impeachment proceedings against the former President. A President removed by recall retains no immunity from prosecution for conduct during their term.

**14.10 The President's platform.** Prior to the recall referendum, the President shall be afforded a fair opportunity, administered by the Electoral Commission, to make their case to the public. Comparable opportunity shall be provided to representatives of the recall campaign.

**14.11 Prohibited interference.** The President under active recall proceedings may not:
  (a) Declare a national emergency for the purpose of delaying the recall referendum
  (b) Issue pardons to persons who organized or funded the recall petition
  (c) Direct federal agencies to take actions designed to influence the outcome of the recall referendum

Violations of this Section are subject to the anti-subversion provisions of Article VI.

## Section 15 — Presidential Accountability and Immunity

### 15.1 — The Core Principle: No Immunity for Criminal Conduct

No person who holds or has held the office of President or Vice President is immune from criminal investigation, indictment, or prosecution for conduct that violates this Constitution or the laws of the United States.

The official nature of an act — the fact that it was performed through the exercise of a constitutional power or statutory authority — does not itself immunize that act from criminal accountability. Official power used to commit crimes is criminal conduct. The channels through which corrupt conduct is executed do not determine whether it is criminal; the conduct itself does.

This Section supersedes any judicial doctrine holding that the exercise of official presidential powers is categorically immune from criminal prosecution. No such categorical immunity exists under this Constitution.

### 15.2 — Official Capacity Is Not Immunity

The fact that conduct was carried out in an official capacity is relevant to context but not to immunity. Official power used to commit a crime remains criminal. Evidence of how official powers were exercised is admissible in a criminal proceeding.

### 15.3 — Absolute Prohibitions: No Immunity in Any Circumstance

For the following categories of conduct, no immunity claim, official-acts defense, or procedural protection of this Section applies:

  (a) Electoral subversion as defined in Article VI Section 1 — any attempt to prevent, obstruct, or reverse the lawful conduct of a federal election or constitutional transfer of power
  (b) Bribery — accepting anything of value in connection with any official decision or action
  (c) Directing, coordinating, or participating in a criminal conspiracy involving other persons, whether or not official powers were used to further the conspiracy
  (d) Using official powers to obstruct a lawful investigation by the Accountability Commission, a federal court, or Congress
  (e) Issuing orders to members of the armed forces to commit acts that violate this Constitution, federal law, or the laws of war
  (f) Perjury or making knowing false statements in any federal proceeding, congressional testimony, or sworn disclosure

### 15.4 — Heightened Procedural Protection for Other Official-Capacity Conduct

For conduct not falling within Section 15.3, charges based on disputed official-capacity conduct may be brought only after the Accountability Commission finds clear and convincing evidence of criminal conduct and a three-judge certification panel determines that the charge falls outside the good-faith exercise of official authority. This protection applies only at the charging stage, not to investigation.

### 15.5 — Indictment of a Sitting President

A sitting President may be indicted by the Accountability Commission. Investigation, indictment, and other pre-trial proceedings may continue while the President remains in office. Trial is stayed until the President leaves office, and the statute of limitations is tolled during that period. Criminal proceedings remain separate from impeachment.

### 15.6 — Former Presidents: No Special Post-Presidential Immunity

No person who has served as President retains any immunity from criminal prosecution after leaving office beyond the procedural protections of Section 15.4 for official-capacity conduct.

### 15.7 — Accountability Commission Jurisdiction

The Accountability Commission (Article XII) has exclusive jurisdiction to investigate and prosecute criminal conduct by sitting and former Presidents and Vice Presidents. The Department of Justice has no jurisdiction over such matters. This allocation exists because the President controls the Department of Justice and must not be in a position to direct, limit, or terminate investigations into their own conduct.

The Accountability Commission's jurisdiction under this Section is in addition to its jurisdiction under Article VI Section 1 and Article VIII Section 1. All presidential misconduct, whether characterized as official or unofficial, falls within the Accountability Commission's mandate.

### 15.8 — No Interference with Investigation

A sitting President may not direct, limit, terminate, or interfere with any investigation by the Accountability Commission into their own conduct or the conduct of any person who may be a co-conspirator or witness in such an investigation. Such interference is itself a criminal offense under Article VI Section 1 and constitutes an additional ground for impeachment under Section 10 of this Article.

The President retains all lawful rights as a subject of investigation but may not use official powers to obstruct the investigation.
