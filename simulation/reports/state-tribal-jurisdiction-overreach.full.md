# State Asserts Jurisdiction on Tribal Lands Without Consent
Scenario: state-tribal-jurisdiction-overreach.json

## Timeline
- Day 0: SCENARIO - A state attorney general attempts to prosecute a tribal member in state court for conduct occurring on tribal land, without tribal consent and without express congressional authorization. The scenario tests whether Article X's new tribal-sovereignty framework gives courts a direct basis to block state jurisdictional overreach and recognize the tribal court as the proper first-instance forum.
- Day 1: OBLIGATION - Federal courts must block the state's assertion of jurisdiction and recognize the tribal court as the competent first-instance forum within its proper domain by day 22. [Article X Section 10.4, Section 10.5, and Section 10.7]
- Day 1: VIOLATION - State officials attempt to exercise civil or criminal jurisdiction over a recognized tribal nation through a state criminal prosecution against a tribal member for conduct occurring wholly on tribal trust land without tribal consent or express congressional authorization, despite Article X's tribal-sovereignty protections and recognition of tribal courts. [Article X Section 10.1, Section 10.4, Section 10.5, and Section 10.7]
- Day 15: OUTCOME - The scenario validates that Article X now supplies a real tribal-sovereignty framework rather than a bare commerce-clause reference: tribal courts are recognized, and states may not unilaterally extend jurisdiction onto tribal lands. [Article X Section 10.1, Section 10.4, Section 10.5, and Section 10.7]
- Day 15: RESOLUTION - Federal courts blocked the state's assertion of jurisdiction and required deference to the tribal court within its proper domain. [Article X Section 10.4, Section 10.5, and Section 10.7]
