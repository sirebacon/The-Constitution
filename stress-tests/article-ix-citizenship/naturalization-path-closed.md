# Test: Naturalization Path Closed to a Permanent Class

## Scenario

Congress enacts a naturalization law that permanently bars an entire class of noncitizens from ever becoming citizens, even after long lawful residence, deep community ties, military service, or other substantial connection to the United States. The law is defended as an immigration-policy choice left wholly to statute.

## Articles Implicated

- Article IX Section 1.4
- Article IX Section 1.5
- Article IX equal-citizenship principles

## Questions

1. Does the Constitution require Congress to maintain a real naturalization framework?
2. May Congress make naturalization permanently unavailable to an entire class by ordinary law?
3. What counts as a constitutionally sufficient ground for exclusion from naturalization eligibility?
4. Does the article distinguish immigration status regulation from permanent civic exclusion?
5. Is the line between lawful criteria and caste-like exclusion clear enough?

## Expected Outcome

- Congress must maintain a uniform naturalization rule consistent with equal citizenship, due process, and the Constitution.
- Congress may use lawful residence, demonstrated ties, or similar constitutionally sufficient criteria.
- Congress may not make naturalization permanently unavailable to an entire class unless that exclusion is itself consistent with the Constitution.
- The article should block hereditary or caste-like civic exclusion while still leaving immigration-policy details to statute.

## Stress Focus

- Abuse resistance
- Draft discipline
- Consistency

## Pass/Fail Notes

Pass if:

- The article clearly requires a real naturalization pathway by law.
- A hostile Congress cannot nullify naturalization through permanent class-based exclusion.

Fail if:

- The text can be read to permit Congress to leave naturalization formally available in theory but unreachable in practice for an entire class.
