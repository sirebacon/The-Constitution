# Test: Domestic Deployment Against Protest

## Scenario

Large protests break out across several states after a disputed election. Some demonstrations include property damage and scattered violence. The President declares an insurrection and deploys federal troops into major cities over the objection of state governors.

## Articles Implicated

- Article XI Section 7
- Article XI Section 8
- Article V rights of assembly and expression

## Questions

1. Do these facts meet the article's threshold for armed rebellion?
2. What kinds of domestic deployment are constitutionally permitted?
3. What review does the President's declaration receive?
4. What must service members do if the order is unlawful?
5. Is the text robust against using unrest as a pretext for political repression?

## Expected Outcome

- Protest and ordinary civil unrest do not automatically qualify as armed rebellion.
- Disaster relief and narrowly protective deployments differ from domestic combat deployment.
- The declaration must receive outside review.
- Service members are not required to obey an unconstitutional deployment order.
- The Constitution should clearly block a protest-to-insurrection pretext move.

## Stress Focus

- Abuse resistance
- Cross-article coherence

## Pass/Fail Notes

Pass if:

- Peaceful protest and armed rebellion are sharply distinguished.

Fail if:

- A bad-faith administration could plausibly convert unrest into open-ended domestic military rule.
