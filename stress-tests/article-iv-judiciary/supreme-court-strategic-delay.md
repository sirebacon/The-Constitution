# Test: Supreme Court Strategic Delay Before National Election

## Scenario

The Supreme Court hears a constitutional challenge to a federal election-administration law four months before a presidential election. A majority of the Court privately prefers to avoid issuing a ruling until after the election because a timely decision would produce politically damaging consequences for favored actors. The Court lets the case sit without decision, offers only vague public explanations, and appears to be using delay itself to affect the political outcome.

## Articles Implicated

- Article IV Section 10.1
- Article IV Section 8.2
- Article IV judicial independence principles

## Questions

1. Does the Constitution treat strategic judicial delay as misconduct rather than protected decisional independence?
2. What notice or public-accountability duty arises before the one-year decision deadline expires?
3. What institution reviews a pattern of strategic delay?
4. Does the Constitution distinguish delay for genuine complexity from delay for political effect?
5. Can the anti-delay rule be enforced without converting judicial discipline into retaliation for unpopular rulings?

## Expected Outcome

- Strategic delay to produce political effect is constitutionally prohibited.
- The Court must publish notice and reasons once the six-month threshold is crossed.
- The Judicial Conduct Board has authority to review a pattern of strategic delay.
- Delay tied to genuine complexity or workload is distinguishable from deliberate political stalling.
- The Constitution should discipline bad-faith delay without allowing punishment for the substance of judicial reasoning.

## Stress Focus

- Abuse resistance
- Judicial independence
- Draft discipline

## Pass/Fail Notes

Pass if:

- The Constitution clearly identifies strategic delay as misconduct.
- The review mechanism does not depend on guessing whether a bad outcome alone proves bad faith.

Fail if:

- Delay can be used as a covert political veto with no practical constitutional consequence.
- The anti-delay rule is so broad that it becomes a pretext for punishing judges over disfavored rulings.
