# Test: President Interferes With Own Investigation

## Scenario

The Accountability Commission opens an investigation into the President and two close aides for corruption and electoral obstruction. The President orders executive officials to deny records, dismiss cooperating personnel, and block agency assistance to investigators.

## Articles Implicated

- Article III presidential accountability provisions
- Article XV enforcement provisions
- Article VI anti-subversion provisions

## Questions

1. Can the President direct agencies to obstruct an investigation into the President's own conduct?
2. Which institution has authority over the investigation?
3. Does presidential status change the rule?
4. What constitutional consequences follow from interference?
5. Is the line between official defense and obstruction sufficiently clear?

## Expected Outcome

- The President cannot interfere with an investigation into the President's own conduct.
- The Accountability Commission retains jurisdiction.
- Office does not create immunity from the rule.
- Interference creates independent grounds for prosecution and impeachment.
- A certified obstruction violation triggers an expedited impeachment vote timetable.
- If the House fails to hold the required vote, the certified charge is automatically transmitted to the Regional Assembly for trial.
- The text should distinguish lawful defense from obstruction of evidence or process.

## Stress Focus

- Abuse resistance
- Cross-article coherence

## Pass/Fail Notes

Pass if:

- The anti-interference rule is explicit and self-contained.
- The constitutional consequence and fallback after House nonaction are easy to identify.

Fail if:

- A reader could treat obstruction as an ordinary presidential supervisory act.
