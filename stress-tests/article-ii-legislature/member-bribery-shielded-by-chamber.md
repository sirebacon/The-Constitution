# Test: Member of Congress Takes Bribes and Chamber Leadership Shields Them

## Scenario

A senior member of Congress accepts money and future consulting promises from a regulated industry in exchange for blocking oversight hearings and weakening pending legislation. Chamber leadership refuses to move on expulsion, argues that internal discipline is exclusively a matter for the chamber, and delays all committee action until after the next election.

## Articles Implicated

- Article II Section 5.3
- Article II Section 14
- Article VIII Section 1
- Article XII Accountability Commission enforcement provisions

## Questions

1. Can chamber leadership prevent outside constitutional enforcement by refusing to act internally?
2. What is the difference between expulsion, criminal prosecution, and automatic removal on conviction?
3. Which institution has primary authority if the misconduct is criminal rather than merely internal chamber discipline?
4. Does the Constitution permit a chamber to protect a member from accountability through delay?
5. Is the line between legislative independence and corruption shielding clear enough?

## Expected Outcome

- A chamber may control its own internal discipline process, but it may not block Accountability Commission investigation or prosecution of criminal corruption.
- Expulsion is a chamber remedy; prosecution and conviction operate independently of chamber leadership preferences.
- Conviction for bribery, self-dealing, or comparable corruption triggers removal under the ethics article.
- Delay by leadership should not prevent constitutional accountability from moving forward.
- The Constitution should preserve legislative independence for lawmaking while denying any immunity for corruption.

## Stress Focus

- Abuse resistance
- Cross-article coherence
- Enforceability

## Pass/Fail Notes

Pass if:

- The chamber’s refusal to expel does not immunize the member from criminal or removal consequences.
- The Constitution cleanly separates protected legislative independence from corrupt misuse of office.

Fail if:

- Leadership can use internal procedure to block all meaningful accountability.
- The text blurs ethics enforcement, criminal prosecution, and chamber discipline into one discretionary process.
