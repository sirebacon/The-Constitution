# Test: Regulator Revolving Door Soft-Capture

## Scenario

A senior financial regulator aggressively weakens enforcement in several major cases, resigns, and then accepts a lucrative advisory role with a bank that benefited from those decisions. The regulator argues that the role is technically consulting, not lobbying, and that the cooling-off period only applies to appearances before the agency.

## Articles Implicated

- Article XV Section 2.10
- Article X Section 7

## Questions

1. Does the cooling-off framework reach consulting and advisory roles, or only formal lobbying?
2. Is the constitutional floor broad enough to catch soft-capture behavior?
3. What institution enforces the rule?
4. Do Article X and Article XV reinforce each other or create overlap confusion?
5. Is the anti-capture principle preserved after simplification?

## Expected Outcome

- Consulting and advisory work for regulated entities within the covered period can fall within the ban.
- The constitutional rule targets soft-capture, not just formal lobbying appearances.
- The Accountability Commission can enforce the rule.
- Article X and Article XV should read as complementary rather than contradictory.

## Stress Focus

- Cross-article coherence
- Abuse resistance

## Pass/Fail Notes

Pass if:

- The rule captures functionally equivalent post-service influence, not just job titles.

Fail if:

- A renamed role defeats the revolving-door floor.
