## The Preamble

We the People of the United States, all of us without exception, establish this Constitution.

Our nation was founded on the promise that all are created equal and possess rights no government may rightfully deny. For too long, that promise was honored for some and denied to others. This Constitution is our covenant to make it real for all.

We establish a government that serves the people and remains answerable to them: strong enough to protect, limited enough to remain free, and bound by law in every exercise of public power. We know that unchecked power corrodes, that rights without remedies are promises without force, and that democracy, if left undefended, may be captured by those who claim its authority while defying its laws and equal citizenship.

We act not for ourselves alone. We honor those who came before us and were denied the equal protection of this promise, and we accept a duty to those who will come after us. This Constitution binds us to leave them a republic capable of self-government, a nation capable of justice, and a planet capable of sustaining life.

To these ends, we ordain and establish this Constitution of the United States of America.

## Design Notes

**On "We the People — all of us, without exception"**
The original "We the People" excluded enslaved persons, women, and those without property. The opening phrase of this Constitution corrects that directly and without ambiguity. It is the first statement of equal inclusion before any right or structure is defined.

**On acknowledging the broken promise**
Several modern constitutions — South Africa's most notably — open with an honest acknowledgment of historical injustice. This is not self-flagellation; it is the reason a new constitution is necessary and the standard against which it will be judged. Pretending the prior constitution fully succeeded would be dishonest and would drain the new one of moral authority.

**On "strong enough to protect, limited enough to remain free"**
This remains the central tension in constitutional design. A government too weak cannot protect rights; a government too powerful becomes the threat to rights. The revised sentence stays declarative and adds "in every exercise of public power" to make the rule of law sound more foundational and less ornamental.

**On future generations**
Design Principle 6 (long-term thinking) needed to appear in the preamble, not just in technical provisions. The obligation to future generations — who cannot vote, petition, or lobby — is stated here as a founding commitment. "A planet capable of sustaining life" is slightly more concrete and less open-ended, while keeping the same grounding for Article XIII and the long-term fiscal provisions of Article II.

**On defending democracy in its own name**
This is the animating purpose of Article VI and the unamendable core of Article VIII, stated in plain language. The revised phrasing ties democratic self-defense more directly to law and equal citizenship, which makes it harder to read as a license for purely rhetorical or partisan suppression while keeping the same warning: democratic systems can be destroyed through formally democratic channels unless the constitution is written to resist that abuse.

**On brevity**
The preamble is five sentences. It is meant to be memorized, recited, and internalized — not studied. Every word was chosen to carry weight. The articles that follow provide the structure; the preamble provides the reason.
