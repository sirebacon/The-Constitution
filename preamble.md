# Preamble

**Status:** `[ DRAFT ]`

## The Preamble

We the People of the United States — all of us, without exception — establish this Constitution.

Our nation was founded on the promise that all people are created equal, endowed with rights that no government may take away. For too long, that promise was made only to some. This Constitution is our commitment to keep it for everyone.

We establish this government to serve the people and to remain answerable to them. We create it to be strong enough to protect the vulnerable, limited enough to remain free, and honest enough to admit when it has failed. We build it knowing that power without accountability corrodes, that rights without protection are merely words, and that democracy left unguarded will not guard itself.

We act not only for ourselves. We act for those who came before us and were denied the full measure of this promise, and for those who will come after us and inherit what we build today. This Constitution binds us to them — it is our obligation to leave them a republic capable of self-governance, a planet capable of sustaining life, and a democracy resilient enough to survive those who would dismantle it from within.

To these ends, we ordain and establish this Constitution of the United States of America.

## Design Notes

**On "We the People — all of us, without exception"**
The original "We the People" excluded enslaved persons, women, and those without property. The opening phrase of this Constitution corrects that directly and without ambiguity. It is the first statement of equal inclusion before any right or structure is defined.

**On acknowledging the broken promise**
Several modern constitutions — South Africa's most notably — open with an honest acknowledgment of historical injustice. This is not self-flagellation; it is the reason a new constitution is necessary and the standard against which it will be judged. Pretending the prior constitution fully succeeded would be dishonest and would drain the new one of moral authority.

**On "strong enough to protect, limited enough to remain free"**
This phrase captures the central tension in constitutional design. A government too weak cannot protect rights; a government too powerful becomes the threat to rights. Both failures are named.

**On future generations**
Design Principle 6 (long-term thinking) needed to appear in the preamble, not just in technical provisions. The obligation to future generations — who cannot vote, petition, or lobby — is stated here as a founding commitment. It gives the environmental rights of Article V and the long-term fiscal provisions of Article II their moral grounding.

**On "democracy resilient enough to survive those who would dismantle it from within"**
This is the animating purpose of Article VI and the unamendable core of Article VIII, stated in plain language. It signals to every reader — citizen, official, judge — that this constitution was written with the knowledge that democratic systems can be destroyed by democratic means, and that preventing that is a founding obligation.

**On brevity**
The preamble is five sentences. It is meant to be memorized, recited, and internalized — not studied. Every word was chosen to carry weight. The articles that follow provide the structure; the preamble provides the reason.
