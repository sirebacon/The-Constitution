# Article VI Notes

## Why Democratic Integrity Needs Its Own Article

Traditional constitutional design often assumes democracy will defend itself once elections and formal branches are in place.

Recent experience shows that is not enough.

Democracy can be broken through:

- coercion
- disinformation tied to official abuse
- procedural sabotage
- refusal to transfer power
- covert foreign influence
- soft coordination between political and administrative actors

This article exists because those are not peripheral concerns. They are constitutional concerns.

## Why Anti-Subversion Rules Are Explicit

Under the current U.S. model, some of the gravest anti-democratic conduct is reached only indirectly through criminal law, norm enforcement, or piecemeal statute.

This article makes the constitutional baseline explicit so institutions do not have to guess whether anti-democratic conduct counts as a system-level violation.

## Why The Article Reaches Modern Infrastructure Problems

Democratic manipulation now often happens through systems that are not neatly classified as ballots, speeches, or tanks.

That is why the article pays attention to:

- foreign political influence routed through intermediaries
- election infrastructure fairness
- dominant civic intermediaries during crises
- coordinated efforts to disable meaningful political competition

## The Main Discipline Constraint

This article must remain sharply tied to democratic legitimacy.

If it grows too broad, it risks becoming a general anti-bad-politics article. Its legitimacy depends on staying anchored to constitutional democracy, not ordinary ideological disagreement.
