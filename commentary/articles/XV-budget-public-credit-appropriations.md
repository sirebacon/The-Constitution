# Article XV Notes

## Why Budget And Public Credit Need Constitutional Rules

Fiscal collapse can destroy democratic government as effectively as formal constitutional breakdown.

If borrowing, appropriations, and payment obligations are vague or too easy to manipulate, political actors can create crisis by threatening the basic functioning of the state.

## What This Article Is Trying To Prevent

The article is built against several familiar dangers:

- shutdown-style hostage politics
- executive manipulation of appropriated funds
- artificial default risk
- off-book fiscal improvisation
- instability caused by treating public credit as a partisan weapon

## Why The Draft Distinguishes Revenue From Appropriation

Taxing and spending are related, but they are not the same constitutional power.

Article XIV governs the power to raise revenue. Article XV governs appropriations, payment obligations, credit, and the continuity of government finance. Keeping that split helps each article stay legible and prevents fiscal authority from collapsing into one vague omnibus power.

## Why The Article Uses Continuity Rules

Some continuity rules in this article are unusual by older constitutional standards, but they exist because fiscal sabotage has become a realistic constitutional failure mode.

The point is not to remove democratic control over the budget. The point is to stop bad-faith actors from turning basic state continuity into an extortion tool.
