# Article VII Notes

## Why Campaign Finance Is Constitutionalized At All

Money in politics is not only a policy problem. It is also a constitutional power-allocation problem.

If private wealth can reliably dominate political communication, candidate viability, and access to office, then formal political equality becomes thin and unstable.

This article therefore treats campaign finance as part of the democratic order rather than a peripheral regulatory topic.

## What The Article Is Trying To Prevent

The main targets are:

- politics-for-sale
- dark money
- concentrated donor dependency
- candidate inequality driven by wealth rather than public support
- informal privatization of representative government

## Why The Article Does Not Try To Ban All Political Spending

The point is not to constitutionalize a permanently frozen theory of political money.

The point is to establish that democratic equality, transparency, and anti-corruption are constitutional values strong enough to justify real regulation. Detailed calibration still belongs mostly to law.

## Why This Article Needed Compression

Campaign finance is one of the easiest places for a draft to start reading like a statute.

That is why later revisions trimmed some of the more code-like machinery. The constitutional level should protect fair competition and prevent plutocratic capture without trying to encode every reporting or enforcement detail.
