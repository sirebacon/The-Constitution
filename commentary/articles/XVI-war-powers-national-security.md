# Article XVI Notes

## What This Article Is Trying To Fix

Under the current U.S. model, war powers are one of the most under-disciplined parts of the constitutional order.

Formal text, later practice, statutory improvisation, and presidential initiative have produced a system in which the nation can drift into armed conflict with weak democratic control.

This article is meant to end that drift.

## Why The Article Is So Restrictive

War is one of the most extreme forms of public power.

It changes life, liberty, treasury, rights, and political culture all at once. That is why this draft treats democratic authorization, timeline discipline, and withdrawal consequences as constitutional rather than merely statutory questions.

## What The Article Is Designed Against

The main targets are:

- unilateral military escalation
- indefinite unauthorized operations
- domestic deployment against political opposition
- military outsourcing to evade constitutional limits
- nuclear-use discretion without democratic accountability

## Why The Article Uses Automatic Consequences

War powers are one of the clearest areas where missed deadlines and vague disapproval are not enough.

That is why this article connects nonauthorization to concrete downstream effects like withdrawal duty, funding consequences, and impeachment triggers. The point is to make unauthorized war politically and constitutionally harder to sustain through inertia alone.
