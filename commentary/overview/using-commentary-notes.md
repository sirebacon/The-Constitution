# Using Commentary Notes

## Purpose

This site keeps the constitutional text and the explanatory material separate on purpose.

The Constitution is the operative text. Commentary notes explain why certain provisions exist, what problem they are solving, and what tradeoffs shaped the drafting choice.

## How To Read Them

Use the constitutional text when you want the rule itself.

Use commentary notes when you want:

- the democratic problem behind a clause
- the abuse pattern the clause is trying to prevent
- the reason this draft chose one design over another
- the reason some details were left to ordinary law instead of constitutionalized

## What Commentary Is Not

Commentary is not binding text.

It is not part of the Constitution, and it is not meant to override or silently modify the article language. It is an explanatory layer for readers, reviewers, and translators.

## Why This Matters

A constitution should remain readable as law.

If the reasons, historical examples, and drafting debates are inserted directly into the article text, the document gets harder to cite, harder to maintain, and less constitution-like. A separate commentary layer keeps the law clean while still making the choices legible.
